## One-Sentence Chapter Purpose

Warranties turn product promises into enforceable contract obligations: what the seller said, what the goods were called, what samples showed, what ordinary use requires, what special purpose the buyer disclosed, and what remedies follow when delivered goods fail to match the deal.

## Why This Belongs in Chapter 8

This material is the practical middle of sales law. Chapter 7 explains contract formation and remedies in general. Chapter 8 narrows the frame to goods under UCC Article 2. Warranties show how sales law allocates risk after formation: even if the parties have a valid contract, the goods may still breach the contract because they fail an express warranty, implied merchantability, implied fitness, title, or noninfringement obligation.

For MBA students, the managerial lesson is direct. Marketing copy, product labels, sales decks, technical specifications, samples, purchase orders, inspection procedures, and standard terms can all become part of the legal risk architecture of a sale.

## Core Doctrine Map

### UCC Article 2 Scope

Article 2 governs transactions in goods. Goods are movable things identified to the contract. OpenStax 8.1 usefully contrasts goods with services and realty, but the chapter should be careful not to imply that Article 2 generally governs services or land. Mixed transactions often require a predominant-purpose analysis: is the transaction mainly for goods, or mainly for services?

Article 2 also loosens some common-law formation rules. Price, delivery, payment, and duration can sometimes be supplied by default rules if the parties intended to contract. Quantity is the harder missing term, except in output and requirements contracts bounded by good faith.

### Good Faith

OpenStax references UCC 1-203 for good faith. That is an older citation. Revised Article 1 places the general obligation at UCC 1-304: every UCC contract or duty imposes an obligation of good faith in performance and enforcement. If the chapter keeps the OpenStax reference, add a note: former UCC 1-203, now UCC 1-304 in revised Article 1.

Good faith matters in warranty and sales disputes because it limits opportunistic behavior: manufactured surprise, bad-faith rejection, sham requirements demands, unreasonable cover, or strategic reliance on boilerplate that contradicts the real bargain.

### Express Warranties: UCC 2-313

An express warranty can arise from:

- an affirmation of fact,
- a promise,
- a description of the goods,
- a sample, or
- a model,

if it becomes part of the basis of the bargain.

The seller does not need to use formal words like "warrant" or "guarantee." The key distinction is factual commitment versus puffery. "Made in the USA," "24k gold," "not from concentrate," "wrinkle-free," and technical performance specifications can create express warranties. "Best in the world" normally does not.

Teaching point: express warranties are not just in a document labeled "Warranty." They can be created by labels, demos, sales emails, catalog descriptions, samples, drawings, and product data sheets.

### Implied Warranty of Merchantability: UCC 2-314

Merchantability is implied when the seller is a merchant with respect to goods of that kind, unless excluded or modified. The simplest classroom formulation: goods must be fit for the ordinary purposes for which goods of that kind are used.

Examples:

- A food product sold commercially must be fit for human consumption.
- A used machine may carry a merchantability warranty, but the expected condition is calibrated to used goods.
- A mass-produced product must satisfy ordinary trade expectations for goods sold under that description.

Teaching point: merchantability asks, "Can this thing do the ordinary job goods like this are supposed to do?"

### Implied Warranty of Fitness for a Particular Purpose: UCC 2-315

Fitness for a particular purpose is different from merchantability. It arises when:

- the seller has reason to know the buyer's particular purpose,
- the seller has reason to know the buyer is relying on the seller's skill or judgment, and
- the buyer actually relies on that skill or judgment.

Teaching point: merchantability is ordinary use; fitness is special use plus reliance. A buyer who designs the goods or supplies exact specifications may have trouble proving reliance on the seller's judgment, but that does not automatically erase every other warranty.

### Warranty of Title and Noninfringement: UCC 2-312

By selling goods, the seller normally warrants that title is good, transfer is rightful, and the goods are free from undisclosed liens or encumbrances. A merchant regularly dealing in goods of that kind also warrants against rightful infringement claims, unless the buyer's own specifications caused the problem.

Modern examples:

- resold equipment subject to an undisclosed security interest,
- counterfeit branded products,
- gray-market inventory,
- a custom part manufactured from buyer-supplied drawings that infringe a patent.

### Disclaimers and Warranty Limits: UCC 2-316

Disclaimers are common in commercial contracts, but Article 2 controls how they work.

Merchantability disclaimers generally must mention "merchantability"; if written, they must be conspicuous. Fitness disclaimers generally must be written and conspicuous. "As is" or "with all faults" language can exclude implied warranties if it reasonably alerts the buyer that implied warranties are excluded.

Express warranties are harder to disclaim. A seller usually cannot make a specific factual promise and then erase it with contradictory boilerplate. UCC 2-316 tries to read warranty-creating language and disclaimer language consistently where reasonable; if they cannot be reconciled, the negation may be ineffective.

Teaching point: standard terms cannot always rescue an overpromising sales process.

### Buyer Remedies

OpenStax's remedy table is useful but should be mapped more precisely to Article 2:

- UCC 2-711: cancellation and buyer remedies in general.
- UCC 2-712: cover with substitute goods.
- UCC 2-713: market-price damages for nondelivery or repudiation.
- UCC 2-714: damages for accepted nonconforming goods, including warranty breach.
- UCC 2-715: incidental and consequential damages.
- UCC 2-716: specific performance or replevin for unique goods or other proper cases.

Punitive damages should be handled cautiously. They are not an ordinary remedy for breach of a sales contract. They generally require an independent basis such as fraud, a statute, tortious conduct, bad faith in a special context, or another non-contract doctrine.

## Corrections and Cautions for the OpenStax Text

- Update or footnote the good-faith citation: former UCC 1-203 is now UCC 1-304 in revised Article 1.
- Avoid suggesting Article 2 generally governs services or realty. Article 2 is goods-focused.
- Replace "warranty of fitness for normal use" with "implied warranty of merchantability." Fitness for a particular purpose is a separate doctrine.
- Treat express and implied warranties as cumulative where reasonable, but use UCC 2-317 and 2-316 carefully for conflicts.
- Avoid listing punitive damages as a normal sales-contract remedy.
- Be careful with "buyer-designed goods." Buyer specifications can defeat reliance for fitness, but merchantability, express warranties, title, and noninfringement issues may still matter depending on facts.

## Suggested Chapter Insert

Warranties are the law's way of making product promises operational. A sales contract does not merely say "goods for money." It also asks what the seller said, what the trade expects, what a merchant should stand behind, what special use the buyer disclosed, and what happens when the delivered thing is not the thing the deal called for. For business readers, the lesson is direct: product descriptions, samples, labels, demos, sales decks, and standard terms are not background noise. They are part of the risk architecture of the deal.

## MBA Classroom Scenario

A restaurant buys a used commercial freezer from a restaurant-equipment dealer. The salesperson says, "It has been tested and is ready for commercial kitchen use." The invoice includes a small-print clause stating, "Seller disclaims all warranties." The freezer fails after two days, ruining $8,000 of food inventory.

Discussion path:

- Did "tested and ready for commercial kitchen use" create an express warranty?
- Is the seller a merchant with respect to restaurant equipment?
- Does the implied warranty of merchantability apply to used equipment?
- Was the disclaimer conspicuous?
- Can boilerplate disclaim an express warranty created by the salesperson?
- Are the spoiled-food losses consequential damages, incidental damages, or both?
- Were those downstream losses foreseeable at the time of contracting?

## Manager's Checklist

- What factual claims did we make in ads, labels, specs, demos, samples, or sales emails?
- Are we a merchant for this kind of good?
- Did the buyer disclose a special purpose?
- Did the buyer rely on our skill or judgment?
- Do our disclaimers mention merchantability when required?
- Are disclaimers conspicuous?
- Do remedy limitations contradict the practical warranty promise?
- Could a limited repair-or-replace remedy fail of its essential purpose?
- Did the buyer give timely notice of breach?
- What consequential damages were foreseeable when the contract was made?

## LLM Exercise Idea

Prompt:

> A small electronics manufacturer orders 500 specialized circuit boards for $42,000. The purchase order states that the boards must meet IPC-A-610 Class 3 standards. The supplier ships 480 boards, and 60 fail incoming inspection. After the purchase order but before shipment, the supplier emailed standard terms disclaiming all implied warranties and limiting remedies to repair or replacement at the supplier's option. Apply UCC Article 2. Identify express warranties, implied warranties, disclaimer issues, battle-of-the-forms issues, buyer notice, and available remedies.

Evaluation rubric:

- Does the model identify the product specification as a likely express warranty?
- Does it distinguish merchantability from fitness for a particular purpose?
- Does it analyze whether the disclaimer is conspicuous and whether it can negate express warranties?
- Does it flag UCC 2-207 if the standard terms arrived after the purchase order?
- Does it identify rejection, acceptance with damages, cover, incidental damages, and consequential damages?
- Does it avoid treating every product failure as strict product liability?
