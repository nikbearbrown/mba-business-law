# Chapter 12 — Unfair Trade Practices and the Federal Trade Commission
*The agency that exists because "buyer beware" turned out not to be enough.*

---

Here is a question that sounds simple until you think about it carefully.

When a company tells you something false about its product — false in a way that causes you to buy it when you otherwise wouldn't — who is responsible for the harm? You, for believing it? The company, for saying it? And if the company is responsible, what is the remedy? A lawsuit by each individual consumer, most of whom were deceived out of ten or twenty dollars and will never find it worth their time to sue?

This is not an abstract question. It is the operational problem that the Federal Trade Commission was created to solve.

Before the FTC existed, the answer was essentially: each consumer could sue under common-law fraud. That standard is deliberately difficult to meet. It requires proving that the false statement was made knowingly, with intent to deceive, and that the specific consumer relied on it to their detriment. That evidentiary burden was designed for serious, targeted frauds — not for the systematic, low-stakes-per-consumer, high-aggregate-profit deceptions that modern advertising makes possible. A supplement company that advertises "clinically proven to reverse aging" without a shred of scientific support is not committing targeted fraud against any individual. It is committing a kind of statistical fraud against a market — harming millions of consumers by small amounts each, in a way that no individual consumer can afford to challenge.

The FTC Act of 1914 created a different mechanism. Instead of requiring each consumer to sue after the fact, it gave a federal agency authority to stop deceptive and unfair practices before they accumulate into millions of small harms — without the consumer ever appearing in court.

The machinery is more interesting than it sounds.

---

The FTC Act's operative provision is Section 5, which prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce." Eleven words of substance. Those eleven words have generated decades of enforcement, rulemaking, and litigation — because the two prongs, *deceptive* and *unfair*, turn out to mean different things and require different analysis. A practice can be deceptive without being unfair. It can be unfair without being deceptive. The evidentiary showing required, the defenses available, and the remedies differ across the two categories. Understanding the distinction is not semantic housekeeping; it determines whether a challenged practice is covered at all.

The deception standard was formally articulated in a 1983 FTC Policy Statement on Deception. Three elements are required.

First, there must be a representation, omission, or practice *likely to mislead* the consumer. Not that it actually misled anyone in particular — the standard is prospective and probabilistic. An advertisement containing a false claim is potentially deceptive even if no individual consumer can be identified who was in fact misled by it. The FTC does not need to find a victim; it needs to find a practice.

Second, the consumer's interpretation must be *reasonable under the circumstances*. The FTC does not protect against interpretations that no reasonable person would make. An advertisement claiming a car "flies through traffic" is not deceptive because no reasonable consumer believes it is asserting literal flight. This is the limiting principle: the deception standard is calibrated to what a typical consumer in the relevant market would take away from the communication, not what the most credulous or the most skeptical consumer might conclude.

Third, the deception must be *material* — the kind of thing that would actually affect a consumer's purchasing decision. A false claim about a feature no consumer cares about is not an FTC violation merely because it is false. Materiality limits the deception standard to falsehoods that matter: price, safety, effectiveness, ingredients, country of origin, endorsements, scientific support.

<!-- → [FIGURE: The deception standard as a three-gate filter — representation/omission/practice → reasonable consumer interpretation → material effect on purchasing decision. Source: Chapter 12 Fig 12.1] -->

The deception standard reaches not only affirmative false statements but two other categories that are often more commercially significant. *Misleading omissions*: a seller's statement can be literally true but create a false impression because it omits material context. A drug advertisement that displays benefits prominently without adequate disclosure of serious side effects can be deceptive through omission even if every word in the ad is technically accurate. And *implied claims*: the FTC looks at what message a reasonable consumer would take away from an advertisement in its totality, not at the literal meaning of each isolated sentence. A supplement company that surrounds "supports immune function" with clinical-looking graphics, lab coats, and before-and-after imagery may be communicating something that its cautious legal language does not literally say.

The *substantiation requirement* is the structural extension of the deception framework that matters most in practice. The FTC's position is that making an objective, verifiable claim without adequate scientific support is itself deceptive — even if the company has no evidence one way or the other about whether the claim is true. You cannot assert a fact you have not established. For health and safety claims, "adequate substantiation" generally means competent and reliable scientific evidence — controlled studies, not testimonials. POM Wonderful settled an FTC enforcement action in 2013 after the agency found its advertising claims about treating heart disease and prostate cancer were unsupported by evidence adequate to the claims being made. The company was not charged because its claims were proven false. It was charged because it had not proven them true before making them.

---

The unfairness standard is harder to satisfy and less frequently invoked than deception, but it covers a different and important category: practices that harm consumers without necessarily misleading them.

The standard was codified in 1994. Three elements are required. The practice must cause *substantial injury to consumers* — real economic harm or physical injury, not trivial or speculative harm. The injury must not be *reasonably avoidable* — if consumers can protect themselves through ordinary care or by reading available information, the practice is less likely to meet the unfairness standard. And the injury must not be *outweighed by countervailing benefits* to consumers or competition — the FTC performs something like a cost-benefit analysis, acknowledging that practices producing some consumer harm may also produce consumer benefits worth preserving.

The key practical difference from deception is the consumer-avoidance element. The deception standard focuses on what the seller said and whether a reasonable consumer would be misled. The unfairness standard focuses on whether the consumer could have avoided the harm — which places weight on market structure and information availability, not just on the content of the seller's communication. A company using aggressive debt-collection tactics — calling consumers repeatedly at odd hours, making implicit threats — may not be making any false statements. Nothing it says is deceptive. But the practice can still be unfair if it causes substantial harm that consumers cannot reasonably avoid and that produces no offsetting benefit to anyone.

---

The theoretical framework becomes concrete in what the FTC actually enforces. Three categories dominate contemporary consumer-protection enforcement.

The first is advertising. The FTC's advertising enforcement runs through two requirements most consumers are unaware of. The substantiation requirement, already described, means advertisers must have their evidence before making objective claims. The *endorsement and testimonial guidelines* require that material connections between endorsers and advertisers be disclosed. A celebrity paid to appear in an advertisement obviously signals the relationship by the nature of the ad. But the guidelines extend to less obvious cases: an influencer who receives free products and posts about them without disclosure, a blogger paid for a review without saying so, a review platform that features promoted content indistinguishably from organic reviews. The FTC's 2023 update to its endorsement guidelines specifically addressed social-media disclosure — a recognition that the mechanisms of paid promotion have evolved substantially since the original guidelines were issued.

The deceptive pricing framework — "was $99.99, now $49.99" — requires that the "original" price be a genuine price at which the product was actually offered for sale, not a fictitious reference price set artificially high to make the discount look larger. The FTC has brought enforcement actions against retailers whose "sale" prices were actually the regular prices, with "original" prices that were never genuinely available to consumers.

The second category is privacy and data security, and the FTC's presence here requires explanation because it was not designed into the agency's original mission.

The United States does not have a comprehensive federal data-privacy statute. What it has is a patchwork of sector-specific statutes — HIPAA for health data, COPPA for children's online data, the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act for financial-services data — and, filling the gaps, the FTC. The FTC's authority in privacy derives from Section 5's deception and unfairness prongs. When a company promises in its privacy policy that it will not share user data with third parties and then shares it, the FTC can bring a deception action — the privacy policy was a representation, consumers reasonably relied on it, and the violation was material. When a company fails to maintain adequate data security and consumers are harmed by the resulting breach, the FTC can bring an unfairness action — substantial consumer injury, not reasonably avoidable by individuals who have no visibility into the company's security practices.

The consequence is that the FTC became the primary federal enforcement agency for data privacy not because Congress designed it that way, but because it had broad statutory authority and no other agency had clear jurisdiction over the conduct.

<!-- → [FIGURE: U.S. privacy regulation landscape — sector-specific statutes (HIPAA, COPPA, GLB) covering their respective domains, with Section 5 FTC authority shown as a gap-filler covering the remaining territory. Source: Chapter 12 Fig 12.3] -->

The two largest FTC privacy settlements illustrate the scope. In 2019, Facebook paid $5 billion to settle FTC charges arising from the Cambridge Analytica matter — user data shared with a political consulting firm without adequate consent, in violation of a 2012 FTC consent order Facebook had already entered into. In 2022, Twitter paid $150 million to settle FTC charges that it had used phone numbers and email addresses collected for two-factor authentication security purposes to serve targeted advertising, again in violation of a prior consent order. Both cases involved violations of prior orders, which is the mechanism through which the FTC converts its otherwise-civil enforcement authority into something with sharper teeth.

The third category is consumer financial services. The FTC enforces the Truth in Lending Act (standardized disclosure of credit terms), the Fair Credit Reporting Act (accuracy of credit reports and consumers' rights to dispute them), and the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (prohibiting abusive, deceptive, and unfair debt-collection practices). The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, created by Dodd-Frank in 2010, has overlapping jurisdiction over many of these areas and is the primary regulator for banks and credit unions. The FTC retains primary jurisdiction over non-bank financial entities — auto dealers, payday lenders, mortgage brokers, debt collectors — that the CFPB does not directly supervise. The division of labor is not always clean.

---

Most people, if they think about government enforcement at all, imagine it ends in a trial. An agency investigates, charges, litigates, and a court renders judgment. FTC consumer-protection enforcement almost never works that way.

The typical trajectory is: investigation, complaint, negotiation, *consent order*. The firm under investigation, rather than litigating, enters into a settlement in which it agrees to specified conduct restrictions and, frequently, a monetary remedy. The firm does not admit liability. The consent order is entered by the FTC and becomes a binding obligation.

Consent orders typically run for twenty years. They often require the firm to establish and maintain specific compliance programs, to report regularly to the FTC on its compliance, and to notify the agency of certain changes in its business structure. Violating a consent order is not merely a breach of contract. It is the basis for civil penalties, which can be substantial.

<!-- → [FIGURE: FTC enforcement lifecycle — investigation → complaint → negotiation → consent order → twenty-year compliance obligation → civil penalties for violation. Source: Chapter 12 Fig 12.4] -->

The escalating-penalty structure for consent-order violations is where the FTC's enforcement leverage actually sits. A firm that violates its original conduct obligations faces civil penalties calibrated to the scale and duration of the violation. The Facebook $5 billion settlement was itself triggered by Facebook's alleged violation of the 2012 consent order. The message is specific: a consent order is not a one-time payment that buys immunity from future scrutiny. It is an ongoing obligation with ongoing enforcement consequences. The prior order raises the stakes for every subsequent decision the company makes in the same domain.

The consent-order structure has both advantages and limits. The advantage is efficiency: an action that would take years to litigate can be resolved in months, and the result is a binding, ongoing obligation rather than a one-time penalty. The limit is that the firm does not admit wrongdoing, which reduces the deterrent signal to other firms that can argue their conduct is distinguishable.

---

Return to the problem the FTC was created to solve. Systematic, low-stakes-per-consumer deceptions — the false "clinically proven" claim, the undisclosed sponsored post, the fictitious reference price — are not efficiently addressed by individual consumer litigation. The consumers harmed are dispersed. Each individual harm is small. The cost of litigation exceeds the individual recovery. The aggregate harm may be large; the per-consumer harm is not. This is exactly the structural condition under which market mechanisms for accountability fail.

The FTC's framework addresses this with a different architecture. A single agency with enforcement authority can bring an action on behalf of all harmed consumers, impose a penalty calibrated to aggregate harm, and obtain injunctive relief that stops the practice going forward. The cost of enforcement is borne by the government rather than by individual consumers, who cannot afford to bear it.

The framework also addresses the information asymmetries that make consumer deceptions possible in the first place. Consumers cannot verify the scientific basis of health claims before purchasing. They cannot audit a company's data-security practices before sharing personal information. They cannot reliably distinguish organic reviews from paid ones. The substantiation requirement, privacy enforcement, and endorsement guidelines each address a specific information gap — requiring disclosure, substantiation, or verification in markets where consumers cannot protect themselves through ordinary care.

The limits are real too. The FTC has finite resources relative to the volume of potentially deceptive commercial activity. Its consent-order process produces no admission of wrongdoing. In rapidly evolving markets — digital advertising, data brokerage, algorithmic pricing, AI-generated synthetic reviews — the agency is consistently applying a 1914 statutory framework to practices its drafters could not have imagined.

The substantive tension in Section 5 enforcement — between a deception standard that requires a false statement and an unfairness standard that can reach harmful practices without one — is also the framework's productive core. The deception standard is well-defined but narrow. The unfairness standard is broader but requires a cost-benefit analysis that is genuinely contestable. A practice the FTC argues is unfair can almost always generate a counterargument that its costs are outweighed by benefits to competition or consumer choice. The tension between the two standards is where most of the interesting FTC enforcement decisions live, and where the boundaries of the framework are still being negotiated.

The agency exists because a market economy generates, as a structural byproduct, practices that cause real harm through channels that individual consumers cannot address and that common-law fraud was not designed to catch. Section 5's eleven words are the statutory response to that structural problem. Whether they are adequate to it — in any given market, at any given technological moment — is always a contestable question. But the problem those eleven words were written to address has not changed.
