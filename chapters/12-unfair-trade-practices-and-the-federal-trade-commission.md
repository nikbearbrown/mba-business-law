# Unfair Trade Practices and the Federal Trade Commission

The *Federal Trade Commission* — established by the Federal Trade Commission Act of 1914 — operates as the U.S. government's primary consumer-protection agency. Its dual mission combines consumer protection (preventing unfair or deceptive practices that harm consumers) and competition enforcement (working alongside the DOJ Antitrust Division). This chapter focuses on the consumer-protection side.

## Learning objectives

- **Identify** the FTC's consumer-protection authority under FTC Act Section 5.
- **Distinguish** *unfair* practices from *deceptive* practices under the FTC's analytical framework.
- **Evaluate** specific FTC enforcement actions and consent orders.

## Concept 1: Section 5 of the FTC Act

Section 5 of the FTC Act prohibits *unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce*. The two prongs are analytically distinct.

**Deceptive practices.** The FTC's deception standard, articulated in the 1983 *FTC Policy Statement on Deception*, requires (1) a representation, omission, or practice that is likely to mislead the consumer; (2) consumer interpretation that is reasonable under the circumstances; (3) the deception is *material* to the consumer's decision. Examples: false advertising, deceptive labeling, undisclosed material terms.

**Unfair practices.** The FTC's unfairness standard, codified in 1994, requires (1) substantial consumer injury; (2) injury not reasonably avoidable by consumers; (3) injury not outweighed by countervailing benefits. The standard is harder to satisfy than the deception standard.

## Concept 2: Specific FTC enforcement areas

**Advertising.** The FTC enforces truthful-advertising standards including substantiation requirements (claims must have a reasonable basis), endorsement-disclosure requirements (sponsored content must be disclosed), and specific industry rules (drug advertising, financial-services advertising).

**Privacy and data security.** The FTC has emerged, in the absence of a comprehensive U.S. federal privacy statute, as the de-facto privacy regulator. Major recent settlements include the 2019 Facebook $5 billion settlement and the 2022 Twitter $150 million settlement, both for violations of prior FTC consent orders.

**Consumer financial services.** The FTC enforces the Truth in Lending Act, Fair Credit Reporting Act, Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, and several other statutes. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (created by Dodd-Frank in 2010) has overlapping jurisdiction in many areas.

**Telemarketing and spam.** The FTC operates the National Do Not Call Registry under the Telemarketing Sales Rule. The CAN-SPAM Act of 2003 provides additional restrictions on commercial email.

## Concept 3: FTC remedies and consent orders

FTC enforcement typically produces *consent orders* — settlements in which the firm agrees to specified conduct restrictions and, often, monetary remedies, without admitting liability. Consent orders are enforceable through subsequent contempt proceedings if violated; they often run for 20 years.

Civil penalties for violations of FTC orders, regulations, or specific statutes can reach substantial amounts. The Facebook $5 billion settlement of 2019 was at the time the largest FTC consumer-protection settlement.

## Summary

The FTC is the dominant U.S. consumer-protection enforcement agency. The deception and unfairness standards under FTC Act Section 5 produce a substantial enforcement docket each year.
