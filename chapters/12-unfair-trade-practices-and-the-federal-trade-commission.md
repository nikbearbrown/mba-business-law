# Chapter 12 — Unfair Trade Practices and the Federal Trade Commission
*The agency that exists because "buyer beware" turned out not to be enough.*

---

Here is a question that sounds simple until you think about it carefully.

When a company tells you something false about its product — false in a way that causes you to buy it when you otherwise wouldn't — who is responsible for the harm? You, for believing it? The company, for saying it? And if the company is responsible, what is the remedy? A lawsuit by each individual consumer, most of whom were deceived out of ten or twenty dollars and will never find it worth their time to sue? Or something else?

This is not an abstract question. It is the operational problem that the Federal Trade Commission was created to solve. Before the FTC existed, the answer was essentially: each consumer could sue under common-law fraud, which required proving that the false statement was made knowingly, with intent to deceive, and that the specific consumer relied on it to their detriment. That standard is deliberately difficult to meet. It was designed for serious, targeted frauds — not for the systematic, low-stakes-per-consumer, high-aggregate-profit deceptions that modern advertising and consumer markets make possible.

The FTC Act of 1914 created a different mechanism. Instead of requiring each individual consumer to sue after the fact, it gave a federal agency the authority to stop deceptive and unfair practices before they accumulate into millions of small harms — and to do so without the consumer ever having to appear in court.

That is the architecture this chapter is about. The machinery is more interesting than it sounds.

---

## What Section 5 actually says and why the two prongs matter

The Federal Trade Commission Act's operative provision is Section 5, which prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce." The statute is eleven words of substance. Those eleven words have generated decades of enforcement actions, policy statements, rulemaking proceedings, and litigation — because the two prongs, *deceptive* and *unfair*, turn out to mean different things and to require different analytical frameworks.

The distinction matters practically. A practice can be deceptive without being unfair. It can be unfair without being deceptive. And the evidentiary showing required, the defenses available, and the remedies appropriate differ across the two categories. The FTC has separate analytical frameworks for each.

### The deception standard

The FTC's deception standard was formally articulated in a 1983 *Policy Statement on Deception* addressed to Congress. Three elements are required.

First, there must be a representation, omission, or practice that is *likely to mislead* the consumer. Not that it actually misled anyone in particular — the standard is prospective and probabilistic. An advertisement that contains a false claim is potentially deceptive even if no individual consumer can be identified who was in fact misled by it.

Second, the consumer's interpretation must be *reasonable under the circumstances*. The FTC does not protect against interpretations that no reasonable person would make. This is the limiting principle: an advertisement that claims a car "flies through traffic" is not deceptive because no reasonable consumer believes it is claiming the car achieves literal flight.

Third, the deception must be *material* — it must be the kind of thing that would actually affect a consumer's purchasing decision. A false claim about a feature no consumer cares about does not become an FTC violation merely because it is false. The materiality requirement limits the deception standard to falsehoods that matter: claims about price, safety, effectiveness, ingredients, country of origin, endorsements.

The FTC's enforcement of the deception standard covers the range of ways a seller can mislead a consumer. Affirmative false statements are the obvious case. But the standard also reaches *misleading omissions* — cases where the seller's statement is literally true but creates a false impression because it omits material context. A drug advertisement that prominently displays benefits without adequate disclosure of serious side effects can be deceptive through omission even if every word in the ad is technically accurate. The standard also reaches *implied claims*: the FTC looks at what message a reasonable consumer would take away from an advertisement in its totality, not just at the literal meaning of each sentence.

The substantiation requirement — the FTC's rule that advertising claims must be supported by a reasonable basis before being made — is a structural extension of the deception framework. A company that advertises that its product "clinically proven to reduce wrinkles by 47 percent" is making a factual claim. The FTC's position is that making such a claim without adequate substantiation for it is itself deceptive, even if the company has no evidence one way or the other about whether the claim is true. You cannot assert a fact you have not established.

### The unfairness standard

The unfairness prong is harder to satisfy and less frequently invoked than deception, but it covers a different and important category: practices that harm consumers without necessarily misleading them.

The FTC's unfairness standard was codified in 1994 after years of contested development. Three elements are required. First, the practice must cause *substantial injury to consumers* — not trivial or speculative harm, but real economic harm or physical injury. Second, the injury must not be *reasonably avoidable* by consumers — if consumers can protect themselves through ordinary care or by reading available information, the practice is less likely to meet the unfairness standard. Third, the injury must not be *outweighed by countervailing benefits* to consumers or competition — the FTC performs something like a cost-benefit analysis, acknowledging that some practices that produce consumer harm also produce consumer benefits.

The unfairness standard catches practices that the deception standard cannot. A company that uses aggressive debt-collection tactics — calling consumers repeatedly at odd hours, making implicit threats — may not be making any false statements. Nothing it says is deceptive. But the practice can still be unfair if it causes substantial harm that consumers cannot reasonably avoid and that produces no countervailing benefit. The harm is real; it is just not produced by a lie.

The key practical difference between the two standards is the consumer-avoidance element. The deception standard focuses on what the seller said and whether a reasonable consumer would be misled by it. The unfairness standard focuses on whether the harm could have been avoided — which places more weight on the structure of the market and the information available to consumers, rather than just on the specific content of the seller's communication.

## What the FTC actually enforces: the major categories

The theoretical framework becomes concrete in the FTC's enforcement docket. Three categories dominate contemporary FTC consumer-protection enforcement.

### Advertising: the substantiation and endorsement frameworks

The FTC's advertising enforcement is built around two requirements that most consumers are unaware exist but that govern a substantial fraction of what they see in advertising.

The *substantiation requirement* holds that advertisers must have a reasonable basis for their claims before making them. For objective, verifiable claims — "clinically proven," "reduces cholesterol," "lasts 10 times longer" — the standard requires competent and reliable scientific evidence. For most health and safety claims, that means controlled studies. The FTC does not pre-approve advertising, but it investigates claims that appear unsupported and has brought major enforcement actions against companies that advertised health benefits without adequate scientific backing. POM Wonderful, the pomegranate juice company, settled an FTC enforcement action in 2013 after the agency found its advertising claims about treating heart disease and prostate cancer were not supported by adequate scientific evidence.

The *endorsement and testimonial guidelines* require that material connections between endorsers and advertisers be disclosed. A celebrity paid to appear in an advertisement obviously discloses the relationship by the nature of the ad. But the guidelines extend to less obvious cases: an influencer who receives free products and posts about them, a blogger who is paid for a review without saying so, a review platform that features promoted reviews indistinguishably from organic ones. The FTC's 2023 update to its endorsement guidelines specifically addressed social-media disclosure requirements, a recognition that the mechanisms of paid promotion have evolved substantially since the original guidelines were issued.

The *deceptive pricing* framework — "was $99.99, now $49.99" — requires that the "original" price be a genuine price at which the product was actually offered for sale, not a fictitious reference price set high to make the discount look larger. The FTC has brought enforcement actions against retailers whose "sale" prices were actually the regular prices and whose "original" prices were never genuinely available.

### Privacy and data security: the FTC as de facto regulator

The United States, unlike the European Union, does not have a comprehensive federal data-privacy statute. What it has instead is a patchwork of sector-specific statutes (HIPAA for health data, COPPA for children's data online, the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act for financial-services data) and, filling the gaps, the FTC.

The FTC's authority in privacy derives from Section 5's deception and unfairness prongs. When a company promises in its privacy policy that it will not share user data with third parties and then shares it, the FTC can bring a deception action — the privacy policy was a representation, consumers reasonably relied on it, and the violation was material. When a company fails to maintain adequate data security and consumers are harmed by the resulting breach, the FTC can bring an unfairness action — substantial consumer injury, not reasonably avoidable by individual consumers who have no visibility into the company's security practices.

The consequence is that the FTC has become the primary federal enforcement agency for data privacy and data security, not because Congress designed it that way, but because no other agency has clear jurisdiction and the FTC's broad statutory authority reaches the conduct.

*Figure 12.3 — U*

The two largest FTC privacy settlements illustrate the scope of this authority. In 2019, Facebook agreed to pay $5 billion to settle FTC charges arising from the Cambridge Analytica matter — in which user data was shared with a political consulting firm without adequate user consent, in violation of a 2012 FTC consent order Facebook had already entered into. The $5 billion settlement was at the time the largest FTC consumer-protection settlement in the agency's history. In 2022, Twitter agreed to pay $150 million to settle FTC charges that it had used phone numbers and email addresses collected for security purposes — two-factor authentication — to serve targeted advertising, again in violation of a prior FTC consent order.

Both cases involved violations of prior consent orders, which is the mechanism through which the FTC converts its otherwise-civil enforcement authority into something with sharper teeth: consent orders are binding obligations, and violating them enables the FTC to seek civil penalties per violation rather than just injunctive relief.

### Consumer financial services: overlapping jurisdictions

The FTC enforces several major consumer-financial-protection statutes: the Truth in Lending Act (which requires disclosure of credit terms in standardized form), the Fair Credit Reporting Act (which governs the accuracy of credit reports and consumers' rights to dispute them), and the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (which prohibits abusive, deceptive, and unfair debt-collection practices).

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, created by the Dodd-Frank Act in 2010, has overlapping jurisdiction over many of these areas and has become the primary regulator for banks, credit unions, and other financial institutions subject to its supervision. The FTC retains primary jurisdiction over non-bank financial entities — auto dealers, payday lenders, mortgage brokers, debt collectors — that the CFPB does not directly supervise. The division of labor is not always clean, and coordination between the two agencies has varied.

## The consent-order mechanism: how FTC enforcement actually works

Most people, if they think about government enforcement at all, imagine it as a process that ends in a trial. An agency investigates, charges, litigates, and a court renders a judgment. That is how criminal enforcement usually works. FTC consumer-protection enforcement almost never works that way.

The typical FTC enforcement trajectory is: investigation → complaint → negotiation → *consent order*. The firm under investigation, rather than litigating, enters into a settlement in which it agrees to specified conduct restrictions and, frequently, a monetary remedy. The firm does not admit liability. The consent order is entered by the FTC and becomes a binding obligation on the firm.

Consent orders typically run for twenty years. They often require the firm to establish and maintain specific compliance programs, to report regularly to the FTC on its compliance, and to notify the FTC of certain changes in its business structure. Violating a consent order is not merely a breach of contract; it is the basis for civil penalties, which can be substantial — up to tens of thousands of dollars per violation per day.

![FTC enforcement lifecycle ](images/12-unfair-trade-practices-and-the-federal-trade-commission-fig-04.png)
*Figure 12.4 — FTC enforcement lifecycle *

The consent-order structure has both advantages and limits. The advantage is efficiency: an enforcement action that would take years to litigate to a final judgment can be resolved in months through a consent order, and the result is a binding, ongoing obligation rather than a one-time penalty. The limit is that the firm does not admit wrongdoing, which reduces the deterrent effect on other firms that did not receive the order and can argue that their conduct is distinguishable.

The escalating-penalty structure for consent-order violations is where the FTC's enforcement teeth actually are. A firm that violates its original conduct obligations faces civil penalties that the agency can calibrate to the scale and duration of the violation. The Facebook $5 billion settlement was itself triggered by Facebook's alleged violation of the 2012 consent order it had entered into after an earlier privacy investigation. The message the FTC sends through consent-order enforcement is specific: a prior order is not a one-time payment that buys immunity. It is an ongoing obligation with ongoing enforcement consequences.

## What the framework is actually doing

Return to the problem the FTC was created to solve. Systematic, low-stakes-per-consumer deceptions — the false "clinically proven" claim, the undisclosed sponsored post, the fictitious reference price — are not efficiently addressed by individual consumer litigation. The consumers harmed are dispersed, each harm is small, and the cost of litigation exceeds the individual recovery. The aggregate harm may be large; the per-consumer harm is not. This is exactly the structural condition under which market mechanisms for accountability fail.

The FTC's framework addresses this with a different architecture. A single agency with enforcement authority can bring an action on behalf of all harmed consumers, impose a penalty calibrated to aggregate harm rather than individual injury, and obtain injunctive relief that stops the practice going forward. The cost of enforcement is borne by the government (and ultimately taxpayers) rather than by individual consumers, who cannot afford to bear it themselves.

The framework also addresses the information asymmetry that makes many consumer deceptions possible in the first place. Consumers generally cannot verify the scientific basis of health claims before purchasing. They cannot audit a company's data-security practices before sharing their personal information. They cannot reliably distinguish organic reviews from paid ones. The FTC's substantiation requirement, privacy enforcement, and endorsement guidelines each address a specific information asymmetry — requiring disclosure, substantiation, or verification in markets where consumers cannot protect themselves through ordinary care.

The limits of the framework are also real. The FTC has limited resources relative to the volume of potentially deceptive and unfair commercial activity. Its consent-order process produces no admission of wrongdoing, which limits the deterrent signal to other firms. In rapidly evolving markets — digital advertising, data brokerage, algorithmic pricing — the agency has repeatedly found itself applying a 1914 statutory framework to practices its drafters could not have imagined.

The substantive tension in Section 5 enforcement — between a deception standard that requires a false statement and an unfairness standard that can reach harmful practices without one — is also the framework's productive core. The deception standard is well-defined but narrow; it cannot reach practices that harm consumers through methods other than lies. The unfairness standard is broader but requires a cost-benefit analysis that is genuinely contestable. A practice that the FTC argues is unfair can almost always generate a counterargument that its costs to consumers are outweighed by its benefits to competition or consumer choice. The tension between the two standards is where most of the interesting FTC enforcement decisions live.

## What you can now do

You can explain what the FTC is and why it exists — not as an abstract description of a regulatory agency, but as a structural answer to the specific problem that market mechanisms and individual litigation cannot efficiently solve.

You can distinguish deceptive practices from unfair practices under Section 5, apply the three-element test for each to a specific fact pattern, and explain why the standards are not interchangeable — why a practice can satisfy one and not the other.

You can analyze the FTC's substantiation and endorsement frameworks as extensions of the deception standard and explain what each requires of an advertiser before a claim can be made or a sponsored post can be published.

You can trace the logic of the FTC's privacy enforcement authority — why an agency created in 1914 to address advertising fraud became the de facto primary federal privacy regulator a century later — and explain the mechanism through which the 2019 Facebook and 2022 Twitter settlements produced the outcomes they did.

And you can explain the consent-order mechanism: why enforcement typically ends in a consent order rather than a trial, why the twenty-year duration matters, and why violation of a prior order is more consequential than the original enforcement action.

The single idea that matters most is the structural insight embedded in the FTC's founding premise: some harms are too small per consumer and too large in aggregate to be addressed by individual litigation. The agency exists to bridge that gap. Whether it bridges it adequately in any given market, at any given time, is always a contestable question — but the structural problem it was designed to address has not changed.
