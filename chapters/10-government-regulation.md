# Chapter 10 — Government Regulation
*How Congress handed the details to someone else, and what happened next.*

---

Here is a puzzle that sits right at the center of American government, and that most people never notice because it has been there so long it looks like furniture.

The Constitution gives Congress the power to make law. Article I, Section 1: *All legislative Powers herein granted shall be vested in a Congress of the United States.* All of them. Congress. Not the president. Not the courts. Not some agency with a three-letter acronym. Congress.

And yet: when the Environmental Protection Agency issues a regulation requiring industrial facilities to limit their emissions, that regulation has the force of law. When the Securities and Exchange Commission requires public companies to file quarterly reports in a specific format, that requirement has the force of law. When the Food and Drug Administration concludes that a drug is not safe and effective and refuses to approve it, that conclusion has the force of law. None of these rules were passed by Congress. Congress did not vote on the specific emission limit, or the specific disclosure format, or the specific clinical evidence standard. Congress handed that job to someone else.

How does that work? Why does it work? And what does it mean for the businesses operating inside the system it produced?

That is what this chapter is about.

---

Start in 1887. The immediate problem was railroads, and it was a specific kind of unfairness that everyone could see. The railroads had developed a practice called rate discrimination — charging more for short hauls than long hauls on the same line. The reason was leverage. A farmer in a small Iowa town, with one railroad and no alternatives, paid higher rates than a shipper in Chicago who could play three carriers against each other. The railroad knew exactly who had options and who didn't, and it priced accordingly.

Congress decided this was unfair and passed the Interstate Commerce Act, creating the Interstate Commerce Commission to stop it. The ICC operated for 108 years, until 1995. By the time it was abolished, its original mission had been superseded by deregulation and its remaining functions transferred elsewhere. But what it left behind was not a set of railroad rules. It left behind a template: a body of federal law governing a specific industry, administered by a specialized agency with expertise in that industry, empowered by Congress to make the rules filling in the details the statute left open.

That template, replicated and expanded from the 1880s through the New Deal and into the present, is what we now call the administrative state. It is the apparatus through which most federal regulation of business actually operates — not through statutes that specify every requirement in detail, but through agencies that translate broad statutory mandates into operational rules.

The natural question is: why doesn't Congress just write the rules itself?

Two reasons, and both are structural rather than accidental.

The first is expertise. The Federal Aviation Administration sets safety standards for commercial aircraft. The Food and Drug Administration evaluates new drug applications. The Nuclear Regulatory Commission licenses reactor designs. The specific requirements — maintenance schedules, clinical trial protocols, containment specifications — are not things that 535 generalist legislators can write with any competence. Congress can articulate the policy objective: safe aircraft, effective drugs, contained nuclear material. It cannot write the technical rule. It needs people who actually understand the subject.

The second is time. Congress is engaged with everything simultaneously. If every OSHA workplace-safety standard, every EPA emissions limit, every SEC disclosure requirement had to go through the full legislative process — committee hearings, floor votes in both chambers, conference committee, presidential signature — the regulatory apparatus would freeze. There are thousands of specific technical requirements in the federal regulatory system. The administrative agency is the mechanism that allows regulatory capacity to scale beyond what the legislative process can produce.

The consequence is delegation: Congress passes an enabling statute that establishes an agency, defines its jurisdiction, articulates its mission, and grants it authority to promulgate regulations within that mission. The EPA operates under the Clean Air Act, the Clean Water Act, the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act, and several others. The SEC operates under the Securities Act of 1933, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, and subsequent statutes. The pattern is consistent across every major agency: Congress authorizes; the agency operationalizes.

The delegation creates an immediate constitutional tension. The Constitution gives lawmaking power to Congress. If Congress hands that power to an agency, is the agency making law? The short answer, developed through a century of constitutional litigation, is yes — within limits. The limits are the subject of the judicial review doctrine. But the delegation itself, the core mechanism of the administrative state, is constitutional and has been since the New Deal. The tension is managed, not resolved.

---

The Administrative Procedure Act of 1946 is the procedural constitution of the administrative state. Before the APA, agencies operated under widely varying and sometimes arbitrary procedures. The APA imposed uniform standards across the federal bureaucracy — specifying how agencies must make rules, how parties can challenge those rules, and on what grounds courts can set them aside.

The standard rulemaking process under the APA begins with a *Notice of Proposed Rulemaking*. The agency publishes the proposed rule in the Federal Register — the daily official publication of federal agency actions. The notice explains what the agency proposes to require, why it proposes to require it, and the legal authority on which it relies. The public then has a comment period — typically sixty to ninety days — to submit written responses.

The comment process is not a vote, and this distinction matters. The agency does not count comments and do whatever the majority requests. What the agency must do is *consider* the significant comments and respond to them in the final rule. If a trade association submits technical data challenging the scientific basis for the proposed rule, the agency must engage with that data. If ten thousand commenters argue that the agency's cost estimate is wrong, the final rule must address that argument. The requirement is reasoned response to material input, not capitulation to it.

After the comment period, the agency reviews what it received, revises the rule as it judges appropriate, and publishes a Final Rule in the Federal Register along with a *preamble* — sometimes hundreds of pages long in complex rulemakings — explaining its reasoning, responding to significant comments, and documenting the factual and legal basis for the rule.

The preamble is not decoration. It is the evidentiary record that courts review when the rule is challenged. A final rule with a thin, conclusory preamble is far more vulnerable to judicial challenge than one with a thorough record demonstrating that the agency considered the relevant factors and reached a reasoned conclusion. Regulatory lawyers spend enormous effort on preambles for exactly this reason — they are building the record that will matter if the rule ends up in court.

---

The federal regulatory apparatus is distributed across approximately 100 agencies with rulemaking authority. The major ones that affect business operations cluster into recognizable domains, and it is worth knowing the landscape.

The Environmental Protection Agency, established in 1970, administers the Clean Air Act, the Clean Water Act, the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act, and Superfund, among others. For any business with a manufacturing facility, a significant chemical inventory, or operations with environmental impact, EPA regulation is the most operationally consequential federal regulatory domain. The SEC, established in 1934 after the 1929 market collapse, administers the federal securities laws — its core mission is investor protection through mandatory disclosure, and any company with publicly traded securities operates under continuous SEC oversight. The Food and Drug Administration, whose origins trace to 1906, controls access to the U.S. market for drugs and medical devices: a drug cannot be marketed in the United States until the FDA has concluded it is safe and effective for its intended use, and the agency's approval process is among the most expensive and time-consuming regulatory requirements in any industry.

OSHA, established in 1970, sets and enforces workplace safety standards — including a general-duty clause requiring employers to address recognized hazards even when no specific standard exists. The FCC, established in 1934, regulates broadcast, telecommunications, and internet service, with jurisdiction that bears on any company in the communications industry and on a broad range of businesses subject to telephone marketing rules. The National Labor Relations Board administers the National Labor Relations Act and develops labor law primarily through adjudicated cases rather than rulemaking — its interpretations govern collective bargaining across essentially all private-sector employers. The FTC holds concurrent antitrust enforcement authority with the Department of Justice and consumer-protection jurisdiction over unfair or deceptive acts or practices, increasingly significant in the digital economy. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, established by Dodd-Frank in 2010, has jurisdiction over consumer financial products and has been legally contested on structural grounds since its creation.

The jurisdictional lines among these agencies are not always clean. Environmental regulation involves the EPA, the Army Corps of Engineers, the Fish and Wildlife Service, and OSHA. Financial regulation involves the SEC, the CFTC, the Federal Reserve, the OCC, the FDIC, and the CFPB. In sectors with overlapping jurisdiction, compliance means mapping all the relevant agencies, not just identifying a lead regulator.

Agency authority is not unlimited, and the mechanism that enforces the limits is judicial review.

The standard of APA review on the substance of agency rules is the arbitrary-and-capricious standard of Section 706: courts set aside agency action that is arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law. The standard is deferential but not a rubber stamp. The Supreme Court's articulation in *Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Association v. State Farm* (1983) is the operative requirement: the agency must have considered the relevant factors and articulated a rational connection between the facts found and the choice made. An agency that ignores significant contrary evidence, fails to consider a material alternative, or offers post-hoc rationalizations for a decision it had already reached will lose. *State Farm* itself arose from NHTSA's decision to rescind a passive-restraint requirement without adequately explaining why. The Court held that the agency had not engaged seriously with the evidence and sent the rule back for a more thorough analysis. The procedural discipline the APA imposes is real.

For forty years after *Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council* (1984), the most important doctrine governing judicial review of agency *statutory interpretation* was Chevron deference. The two-step framework was straightforward: first, if Congress has directly addressed the question, apply the statute. Second, if the statute is ambiguous, ask only whether the agency's interpretation is reasonable — if it is, defer to it. The practical effect was significant. When the Clean Air Act was ambiguous about whether greenhouse gases were air pollutants, the EPA's answer received judicial deference as long as it was reasonable. Agencies accumulated interpretive authority over the statutes they administered, and courts systematically stood back from second-guessing that authority.

In June 2024, *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* overruled *Chevron*. Chief Justice Roberts's majority opinion held that courts must exercise independent judgment in determining the best reading of ambiguous statutes. The Administrative Procedure Act requires courts to decide all relevant questions of law — and statutory interpretation is a question of law, properly for courts, not agencies. Forty years of Chevron deference ended with that opinion.

The immediate consequence is exposure. Regulations built on aggressive interpretations of ambiguous statutory authority are now more vulnerable. An agency that stretched its enabling statute to reach a new domain — claiming authority over digital assets under a securities law written in 1934, or authority to restructure the power grid under a Clean Air Act provision designed for facility-level controls — now faces a court that will independently assess whether the statute actually supports that reading. The *Chevron* cushion is gone.

That second example is *West Virginia v. EPA* (2022), decided two years before *Loper Bright*, which applied the *major questions doctrine*: when an agency claims authority to regulate matters of vast economic and political significance, it needs clear congressional authorization, not merely a plausible reading of ambiguous text. The EPA had argued that a Clean Air Act provision authorizing regulation of emissions at existing power plants authorized it to shift generation across the entire U.S. power sector toward renewables. The Court held this was a major question, that Congress had not clearly authorized it, and that the rule was invalid.

The major questions doctrine and the post-*Loper Bright* framework operate together. The bigger the regulatory claim — the more it looks like a significant restructuring of economic relationships rather than a technical implementation of an established statutory requirement — the more clearly Congress must have authorized it. Rules resting on explicit statutory language are stable. Rules resting on agency interpretation of ambiguous authority, which describes a significant portion of the regulatory landscape built up over the Chevron era, are more exposed than they were before 2024.

What does all of this mean for businesses operating inside the system?

Three structural features are constant across industries, and they are worth naming clearly.

The first is compliance cost. Regulatory requirements impose real costs: environmental compliance staff, OSHA safety programs, SEC disclosure infrastructure, FDA pharmacovigilance programs. For heavily regulated industries, these costs are substantial and essentially permanent. A pharmaceutical company's regulatory affairs function can run to hundreds of employees. A mid-sized bank's compliance organization is similarly large. Regulatory cost is properly understood as a fixed feature of operating in regulated industries — not an externality imposed on an otherwise efficient market, but part of the cost structure of doing business in a context where the government has decided certain outcomes matter enough to require them.

The second is regulatory risk. The regulatory environment is not static. Agencies promulgate new rules, revise existing ones, change enforcement priorities, and — as *Loper Bright* illustrates — find their interpretive authority renegotiated by courts. A business that built its operating model around a specific regulatory interpretation may find that interpretation reversed. Managing regulatory risk means monitoring the environment, participating in rulemakings when proposed rules would affect operations, and maintaining operational flexibility in structures that depend on regulatory assumptions.

The third feature is less often acknowledged: regulatory opportunity. Requirements that apply uniformly to all competitors raise compliance costs uniformly — no competitive disadvantage from the requirement itself. But requirements that your firm can meet more cheaply than competitors, because of superior process, technology, or scale, create competitive advantage. Regulatory expertise that allows faster navigation of complex approval processes — FDA drug approvals, SEC registration, EPA permits — translates into commercial timing advantages. The administrative state is not only a constraint. It is also a competitive landscape, and firms that understand it well enough to operate inside it strategically have advantages over firms that treat it purely as an obstacle.

---

The ICC set the template in 1887 because Congress needed a mechanism to translate a policy decision — railroad rate discrimination should stop — into operational rules that could govern thousands of specific transactions. The same logic that produced the ICC produces the EPA and the SEC and the FDA and every other agency on the list. The translation problem — from statutory policy to operational requirement — is not solved by legislation alone. It requires an institution with expertise, continuity, and the procedural discipline to make rules that can survive judicial review.

The APA is the framework that imposes that discipline. The notice-and-comment process is not bureaucratic ritual; it is the mechanism by which the agency assembles the evidentiary record that makes its rules defensible. The arbitrary-and-capricious standard is not a formality; it is the mechanism by which courts ensure that agencies are actually reasoning rather than just issuing commands. And *Loper Bright* is not an anomaly; it is a periodic recalibration of where the boundary between legislative authority and administrative authority actually sits.

The puzzle I started with — why does Congress hand its lawmaking power to someone else? — turns out to have a clean answer. It hands the details to agencies because details require expertise Congress doesn't have and time Congress doesn't have. It keeps control through the enabling statute, the APA's procedural requirements, and judicial review. The resulting system is not tidy. But it is the system, and understanding it is the minimum competency for operating inside the regulatory environment the United States has built.
