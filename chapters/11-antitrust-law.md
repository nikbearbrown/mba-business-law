# Chapter 11 — Antitrust Law
*What the government does when a market stops being a market.*

---

Here is a question that sounds simple until you think about it carefully: when does a company become too successful?

In a market economy, the whole point is to outcompete your rivals. You build a better product, cut your costs, win customers, grow. If you do this well enough for long enough, you end up dominating your market. And at that point — here is where it gets interesting — the government may decide you have broken the law.

This seems contradictory. The law encouraged you to compete. Competition produced a winner. Now the winner is illegal?

The resolution is a distinction that takes some work to see clearly: the difference between *competing* and *foreclosing competition*. Winning a market by making a better product is not only legal — it is the purpose of the system. Winning a market by making it structurally impossible for any competitor to challenge you is something different. The first creates value for consumers. The second extracts value from consumers who have nowhere else to go. Antitrust law is the apparatus that tries to keep those two things separated.

How it does that — the machinery, where it came from, what it can handle, and where it currently struggles — is what this chapter is about.

---

## Standard Oil and the statute it produced

In 1879, John D. Rockefeller created the Standard Oil Trust. The mechanism was elegant: the shareholders of forty competing oil companies transferred their shares to nine trustees, who managed all forty as a single coordinated enterprise. The companies nominally remained separate. In practice, they were one entity that controlled, by the late 1880s, roughly 90 percent of U.S. petroleum refining.

What Standard Oil did with that control is the textbook of anticompetitive behavior. It negotiated secret rebates from railroads that competitors could not obtain. It priced below cost in specific markets to drive out local rivals, subsidizing the losses with monopoly profits from markets where it faced no competition. It acquired pipelines and storage facilities and refused to give competitors access on reasonable terms. The result was not just market dominance — it was a system that made new entry nearly impossible and extracted profits from every downstream industry that depended on petroleum.

![Standard Oil's competitive playbook](images/11-antitrust-law-fig-01.png)
*Figure 11.1 — Standard Oil's competitive playbook*

Congress responded with the *Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890*. Section 1 prohibits contracts, combinations, or conspiracies in restraint of trade. Section 2 prohibits monopolization, attempted monopolization, or conspiracy to monopolize. The text takes up less than a page. That brevity was intentional. Congress recognized that specifying every anticompetitive practice in advance was impossible; the industries of 1950 would look nothing like 1890, and the industries of today look nothing like either. The statute needed to be a principle, not a list.

In 1911, the Supreme Court applied that principle to Standard Oil, ordering the company dissolved into 34 successor firms. Several of those successors — Exxon, Mobil, Chevron, Amoco — became major corporations in their own right. The structural breakup established the basic framework of antitrust enforcement, and the questions it raised — how much market power is too much, what practices are forbidden, what remedies are appropriate — remain contested today.

Congress added two more statutes in 1914. The *Clayton Act* targets specific practices the Sherman Act addresses only in general terms — exclusive-dealing arrangements, tying, mergers that substantially lessen competition — and provides the primary statutory framework for merger review. The *Federal Trade Commission Act* creates the FTC and prohibits unfair methods of competition, giving it concurrent civil-enforcement authority alongside the DOJ Antitrust Division. These three statutes — Sherman, Clayton, FTC Act — are the core of U.S. antitrust law, operating in parallel ever since.

## Two ways to analyze a violation

Suppose you have evidence that a group of competing companies agreed to charge the same prices. Which legal standard determines whether what they did was illegal?

The answer depends on which category the conduct falls into. The two categories are structured very differently, and understanding the difference is the analytical core of antitrust practice.

The first is *per se* analysis. Certain practices are automatically illegal. No inquiry into competitive effects. No defense based on business justification. No argument that the conduct actually helped consumers in this particular case. The practice happened; the violation is established. The per-se categories include horizontal price-fixing, market-allocation agreements, bid-rigging, and certain group boycotts.

The logic is that these practices are so reliably harmful, and so rarely defensible, that examining each case individually costs more than it's worth. A group of competing companies agreeing on prices is not doing something that might have competitive benefits in some circumstances; it is extracting money from buyers who would be better served by genuine competition. The per-se rule saves courts from relitigating that conclusion every time the same conduct appears in a new fact pattern.

The second is *rule of reason* analysis. Most antitrust conduct is not automatically illegal. It is evaluated based on actual effects in the specific market context. What is the defendant's market power? Does the practice harm competition, or reflect a legitimate business arrangement? Are there pro-competitive justifications that outweigh competitive harm? The rule of reason is a balancing test, producing less predictable outcomes than per-se analysis precisely because it is responsive to the specific facts of each case.

<!-- → TABLE: Standard of analysis | Defenses available | Evidence required | Predictability for defendants | Examples of conduct — two columns: Per Se | Rule of Reason. Per-se column should emphasize absence of defenses; rule-of-reason column should emphasize multi-factor balancing. -->

The boundary between the two categories has shifted substantially over the past five decades. In the 1960s and 1970s, the per-se categories were broad. Many vertical restraints — restrictions a manufacturer imposed on distributors — were treated as per-se illegal even when competitive effects were ambiguous. The shift began with *Continental T.V. v. GTE Sylvania* in 1977, which moved vertical non-price restraints from per-se to rule-of-reason. *Leegin Creative Leather Products* in 2007 completed the movement by overruling nearly a century of precedent to hold that vertical minimum resale price maintenance was no longer per se illegal either.

The intellectual engine of the shift was the Chicago School of antitrust economics, developed at the University of Chicago in the 1960s and 1970s. The core argument: most of the practices the old per-se rules condemned were actually efficiency-enhancing arrangements — they reduced transaction costs, prevented free-riding on distribution investment, or aligned incentives across supply chains. The per-se rules were suppressing pro-competitive conduct in the name of competition.

The counterargument — which has gained substantial force over the past decade — is that the Chicago School's models assumed markets correct themselves efficiently, that entry is relatively easy, and that dominant firms cannot sustain anticompetitive positions indefinitely. In technology platform markets, where network effects, switching costs, and data advantages can make incumbent positions nearly unassailable, those assumptions break down. The debate between these two frameworks is the central contested question in contemporary antitrust.

## What happens when companies merge

The Sherman Act governs what existing firms do. A separate question is what happens *before* firms become dominant — specifically, whether acquisitions that would create or reinforce market power should be blocked before they are consummated.

Clayton Act § 7 prohibits mergers that may substantially lessen competition or tend to create a monopoly. The Hart-Scott-Rodino Act of 1976 added a pre-merger notification requirement: firms above specified size thresholds must file with the FTC and DOJ and wait before closing. The current threshold is approximately $119 million. Transactions above it give the agencies a review window to assess competitive effects before the deal closes — which matters because unwinding a closed merger is far more disruptive than blocking an unconsummated one.

![HSR merger review process](images/11-antitrust-law-fig-02.png)
*Figure 11.2 — HSR merger review process*

Most reviewed mergers clear without incident. The agencies find no competitive concerns, or concerns are resolved through consent decrees — agreements under which the merging parties divest specified assets or accept behavioral restrictions as a condition of approval. A small fraction are challenged; the agencies file suit to block the transaction, and a court determines whether the merger violates the Clayton Act.

The analytical centerpiece of merger review is the *Herfindahl-Hirschman Index* — a measure of market concentration calculated by squaring each firm's market share and summing the results. A monopolist at 100 percent share has an HHI of 10,000. Ten firms each at 10 percent produce an HHI of 1,000. The agencies' current guidelines treat post-merger HHIs above 1,800 with increases above 100 as raising significant competitive concerns.

![HHI illustration](images/11-antitrust-law-fig-03.png)
*Figure 11.3 — HHI illustration*

The HHI is a starting point, not an answer. The harder question is always market definition: which products and geographies constitute the relevant market? A merger between two firms each holding 10 percent of a broadly defined market looks different from the same merger if the relevant market is narrower and each firm holds 40 percent. Parties and agencies fight most intensely over market definition precisely because the concentration numbers — and therefore the entire legal analysis — follow from it.

The contemporary intensification of merger enforcement, beginning around 2021, has substantially changed the practical landscape. The DOJ and FTC have challenged transactions that would have cleared in prior administrations — in technology, healthcare, and defense — and have lost several of those challenges in court, suggesting that enforcement has pushed beyond what current doctrine clearly supports. Whether courts will eventually update doctrine to reflect the agencies' theories, or whether the agencies will moderate, is the live operational question.

## The platform problem

For most of antitrust history, the dominant model of anticompetitive harm was straightforward: a firm with market power raises prices above competitive levels, restricts output, and extracts profits from buyers who cannot go elsewhere. The remedy was clear: break up the monopoly or prohibit the cartel, and prices fall.

Technology platform markets strain this model in a specific way. Consider Google's search business. Google's search engine is free to users. On the standard price-above-competitive-level model, there is no consumer harm to identify — the price is zero. But Google has, through its search distribution agreements with Apple and other device manufacturers, paid approximately $10 billion per year to be the default search engine on those devices. Those payments ensure that the vast majority of search queries flow through Google's system. Alternatives exist — Bing, DuckDuckGo, others. Users could switch. But default placement, combined with the habit formation of billions of users, makes Google's position nearly impregnable.

The DOJ filed suit against Google in 2020 under Sherman Act Section 2, arguing that the default-placement agreements were the mechanism by which Google had maintained its monopoly in general search. In 2024, the district court found for the government on liability: Google had illegally maintained its search monopoly through the default-placement agreements. As of 2025, the remedies phase is ongoing, with the government seeking structural relief — potentially including forced divestiture of Chrome or Android — and Google arguing that behavioral remedies suffice.

The FTC has brought a parallel case against Meta, arguing that the acquisitions of Instagram in 2012 and WhatsApp in 2014 were anticompetitive — that Meta bought potential competitors before they could challenge Facebook's dominance in social networking. The theory requires treating acquisitions that cleared merger review at the time as Sherman Act violations, which is legally unusual. The case turns on whether the acquisitions were motivated by a desire to neutralize competitive threats rather than to create value.

The DOJ brought a case against Apple in 2024 focused on the App Store's restrictions on competing payment systems and app distribution, arguing that Apple has used its control over iOS to maintain a monopoly in the market for iOS apps. Apple contests the market definition, arguing that the relevant market is the broader smartphone ecosystem — in which its share is 20 to 30 percent, well below monopoly levels.

All three cases reflect the same underlying tension: doctrine developed for industrial-era markets is being applied to markets whose structural features — network effects, zero-price products, data as a competitive input, platform control over complementary products — the doctrine's architects did not anticipate. Whether the doctrine can be extended to address those features, or requires fundamental revision, is a genuine open question being resolved through the outcome of these cases.

## The loop closes: Standard Oil to Google

Return to Standard Oil's competitive playbook and run it forward a century.

The practices that built Rockefeller's dominance — predatory pricing in specific markets subsidized by monopoly profits elsewhere, exclusive access to essential infrastructure, bundling to foreclose competition in adjacent markets — are structurally identical to what the contemporary technology cases allege. The legal framework applied to Standard Oil is the same framework, on the same statutory text, being applied to Google, Meta, and Apple today.

What has changed is not the statute but the markets.

![Standard Oil vs. platform monopoly — structural comparison](images/11-antitrust-law-fig-04.png)
*Figure 11.4 — Standard Oil vs. platform monopoly — structural comparison*

Standard Oil competed in a commodity market where the monopoly was built primarily through control of distribution infrastructure. Breaking it into competing refiners worked because petroleum refining doesn't exhibit network effects; two refinery companies in the same market actually compete for the same buyers on the merits. Breaking a search engine into two competing search engines might not work for the same reason the Standard Oil breakup *did* work — because the value of a search engine depends on the volume of query data available to train its algorithms, and half the data is much less than half the value. The structural feature that makes platform monopolies hard to dislodge is the same feature that makes them hard to break up.

This is the frontier question of contemporary antitrust: whether the tools developed to address industrial-era monopoly are adequate for markets where the monopoly is self-reinforcing in ways that physical infrastructure monopolies were not. It is not settled. The cases are still being decided. The doctrine is still being written.

## Back to the opening paradox

I started with a question: when does a company become too successful?

The chapter's apparatus gives you the answer with precision. The law draws a line between winning through competition and winning by foreclosing competition. Per-se rules apply to practices so reliably harmful — price-fixing, bid-rigging, market division — that the line is crossed every time the conduct occurs, no further analysis needed. Rule-of-reason analysis applies to everything else and requires evidence of actual competitive effects in the specific market context. Merger review gives the government a pre-closing opportunity to prevent competitive harm before it is locked in. And the enforcement frontier — in technology platforms, in healthcare, in any sector where structural barriers to entry make incumbents nearly unassailable — is where those tools are being actively tested against market structures their designers never imagined.

The distinction the law is trying to draw is real, even when it is hard to apply. Beating your rivals by making a better product makes consumers better off. Beating your rivals by paying $10 billion per year to be the default search engine on every device, while using that default position to prevent any competing search engine from reaching the query volume needed to compete — that is a different thing.

Whether it is always easy to tell the two apart is another matter. But the distinction is the point. Antitrust law exists because without it, the endpoint of unrestrained competition is not a market — it is a monopoly that replaces the market. The law's job is to prevent that replacement. Standard Oil is where it started. The platform cases are where it currently stands.
