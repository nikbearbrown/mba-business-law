# Antitrust Law

In 1911, the U.S. Supreme Court decided *Standard Oil Co. of New Jersey v. United States*, ordering the breakup of John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil — the company that had, through aggressive acquisition and integration over the previous four decades, controlled approximately 90 percent of U.S. petroleum refining. The Court ordered the company dissolved into 34 successor companies. Several of the successors — Exxon, Mobil, Chevron, Amoco — became major contemporary firms in their own right. The 1911 decision established the basic framework of U.S. antitrust enforcement.

This chapter is the operating manual for U.S. antitrust law as it applies to contemporary business.

## Learning objectives

- **Identify** the three major U.S. antitrust statutes — the Sherman Act, the Clayton Act, the Federal Trade Commission Act — and **distinguish** their respective scopes.
- **Apply** the *rule of reason* and *per se* analyses to specific anticompetitive practices.
- **Evaluate** merger review under the Hart-Scott-Rodino Act.
- **Articulate** the contemporary debate over antitrust enforcement in technology platform markets.

## Concept 1: The three antitrust statutes

**The Sherman Act of 1890.** Section 1 prohibits *contracts, combinations, or conspiracies in restraint of trade*. Section 2 prohibits *monopolization, attempted monopolization, or conspiracy to monopolize*. The Act provides for both civil and criminal enforcement; criminal violations carry up to ten years' imprisonment for individuals and corporate fines up to $100 million per violation.

**The Clayton Act of 1914.** Targets specific practices — exclusive-dealing arrangements, tying arrangements, mergers — that the Sherman Act addresses only generally. Clayton Act § 7 is the primary federal merger-review statute.

**The Federal Trade Commission Act of 1914.** Creates the FTC and prohibits *unfair methods of competition* (Section 5). The FTC has concurrent civil-enforcement authority with the DOJ Antitrust Division.

## Concept 2: Per se vs. rule of reason

Antitrust violations are analyzed under one of two frameworks.

**Per se analysis** treats specified practices as automatically illegal — no inquiry into competitive effects, no defense available. Per-se categories include horizontal price-fixing, market-allocation agreements among competitors, bid-rigging, and group boycotts. Defendants charged with per-se violations are convicted on proof of the conduct.

**Rule of reason analysis** weighs the competitive effects of the challenged practice. Practices that are not per-se illegal are evaluated based on their actual market effects, the parties' market power, the existence of pro-competitive justifications, and other factors. Most antitrust cases now operate under the rule of reason.

The shift from per-se to rule-of-reason has been substantial over the past four decades. *Continental T.V. v. GTE Sylvania* (1977) moved vertical non-price restraints from per-se to rule-of-reason. *Leegin Creative Leather Products v. PSKS* (2007) moved vertical price-fixing (resale price maintenance) from per-se to rule-of-reason. The current per-se categories are narrower than they were in the 1970s.

## Concept 3: Merger review

The Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act of 1976 requires firms above specified size thresholds to file pre-merger notifications with the FTC and DOJ Antitrust Division and to wait a specified period before consummating the merger. The agencies use the waiting period to review the merger for anticompetitive effects.

The current threshold (2024) is approximately $119 million. Mergers above the threshold trigger HSR filing; the agencies issue a *Second Request* for additional information when they identify potential concerns. Most reviewed mergers are cleared; a small fraction are challenged or modified.

Recent high-profile antitrust enforcement has focused on technology platforms — the DOJ's 2020 case against Google's search-distribution practices (with the 2024 liability finding); the FTC's case against Meta over the Instagram and WhatsApp acquisitions; the DOJ's 2024 case against Apple's App Store practices. The cases reflect a substantial intensification of technology-sector antitrust enforcement that was relatively quiet for two decades.
