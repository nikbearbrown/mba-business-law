# Chapter 11 — Antitrust Law
*What the government does when a market stops being a market.*

---

Here is a question that sounds simple until you think about it carefully: when does a company become too successful?

In a capitalist economy, the whole point is to outcompete your rivals. You build a better product, cut your costs, win customers, grow. If you do this well enough for long enough, you end up dominating your market. And at that point — here is the puzzle — the government may decide you have broken the law.

This seems contradictory. The law encouraged you to compete. Competition produced a winner. Now the winner is illegal? The resolution to the puzzle is the distinction between *competing* and *foreclosing competition*. Winning a market by making a better product is not only legal — it is the purpose of the system. Winning a market by making it structurally impossible for any competitor to challenge you is something different. The first creates value for consumers. The second extracts value from consumers who have nowhere else to go. Antitrust law is the apparatus that tries to keep those two things separated.

---

## Where the law comes from

In 1879, John D. Rockefeller created the Standard Oil Trust. The mechanism was elegant: the shareholders of forty competing oil companies transferred their shares to nine trustees, who managed all forty companies as a single coordinated enterprise. In exchange, the shareholders received trust certificates that paid dividends. The companies nominally remained separate. In practice, they were a single entity that controlled, by the late 1880s, approximately 90 percent of U.S. petroleum refining.

What Standard Oil did with that control is the textbook of anticompetitive behavior. It negotiated secret rebates from railroads that its competitors couldn't obtain. It priced below cost in specific markets to drive out local competitors, subsidizing the losses with profits from markets where it faced no competition. It acquired pipelines and storage facilities and then refused to give competitors access on reasonable terms. The result was not just market dominance — it was a system that made new entry nearly impossible and that extracted profits from the entire downstream industry that depended on petroleum.

Congress responded with the *Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890*, the first major federal antitrust statute. Section 1 prohibits *contracts, combinations, or conspiracies in restraint of trade*. Section 2 prohibits *monopolization, attempted monopolization, or conspiracy to monopolize*. The text is spare — the statute takes up less than a page — because Congress recognized that specifying every anticompetitive practice in advance was impossible. The industries of 1950 would look nothing like the industries of 1890; the industries of 2025 would look nothing like either. The statute needed to be a principle, not a list.

In 1911, the Supreme Court applied that principle to Standard Oil itself, ordering the company dissolved into 34 successor companies. Several of those successors — Exxon, Mobil, Chevron, Amoco — became major firms in their own right. The structural breakup established the basic framework of U.S. antitrust enforcement, and the questions it raised — how much market power is too much, what practices are forbidden, what remedies are appropriate — remain contested more than a century later.

Congress followed with two more statutes in 1914. The *Clayton Act* targets specific practices the Sherman Act addresses only generally — exclusive-dealing arrangements, tying arrangements, mergers — and provides the primary statutory framework for merger review. The *Federal Trade Commission Act* creates the FTC and prohibits *unfair methods of competition*, giving the FTC concurrent civil-enforcement authority with the DOJ Antitrust Division. The three statutes together constitute the core federal antitrust framework, and they have operated in parallel — sometimes in coordination, sometimes in competition — ever since.

## The two ways of analyzing an antitrust violation

Suppose you're a federal prosecutor, or the plaintiff's lawyer in a private antitrust suit, and you have evidence that a group of competing companies agreed to charge the same prices. What legal standard do you apply to determine whether what they did was illegal?

The answer depends on which category the conduct falls into — and the two categories are structured very differently.

The first is *per se* analysis. Certain practices are treated as automatically illegal. No inquiry into competitive effects. No defense based on business justification. No argument that the conduct actually helped consumers. The conduct happened; the violation is established. The per-se categories include horizontal price-fixing (competitors agreeing on prices), market-allocation agreements (competitors dividing territories so they don't compete with each other), bid-rigging (competitors coordinating on which of them will win a competitive procurement), and group boycotts (competitors jointly refusing to deal with specific customers or suppliers).

The logic behind per-se treatment is that these practices are so reliably harmful, and so rarely defensible, that the cost of examining each case individually exceeds the benefit. A group of competing companies agreeing to fix prices is not doing something that might have competitive benefits in some circumstances; it is extracting money from buyers who would be better served by genuine competition. The per-se rule saves courts from having to relitigate that conclusion every time the same conduct appears in a new fact pattern.

The second is *rule of reason* analysis. Most antitrust conduct — exclusive dealing arrangements, vertical price restrictions, tie-in sales, a wide range of distribution practices — is not automatically illegal. It is evaluated based on its actual effects in the specific market context. What is the defendant's market power? Does the practice actually harm competition, or does it reflect a legitimate business arrangement? Are there pro-competitive justifications that outweigh the competitive harm? The rule of reason is a balancing test, and it produces less predictable outcomes than per-se analysis precisely because it is responsive to the specific facts of each case.

The boundary between the two categories has shifted substantially over the past five decades. In the 1960s and 1970s, the per-se categories were broad. The Supreme Court treated many vertical restraints — restrictions a manufacturer imposed on its distributors — as per-se illegal even when the economic effects were ambiguous. The shift began with *Continental T.V. v. GTE Sylvania* in 1977, which moved vertical non-price restraints (territorial restrictions on distributors, for example) from per-se to rule-of-reason. *Leegin Creative Leather Products v. PSKS* in 2007 completed the movement by overruling nearly a century of precedent to hold that vertical price-fixing — a manufacturer's agreement with its retailers on minimum resale prices — was no longer per se illegal either.

The intellectual engine of the shift was the Chicago School of antitrust economics, centered at the University of Chicago in the 1960s and 1970s. The core argument: most of the practices the old per-se rules condemned were actually efficiency-enhancing arrangements that reduced transaction costs, prevented free-riding, or aligned incentives across distribution chains. The per-se rules were suppressing pro-competitive conduct in the name of competition. The rule of reason, applied correctly with economic rigor, would do better.

The counterargument — which has gained renewed force in the past decade — is that the Chicago School's models assumed markets correct themselves efficiently, that entry is easy, and that dominant firms cannot sustain anticompetitive positions indefinitely. In technology platform markets, where network effects, switching costs, and data advantages can make incumbent positions nearly unassailable, those assumptions break down. The debate between these two frameworks is the central contested question in contemporary antitrust.

## What happens when companies merge

The Sherman Act governs what existing firms do. A separate question is what happens before firms become dominant — specifically, whether mergers and acquisitions that would create or reinforce market power should be blocked before they are consummated.

The Clayton Act § 7 prohibits mergers that may substantially lessen competition or tend to create a monopoly. The Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act of 1976 added a pre-merger notification requirement: firms above specified size thresholds must file with the FTC and DOJ Antitrust Division and wait before closing the transaction. The current threshold (2024) is approximately $119 million. Transactions above the threshold give the agencies a review window to assess competitive effects.

Most reviewed mergers clear without incident. The agencies identify no competitive concerns, or the concerns are resolved through *consent decrees* — agreements in which the merging parties divest specified assets or accept behavioral restrictions as a condition of approval. A small fraction of reviewed mergers are challenged; the agencies file suit to block the transaction, and a court determines whether the merger violates the Clayton Act.

The analytical centerpiece of merger review is the *Herfindahl-Hirschman Index* (HHI) — a measure of market concentration calculated by squaring each firm's market share and summing the results. A market with one firm at 100% share has an HHI of 10,000 (monopoly). A market with ten firms each at 10% share has an HHI of 1,000 (relatively unconcentrated). The agencies' current merger guidelines treat post-merger HHIs above 1,800 with increases above 100 as raising significant competitive concerns.

The HHI is a starting point, not an answer. The harder question is always market definition: which products and geographies are in the relevant market? A merger between two firms that each have 10% of a broadly defined market looks different from the same merger if the relevant market is defined more narrowly and each firm has 40%. Parties and agencies fight most intensely over market definition precisely because the concentration numbers, and therefore the legal analysis, follow from it.

Right: same market after top two firms merge, resulting in 45/20/15/10/10 (HHI = 2,850, highly concentrated, delta = 1,200). Reader should see how a merger can move a market from the "unconcentrated" to "highly concentrated" zone and why the post-merger HHI and the delta both matter to the agencies.] -->

The contemporary intensification of merger enforcement began around 2021 and has substantially changed the practical landscape. The DOJ and FTC have challenged transactions that would have cleared in prior administrations — including transactions in technology, healthcare, and defense markets — and have lost a number of those challenges in court, suggesting that the agencies have pushed enforcement beyond what current doctrine clearly supports. Whether the courts will eventually update their doctrine to reflect the agencies' updated theories, or whether the agencies will moderate their enforcement posture, is the live operational question in merger review.

## The platform problem

For most of antitrust history, the dominant model of anticompetitive harm was straightforward: a firm (or a cartel) with market power raises prices above competitive levels, restricts output, and extracts profits from buyers who cannot go elsewhere. The remedy was correspondingly clear: break up the monopoly, or prohibit the cartel, and let prices fall to competitive levels.

Technology platform markets strain this model. Consider Google's search business. Google's search engine is free to users. On the standard price-above-competitive-level model, there is no consumer harm to speak of — the price is zero. But Google has, through its search distribution agreements with Apple and other device manufacturers, paid approximately $10 billion per year (as of the early 2020s) to be the default search engine on those devices. Those payments ensure that the vast majority of search queries — and therefore the vast majority of search advertising revenue — flow through Google's system. Bing exists. DuckDuckGo exists. Users could switch. But default placement, combined with the habit formation of billions of users, makes Google's position nearly impregnable.

The DOJ filed suit against Google in 2020 under Sherman Act Section 2, arguing that the default-placement agreements were the mechanism by which Google had maintained its monopoly in general search. In 2024, the district court found for the government on the liability question: Google had illegally maintained its search monopoly through the default-placement agreements. As of 2025, the remedies phase of the case is ongoing, with the government seeking structural relief — potentially including forced divestiture of the Chrome browser or the Android operating system — and Google arguing that behavioral remedies are sufficient.

The Google case is the most significant antitrust enforcement action in the United States since the Microsoft case of the late 1990s and early 2000s. It raises questions the existing doctrine is not fully equipped to answer. The harm to users is not high prices — it is foreclosed competition and reduced innovation in search. The standard consumer-welfare framework, which focuses on price effects, struggles to quantify harms measured in terms of innovation and choice. The case for structural remedies — breaking Google apart — runs into the question of whether the parts would actually compete with each other or whether the structural features of the search market (data advantages, network effects, default inertia) would simply reconcentrate the industry in the same firm or a different one.

The FTC has brought a parallel case against Meta, arguing that the acquisitions of Instagram in 2012 and WhatsApp in 2014 were anticompetitive — that Meta bought potential competitors before they could challenge Facebook's dominance in social networking. The theory requires treating acquisitions that cleared merger review at the time as Sherman Act violations, which is legally unusual. The case turns on whether the acquisitions were motivated by a desire to neutralize competitive threats rather than to create value through integration.

The DOJ brought a case against Apple in 2024 focused on the App Store's restrictions on competing payment systems and app distribution, arguing that Apple has used its control over iOS distribution to maintain a monopoly in the market for iOS apps. Apple contests the market definition, arguing that the relevant market is the broader smartphone ecosystem and that its 20-30% market share in that market does not constitute monopoly power.

All three cases reflect the same underlying tension: the antitrust doctrine developed in the industrial-era and early-commercial-era is being applied to markets with structural features — network effects, zero-price products, data as a competitive input, platform control over complementary products — that the doctrine's architects did not anticipate. Whether the doctrine can be extended to address those features, or whether it requires fundamental revision, is a genuine open question that will be resolved primarily through the outcome of these cases.

## The framework in operation

Return to the Standard Oil structure and run it forward a century.

The practices that built Standard Oil's dominance — predatory pricing in specific markets subsidized by monopoly profits elsewhere, exclusive access to essential infrastructure (pipelines then, app stores and operating systems now), bundling to foreclose competition in adjacent markets — are structurally identical to what the contemporary technology cases allege. The legal framework that addressed Standard Oil's conduct is the same framework, on the same statutory text, being applied to Google, Meta, and Apple today.

What has changed is not the statute but the markets. Standard Oil competed in a commodity market with relatively easy entry at the production level, where the monopoly was built primarily through control of distribution infrastructure. Technology platform monopolies are built on network effects that make the dominant platform more valuable to each user the more users it has — which means the monopoly is self-reinforcing in a way that Standard Oil's was not. Breaking up Standard Oil into competing refiners worked because petroleum refining doesn't exhibit network effects; two refinery companies in the same market actually compete. Breaking up a search engine into two competing search engines might not work for the same reason, because the value of a search engine depends on the volume of query data it can use to train its algorithms, and half the data is much less than half the value.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: Standard Oil vs. Google structural comparison — two parallel columns. Left: Standard Oil's monopoly mechanics [control of distribution infrastructure → exclusion of competitors → price extraction]. Right: Google's monopoly mechanics [control of default placement → exclusion of search competitors through habit formation and data advantages → advertising revenue extraction]. Bottom row: Why the same remedy (structural breakup) worked then but may not work now [petroleum refining has no network effects / search has strong data-driven network effects]. Reader should see the structural parallel and the structural difference simultaneously.] -->

This is the frontier question of contemporary antitrust: whether the tools developed to address industrial-era monopoly are adequate for the structural features of digital markets, and if not, what would replace them. It is not a question that has been answered. The cases are still being decided. The doctrine is still being written.

The practitioner's takeaway from this chapter is the framework, not the resolution. Per-se rules apply to a narrow set of practices so reliably harmful that market effects don't need to be measured. Rule-of-reason analysis applies to everything else and requires evidence of actual competitive effects. Merger review gives the government a pre-closing opportunity to prevent competitive harm before it is locked in. And the enforcement frontier — in technology markets, in healthcare, in any sector where platform dynamics create structural barriers to entry — is where the framework is being actively tested and revised.

Understanding the framework means knowing which analytical track applies to a given practice, what evidence each track requires, and where the contested questions in contemporary enforcement are focused. The rest is application.

### Warm-up

**Exercise 11.1.** *Tests learning objective: per se vs. rule of reason.* Three competing regional grocery chains meet and agree to divide their city into non-overlapping delivery zones. Identify which analytical framework applies, explain why, and state whether a court needs to examine competitive effects to find a violation. Difficulty: easy.

**Exercise 11.2.** *Tests learning objective: the three statutes.* A manufacturer tells its retailers they may not sell its products below a specified minimum price. Identify which statute(s) could apply, which analytical framework governs after *Leegin*, and what the plaintiff would need to show to establish a violation. Difficulty: easy.

### Application

**Exercise 11.3.** *Tests learning objective: merger review.* Two hospital systems in a mid-sized city propose to merge. Hospital A has 45% of inpatient admissions; Hospital B has 30%. Calculate the pre-merger and post-merger HHI, identify whether the result triggers significant concern under the agencies' guidelines, and describe the likely review path under HSR. Difficulty: moderate.

**Exercise 11.4.** *Tests learning objective: market definition.* The DOJ and Apple dispute whether the relevant market in the App Store case is "iOS app distribution" or "smartphone ecosystems." Construct the strongest argument for each market definition and explain how the choice affects the monopoly-power analysis. Difficulty: moderate.

### Synthesis

**Exercise 11.5.** *Tests learning objective: the platform problem.* The Chicago School's consumer-welfare standard focuses on price effects. Apply that standard to Google's search monopoly (where the consumer price is zero) and explain why it produces an incomplete analysis. Then propose an alternative welfare standard that would capture the harm the DOJ alleged, and identify the measurement challenges your proposed standard creates. Difficulty: harder.

### Challenge

**Exercise 11.6.** *Tests learning objective: remedies design.* The DOJ has proposed forced divestiture of Chrome or Android as a remedy in the Google search case. Design an alternative remedy — behavioral rather than structural — that would address the default-placement mechanism without breaking up the company. Identify what your remedy would require Google to do, how compliance would be monitored, and what competitive harm, if any, your remedy would fail to address. Difficulty: challenge.
