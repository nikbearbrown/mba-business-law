# Chapter 5 — Criminal Liability
*The one risk you cannot settle your way out of.*

---

Here is what makes criminal law different from everything else in this book.

When a contract dispute goes wrong, you can be sued. When a tort claim goes wrong, you can be sued. When you run afoul of a regulation, you can be fined. In every one of those cases, money is the mechanism: you pay something, the problem resolves, you return to running your business. The consequences can be large — we will see cases in this chapter where they ran to tens of billions of dollars — but they operate in the same domain as the original transaction. Value was exchanged, value was lost, books are rebalanced.

Criminal law does not work that way. When the government charges you with a crime, the remedy on the table has nothing to do with rebalancing books. The remedy is imprisonment. No settlement buys that back. No insurance policy covers it. No board-approved remediation plan substitutes for it. The executive who walks out of a courtroom in handcuffs does not come back on Monday morning to run the firm.

This is why criminal liability is the most consequential single risk in business operation. Not the largest financial risk — civil litigation produces larger dollar figures. The most consequential, because it is the one category where the practitioner's personal liberty, not just the firm's balance sheet, is on the line. Everything else in business law operates in a world where you can pay your way to resolution. Criminal law is the exception. Understanding why, and understanding the structure that produces that exception, is what this chapter is about.

---

## What white-collar crime actually is

In 1939, the criminologist Edwin Sutherland stood up at the American Sociological Society's annual meeting and introduced a term that has been in operational use ever since: *white-collar crime* — crime committed by persons of respectability and high social status in the course of their occupation. The FBI's contemporary definition is consistent: white-collar crime is characterized by deceit, concealment, or violation of trust and is not dependent on the application or threat of physical force or violence.

Three structural features in that definition recur in every specific case, and they are worth naming precisely.

*Deceit.* The offender misrepresents facts — to investors, to regulators, to partners, to customers. The misrepresentation is intentional. The purpose is monetary gain. This is the core of fraud in all its subcategories.

*Concealment.* The offense is hidden within the normal course of business activity. This is what makes white-collar crime difficult to detect and expensive to prosecute. A mugging is visible; an embezzlement running for five years through a corporate accounts-payable system is not. The concealment is frequently itself a separate criminal act — document destruction, false entries in corporate records, misleading statements to auditors. The cover-up becomes the crime alongside the original offense.

*Violation of trust.* The offender typically occupies a position of authority or fiduciary responsibility within a legitimate organization. The crime is the weaponization of that trust. The bookkeeper trusted with access to corporate funds, the investment manager trusted with client assets, the executive trusted with investor capital — the white-collar offense is the conversion of that trust into a private extraction mechanism.

These three features produce a distinctive profile. The offenders tend to be older, more educated, and more established than street-crime offenders. The offenses tend to run for extended periods before detection — months, years, sometimes decades. Detection typically happens through external review: a bank raising concerns, an auditor asking uncomfortable questions, a whistleblower. Not through anyone catching the offense in progress. And when the consequences arrive, they can be enormous.

## The canonical case

In December 2008, an investor in a private fund operated by a 70-year-old New York money manager named Bernard Madoff requested redemption of approximately $7 billion in account balances. Madoff Investment Securities did not have the funds available. The reason was not that markets had moved against the firm's positions. The reason was that the firm had no positions. Madoff had been operating, since at least the early 1990s and possibly since the firm's founding decades earlier, a pure Ponzi scheme: paying earlier investors with the deposits of later investors, with no underlying investment activity at all. Total estimated investor losses: approximately $65 billion.

Madoff confessed to his sons in December 2008. His sons reported him to federal authorities. He was arrested December 11. He pleaded guilty in March 2009 to eleven federal felonies: securities fraud, wire fraud, mail fraud, money laundering, perjury, theft from an employee benefit plan, and several others. The sentence was 150 years in federal prison. He died in custody in April 2021.

![Madoff timeline](images/05-criminal-liability-fig-02.png)
*Figure 5.2 — Madoff timeline*

The Madoff case is the largest single instance of white-collar crime in American history. It is also, structurally, completely unremarkable. The elements — a position of trust, an intentional misrepresentation sustained over years, concealment within apparently normal business operations, eventual discovery through external pressure — are identical to thousands of smaller cases prosecuted every year at the federal and state level. What distinguishes Madoff is scale, not mechanism. The mechanism is the template.

To understand why the mechanism produces the legal consequences it does, you need to understand how the statutory framework is built.

## The fraud statute system

Most white-collar crime is, at its core, fraud: the intentional misrepresentation of material facts for monetary gain. Federal law has constructed a framework of specific fraud statutes that function as a system, and the architecture is worth understanding.

The broadest instruments are the mail fraud and wire fraud statutes — 18 U.S.C. §§ 1341 and 1343. They prohibit any scheme to defraud that uses the U.S. mails or interstate wires in furtherance of the scheme. The breadth is deliberate. Almost any modern fraud touches interstate wires somewhere — an email, a phone call, an electronic funds transfer. That contact alone is sufficient to establish federal jurisdiction for a felony prosecution. The mail and wire fraud statutes are the most-charged federal fraud statutes in operation precisely because they reach virtually any fraudulent scheme that modern commerce produces.

Layered on top of the general statutes are category-specific ones: securities fraud under 18 U.S.C. § 1348 and Rule 10b-5 of the Securities Exchange Act; bank fraud under 18 U.S.C. § 1344; healthcare fraud under 18 U.S.C. § 1347; tax fraud. Each has its own enforcement regime and sentencing framework. The securities-fraud regime is particularly developed, because the volume of securities transactions and the scale of potential harm create large incentives to commit fraud and substantial regulatory infrastructure to prosecute it.

One mechanism worth understanding in detail is *embezzlement* — the unauthorized taking of property by someone who has lawful possession of it. The distinction from ordinary theft is precise: the thief takes what was never his; the embezzler takes what was temporarily, legitimately in his custody. The bookkeeper with check-writing authority who diverts funds to personal use is the classical case.

The Koss Corporation case shows how this plays out in practice. Sujata Sachdeva served as Vice President of Finance and Principal Accounting Officer at Koss, a Milwaukee audio-equipment manufacturer, from approximately 2004 through 2009. She embezzled approximately $34 million — primarily by drawing checks on corporate accounts for personal purchases. The documented purchases included roughly 22,000 items of clothing, 460 pieces of jewelry, and substantial sums in luxury travel. The embezzlement was not detected by the company's auditors. It was discovered by American Express, which raised concerns about the source of funds being used to settle Sachdeva's personal credit-card bills. She was convicted of multiple counts of wire fraud and sentenced to eleven years in federal prison, ordered to pay $34 million in restitution. Koss's auditor, Grant Thornton, subsequently faced civil litigation for failing to detect the embezzlement over a five-year period.

The mechanism in Sachdeva's case is the same as in Madoff's: a position of trust, a sustained intentional misrepresentation, concealment within normal business records, detection through external review that internal controls missed. The scale differs by a factor of nearly two thousand. The structure is identical.

## Beyond fraud: the broader enforcement map

The fraud framework is the center of white-collar criminal law, but the map extends beyond it. A practitioner who understands general fraud but not the specific enforcement categories operates with an incomplete picture.

*Antitrust.* The Sherman Act of 1890 prohibits contracts, combinations, or conspiracies in restraint of trade and monopolization or attempted monopolization. It provides for both civil and criminal enforcement. Naked price-fixing, bid-rigging, and market-allocation agreements are typically prosecuted as criminal felonies: corporate fines can reach $100 million per violation, individual sentences up to ten years. The Department of Justice's Antitrust Division maintains a leniency program under which the first member of a price-fixing conspiracy to report it receives immunity from prosecution. That program has been the primary source of cartel-enforcement cases for two decades — a structural design that makes the conspiracy itself the mechanism of its own detection.

*RICO.* The Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act of 1970 was designed as an anti-organized-crime statute. It has been applied well beyond that original target. RICO prohibits the operation of an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity — defined as two or more predicate offenses (mail fraud, wire fraud, bribery, extortion, and many others) committed within ten years as part of an ongoing enterprise. Penalties run to twenty years per count, plus criminal forfeiture of assets traceable to the racketeering. The civil-RICO framework permits private parties to sue for treble damages, which makes it an attractive instrument in commercial disputes involving systematic fraud. The interesting design question is why RICO creates charges based on predicate offenses that are themselves separately prosecutable. The answer is that the pattern requirement captures something the individual charges miss: the ongoing criminal enterprise as a distinct object of prosecution, separate from the individual offenses that constitute it.

*The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act.* The FCPA of 1977 prohibits U.S. persons and U.S.-listed firms from paying anything of value to foreign government officials to obtain or retain business. It is enforced jointly by the SEC and the DOJ. Its jurisdictional reach is broad: it applies to U.S. citizens anywhere in the world, to U.S.-incorporated firms anywhere, and to foreign firms with U.S.-listed securities in connection with conduct anywhere. Telia settled for $965 million in 2017 for systematic bribery of Uzbek officials to obtain telecommunications licenses. That scale of consequence is now routine in FCPA enforcement.

*Money laundering.* 18 U.S.C. §§ 1956 and 1957 criminalize the process of disguising proceeds of criminal activity as legitimate funds, with penalties up to twenty years per count. The Bank Secrecy Act imposes transaction-reporting requirements on financial institutions; failure to file required Suspicious Activity Reports is itself a federal crime. The path from regulatory enforcement to criminal prosecution, in cases involving systematic misconduct, is well-worn.

## The Constitution in the boardroom

Criminal prosecutions operate inside the Bill of Rights. Three constitutional protections recur frequently enough in business-crime contexts that they need explicit attention — and they operate differently than you might expect from their ordinary applications.

The *Fourth Amendment* prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures. In street crime, the Fourth Amendment question is usually whether the police could search the defendant's person or premises. In white-collar prosecutions, the operative question is almost never about physical searches of business premises. It is about subpoenas and warrants directed at third parties — banks, accounting firms, internet service providers — who hold the documentary and electronic evidence that establishes the offense. The document was created in the course of business, stored by a financial institution or email provider, and obtained by prosecutors through compelled disclosure. Whether the exclusionary rule applies to compelled third-party disclosures — and under what conditions — is the actual Fourth Amendment battleground in most white-collar cases.

The *Fifth Amendment* provides that no person shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself. Two applications matter here, and they are frequently confused. First: the protection applies primarily to *testimonial* compulsion — what you are forced to say — not to the production of pre-existing documents. You can generally be compelled to produce business records even if those records incriminate you. What you cannot be compelled to do is testify about their meaning. Second, and more important: corporations are not natural persons. A corporation can generally be compelled to produce its records. The individual officer who created those records can assert a personal Fifth Amendment privilege against testifying about them. When a firm is under investigation, the Fifth Amendment interests of the firm and of its individual employees can diverge sharply — which is why experienced white-collar defense counsel always advises clients to assess individual and corporate exposure separately, from the first day.

The *Sixth Amendment* provides for the right to counsel in criminal prosecutions and the right to jury trial. The right to counsel attaches at the initiation of formal proceedings. In the typical white-collar investigation, however, the defendant is not arrested and questioned in custody the way a street-crime suspect is; he is called before a grand jury or interviewed voluntarily. That procedural difference matters: the *Miranda* doctrine, which requires that custodial interrogation be preceded by warnings about Fifth and Sixth Amendment rights, applies with less force in most white-collar contexts than in street-crime ones. What applies instead is the pre-trial grand jury process, which operates under different rules and gives prosecutors substantial coercive authority that defense counsel must navigate carefully.

## Criminal and civil: two tracks, one set of facts

The same underlying conduct can produce both criminal and civil consequences simultaneously. This is a structural feature of the system, not an anomaly, and understanding how the two tracks interact is practically important.

Criminal cases are brought by the government. The standard of proof is beyond a reasonable doubt — the highest standard in American law. The remedies are imprisonment, fines paid to the government, and forfeiture of assets traceable to the crime. The full force of the Fourth, Fifth, and Sixth Amendments applies.

Civil cases are brought by private parties or by the government in regulatory enforcement actions. The standard of proof is preponderance of the evidence — more likely than not — or, for certain claims, clear and convincing evidence. The remedies are monetary damages paid to the plaintiff, injunctions, and specific performance. The constitutional protections specific to criminal proceedings do not apply with the same force.

Two columns: Criminal | Civil. Reader should be able to use as a checklist when analyzing a fact pattern that might produce both. -->

The Madoff case illustrates what this looks like in practice. The criminal prosecution produced a 150-year sentence. A separately appointed civil trustee ran a parallel recovery effort; through 2024, approximately $14 billion of the estimated $20 billion in actual cash losses had been recovered — a civil process producing real financial restitution running alongside and after the criminal proceeding. The Volkswagen diesel-emissions fraud produced criminal pleas by the corporation and individual executives, civil settlements totaling more than $30 billion, and regulatory enforcement actions across multiple jurisdictions. Wells Fargo followed the same multi-track pattern: regulatory enforcement first, then criminal investigations and prosecutions of individual employees.

Criminal prosecution typically moves first, because the government's coercive authority — grand jury subpoenas, search warrants — operates on a different timeline than civil discovery. The criminal resolution then frames the civil proceedings that follow. A criminal conviction establishes facts that civil litigants can use; a deferred prosecution agreement may require the firm to cooperate in ways that benefit civil plaintiffs; an acquittal complicates but does not foreclose civil recovery. The practitioner facing potential exposure must assess both tracks simultaneously, from the beginning, because the decisions made in the first days — whether to cooperate, what to produce, who to retain — affect outcomes on both tracks for years.

## The apparatus in operation

Consider a mid-sized financial services firm — call it Hartley — notified that the Department of Justice is investigating its anti-money-laundering compliance. The investigation was triggered by a Suspicious Activity Report filed by a peer institution about transactions Hartley processed for a foreign client subsequently indicted on drug-trafficking charges. The Hartley executive responsible for the foreign-client relationship has retained personal counsel.

Here is how the chapter's apparatus applies.

The criminal exposure falls into recognizable categories: money laundering under 18 U.S.C. §§ 1956–1957 if Hartley knowingly processed illicit funds; Bank Secrecy Act violations if Hartley failed to file required SARs; aiding and abetting the underlying drug-trafficking offense if the firm's conduct facilitated it. The exposure depends on what Hartley knew and when it knew it — the standard question in any fraud or conspiracy prosecution, and the question that will drive every subsequent decision.

The enforcement infrastructure is multi-pronged. DOJ leads the criminal investigation. FinCEN conducts parallel regulatory enforcement. Private parties harmed by the underlying drug-trafficking may sue under civil RICO if the pattern requirement is satisfied. Three separate proceedings can run simultaneously, each with its own standards, procedures, and potential remedies. This is not extraordinary; it is the normal architecture of a serious white-collar investigation.

The constitutional analysis separates the firm from the individual. Hartley-the-corporation has limited constitutional protection; it can generally be compelled to produce its records. The executive has substantially more: a Fifth Amendment right against compelled self-incriminating testimony, a Sixth Amendment right to counsel in any formal proceeding, and a critical interest in assessing personal exposure separately from the firm's. The firm's cooperation with prosecutors — potentially beneficial to Hartley's own negotiating position — may produce evidence that the executive's counsel needs to contest. Those interests can diverge sharply and quickly. Treating them as identical is a common and expensive mistake.

![Hartley fact pattern mapped across the chapter's three analytical frames](images/05-criminal-liability-fig-03.png)

The full resolution of a matter this complex typically runs for years across multiple proceedings. A deferred prosecution agreement for the firm, individual guilty pleas from relevant executives, and a multi-million-dollar fine do not end it. Civil litigation by private parties can follow; regulatory consequences can follow. The criminal proceeding is the first track to resolve, not the only one.

## Back to the opening

I started this chapter with a claim: criminal liability is the most consequential single risk in business operation, because it is the one category where the mechanism of resolution is not money.

The apparatus this chapter has built gives that claim precise content. The fraud statutes are broad enough to reach virtually any scheme that uses modern commerce's infrastructure. The enforcement map extends to antitrust, RICO, FCPA, and money laundering. The constitutional framework operates differently for firms than for individuals, creating divergent interests that must be assessed separately. And the criminal-civil distinction means that a criminal conviction is not the end of a matter — it is the beginning of a much longer multi-track process.

What the criminal framework does, at its core, is impose a category of consequence that money cannot substitute for. That is not an accident of legal design. It is a deliberate structural choice: certain violations of trust, certain acts of sustained deceit, certain extractions from legitimate organizations are treated as wrongs against the community, not just against specific victims, and the community's remedy is liberty, not payment.

Madoff paid $150 years. No firm's balance sheet covers that line.
