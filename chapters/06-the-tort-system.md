# Chapter 6 — The Tort System
*What the coffee cup actually taught us.*

---

Let me tell you what you think you know about the McDonald's coffee case, and then let me tell you what actually happened.

In February 1992, an 81-year-old woman named Stella Liebeck bought a cup of coffee at a McDonald's drive-through in Albuquerque. She placed the cup between her knees to add cream and sugar, and spilled it. She sued. The jury awarded her millions of dollars. You have probably heard this described as the textbook example of lawsuit abuse — a woman spills coffee on herself and wins the lottery because an American jury felt sorry for her. That description is so far from the actual record that it functions less as a summary of the case and more as a test of whether you understand what the tort system is actually doing.

Here is what the description leaves out. McDonald's coffee was, by corporate specification, held at 180 to 190 degrees Fahrenheit — substantially hotter than competitors, and hot enough to cause third-degree burns within seconds of skin contact. In the decade before Liebeck spilled her cup, McDonald's had received approximately 700 complaints about that temperature, including reports of severe burns to children. The company had internally documented the risk. It had done a cost-benefit analysis and concluded that the injury claims were an acceptable cost relative to the benefit of serving coffee customers preferred hotter. So it kept the temperature.

Liebeck sustained third-degree burns over six percent of her body — thighs, buttocks, groin. She was hospitalized for eight days, underwent skin grafts, and spent two years in medical treatment. Her medical expenses approached $20,000. McDonald's offered her $800.

The jury awarded $200,000 in compensatory damages — reduced to $160,000 because the jury found Liebeck 20 percent at fault for placing the cup between her knees — and $2.7 million in punitive damages, which the jury calculated as roughly two days of McDonald's coffee sales. The trial judge found the punitive award excessive and reduced it to $480,000. The parties settled for an undisclosed amount. McDonald's subsequently lowered its coffee temperature.

This is not a story about a sympathetic old woman and a generous jury. It is a story about a company that knew its product caused severe burns, documented that knowledge internally, calculated that the injury costs were acceptable, and did not change anything. The jury saw the documentation. The punitive damages were calibrated to the scale of the company, not just to Stella Liebeck's medical bills. The judge's reduction reflects the legal system's ongoing calibration of punitive awards against constitutional limits. The whole sequence — injury, lawsuit, verdict, reduction, settlement, temperature change — is the apparatus working as designed.

What I want to do in this chapter is explain that apparatus. Not just the legal categories, but the logic underneath them: why each piece is structured the way it is, what work each piece does, and whether the whole thing makes sense as a system for handling harm.

---

Start with the most basic question: what is a tort, and why does this category exist?

A tort is a civil wrong — an act or omission that causes harm to another person and gives rise to civil liability. The word *civil* is doing crucial work. The tort system runs parallel to the criminal system and does something different from it. A criminal prosecution is brought by the government, the penalty is imprisonment or a fine paid to the state, and the standard of proof is beyond reasonable doubt. A tort suit is brought by the injured party, the remedy is almost always money paid by the defendant to the plaintiff, and the standard of proof is preponderance of the evidence — more likely than not.

The same act can be both. O.J. Simpson was acquitted of criminal murder in 1995 and found civilly liable for the wrongful deaths of Nicole Brown Simpson and Ron Goldman in 1997. The acquittal meant the government could not prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt. The civil verdict meant the plaintiffs proved, by a preponderance, that Simpson was responsible for the deaths. Different standard, different proceeding, different outcome.

The tort system exists because the criminal system cannot do, and was never designed to do, what the tort system does. Criminal law addresses wrongs against society. Tort law addresses wrongs against specific individuals and attempts to make those individuals whole. When McDonald's coffee burned Stella Liebeck, the government had no particular interest in prosecuting McDonald's. Liebeck had a direct interest in recovering her medical costs and being compensated for her suffering. The tort system is the mechanism through which she pursued that interest — and, not incidentally, the mechanism through which McDonald's faced consequences for a documented internal decision to accept the cost of burning its customers.

The system organizes wrongs into three primary categories, and the categories matter because the legal machinery differs substantially across them.

*Intentional torts* require a conscious and purposeful act. The defendant need not have intended the specific harm — just the act that caused it. If I deliberately push you and you fall and break your wrist, I committed battery even if I had no intention of breaking your wrist. I intended the push. The major intentional torts — assault, battery, false imprisonment, defamation, invasion of privacy, intentional infliction of emotional distress, trespass — all share this structure: deliberate conduct, harmful result.

*Negligence* is the dominant category in commercial litigation and the one that generates most of the legal exposure most businesses actually face. It does not require intent. It requires that the defendant failed to exercise the standard of care that a reasonable person would have exercised under the circumstances. That is the whole thing, and it contains a precise machinery beneath the surface.

*Strict liability* imposes liability without regard to fault at all. Certain activities and certain products carry liability for the harms they produce even when the defendant exercised every reasonable precaution. The question is not whether the defendant was careful. The question is whether the activity or product caused the harm.

The Liebeck case was a negligence case. Understanding why requires understanding what negligence actually demands.

---

The plaintiff in a negligence case must establish four elements. Every law student learns this list in the first weeks of torts. What the list does not convey on its own is why each element is structured the way it is, and what work each one does.

*Duty.* The defendant must have owed the plaintiff a duty of care. Not everyone owes a duty to everyone else — the law is selective about this. The duty arises from a recognized legal relationship: manufacturer to consumer, driver to other road users, business to its customers, professional to client. The scope of the duty depends on the relationship and on the foreseeability of harm. A manufacturer owes a duty to the foreseeable users of its product, not to every person on earth.

Foreseeability is the boundary of duty, and the 1928 case *Palsgraf v. Long Island Railroad* is the standing illustration of why. A railroad employee helped a passenger board a moving train. The passenger was carrying fireworks wrapped in newspaper. The fireworks fell, exploded, and the blast knocked over a scale at the other end of the platform, which fell on a woman named Helen Palsgraf. She sued the railroad. Judge Cardozo held that the railroad owed no duty to Palsgraf — the harm to her was not a foreseeable consequence of helping a passenger board a train. The case establishes foreseeability as the criterion: you owe a duty to people who might foreseeably be harmed by your conduct, not to anyone who might be harmed through any conceivable chain of events.

There is one important exception. Standard negligence doctrine generally imposes no duty to act affirmatively to help strangers. A bystander who watches someone drown has no legal obligation to attempt a rescue. The exception is *special relationships* — relationships that, by their nature, create affirmative duties to act. Doctors owe affirmative duties to patients. Lifeguards owe affirmative duties to swimmers in their area. Common carriers owe heightened duties to passengers. When you see a case about a business's liability for a customer's injury, the threshold question — is there a duty here? — is almost always answered yes, because the business-customer relationship is a recognized special relationship.

McDonald's, operating a commercial restaurant and selling food and beverages to the public, owed its customers a duty of care not to sell products carrying unreasonable risks of harm. Liebeck was a customer. The duty existed. That part was not contested.

*Breach.* The defendant must have failed to exercise the care a reasonable person would have exercised under the circumstances. The standard is the *reasonable person* — a hypothetical individual exercising ordinary care and judgment, calibrated to the situation. The standard is objective: the defendant's genuine belief that it was being careful does not establish the absence of breach. If the reasonable actor in the same situation would have taken precautions the defendant did not take, the defendant breached the duty.

Notice what this standard is actually measuring. It is not asking whether the defendant was a bad actor, or whether the defendant intended harm. It is asking whether the defendant's conduct fell below the level we expect of ordinarily careful people in the same situation. The standard is calibrated to ordinary competence, not extraordinary care. And it is applied to conduct, not intentions.

McDonald's knew, from 700 prior complaints over a decade, that its coffee temperature caused severe burns. It had internally documented the risk. It had calculated that injury claims were an acceptable cost relative to keeping the temperature high. The reasonable restaurant operator, possessing that knowledge, would have adjusted the temperature or at minimum warned customers explicitly about the burn risk. McDonald's did neither. The jury found breach. On these facts, that finding is difficult to argue with.

*Causation.* The defendant's breach must have caused the plaintiff's harm. The doctrine has two components worth distinguishing. *Cause-in-fact*, sometimes called but-for causation: the harm would not have occurred but for the defendant's breach. *Proximate cause*: the harm was a foreseeable consequence of the breach, not an unlikely chain of events too remote to justify liability.

Proximate cause does the work of limiting liability to harms that were reasonably connected to the breach. A driver who runs a red light is the but-for cause of any accident at that intersection — but whether that driver is the proximate cause of damage to a vehicle six blocks away, through a domino-chain of accidents triggered by the first collision, is a different question. At some point the connection becomes too attenuated. Proximate cause is where courts draw that line.

In Liebeck's case, causation was not seriously contested. The coffee caused the burns. The burns were exactly the kind of harm that the known temperature risk made foreseeable. Cause-in-fact and proximate cause were both established.

*Damages.* The plaintiff must have suffered measurable harm. Negligence does not produce liability for risk that did not materialize into actual injury. Near-misses are not compensable. The requirement is not formalistic; it is the mechanism by which the tort system stays focused on actual harm rather than hypothetical harm.

Third-degree burns over six percent of her body. Eight days of hospitalization. Skin grafts. Two years of treatment. Approximately $20,000 in medical costs. These are actual, documented harms with documented costs. The damages element was established.

All four elements were established. That is why she won. Not because the jury felt sorry for her. Because the framework was satisfied.

---

The negligence framework does not end with the plaintiff's case. The defendant has tools too.

The oldest defense is contributory negligence: the plaintiff's own negligence contributed to her harm, so she should recover nothing. The doctrine is historically severe — any degree of contributory fault, even one percent, barred recovery entirely. Most U.S. states replaced it with *comparative negligence*, under which the plaintiff's recovery is reduced by her share of fault rather than eliminated.

The Liebeck jury found that she was 20 percent at fault for placing the cup between her knees. Her $200,000 compensatory award was reduced by $40,000 to reflect her share. McDonald's paid $160,000 in compensatory damages. This is comparative negligence operating exactly as designed: acknowledging the plaintiff's contribution without making it a complete bar.

Two versions of comparative negligence exist. *Pure comparative* states reduce recovery by the plaintiff's fault percentage regardless of how high it is — a plaintiff who is 90 percent at fault recovers 10 percent of her damages. *Modified comparative* states bar recovery entirely if the plaintiff's fault exceeds a threshold, typically 50 percent. The distinction matters most in cases where both parties contributed substantially to the harm and the fault percentages are genuinely contested.

The second defense is assumption of risk: a plaintiff who voluntarily and knowingly assumed a particular risk cannot recover for harm that risk produced. The defense applies most cleanly in recreational settings — the spectator hit by a foul ball at a baseball game assumed the foreseeable risk of attending — and in cases involving an express written release. A skier who signs a liability waiver before using the lift has expressly assumed certain risks.

The enforceability of liability waivers varies by state and by the nature of the risk. Courts generally enforce waivers for recreational risks but scrutinize waivers that attempt to disclaim liability for gross negligence or willful misconduct. There is a meaningful legal difference between a waiver for inherent risks of a dangerous activity and a waiver that a business uses to insulate itself from the consequences of its own negligent maintenance. Courts recognize the difference, even when the waiver language does not.

---

The third major category — strict liability — works differently from everything above. The question is not whether the defendant was careful. The question is whether the activity or product caused the harm.

Strict liability applies to two major categories. The first is abnormally dangerous activities: activities not of common usage that create a high degree of risk even when conducted with all reasonable care. Dynamite blasting. Keeping wild animals. Certain industrial chemical operations. The liability follows the activity because the risk is inherent and the activity creates it. The manufacturer of explosives who sells them to a legitimate mining operation has exercised all reasonable care — and is still the right party to bear the cost if those explosives cause harm. It created the risk. It is better positioned than any individual plaintiff to account for that risk in product pricing and safety design.

The second and more commercially significant category is product liability: manufacturers and sellers of products are strictly liable for harm caused by defective products. Three defect categories, and distinguishing them is important because the analysis differs.

A *manufacturing defect* exists when a specific unit deviates from the manufacturer's own design specifications. The lug nut that was not properly torqued at the factory. The product was supposed to be safe; this particular unit was not because something went wrong in production.

A *design defect* exists when the product as designed is unreasonably dangerous — every unit built to specification is dangerous because the specification itself is wrong. The analysis here asks whether the product's design was reasonable given the risks it created, usually measured against the utility it provided or against whether a safer alternative design was feasible.

A *warning defect*, sometimes called failure-to-warn, exists when the product lacks adequate warnings about its risks. Even a well-designed product, properly manufactured, can be defective if users are not adequately informed of non-obvious dangers that the manufacturer knew or should have known about.

The policy logic of strict product liability is not immediately obvious, and it is worth understanding rather than just accepting. Why should a manufacturer who exercised all reasonable care still be liable for harm its product causes? The answer has two parts. First, the manufacturer is in the best position — better than any individual consumer — to know the risks its product creates and to invest in reducing them. Placing liability on the manufacturer creates the right incentives to investigate, to test, to redesign. Second, the cost of product-related injuries can be distributed across all consumers through product pricing. The manufacturer functions, in effect, as an insurer of product safety, and the cost of that insurance is embedded in the price. The argument is economic, not moral: route the cost of product injuries to the party best positioned to reduce them and to spread the residual risk.

---

The Ford Pinto cases of the 1970s are the other essential illustration — and they show what happens when the internal documents are even more damaging than the ones in Liebeck.

The Pinto's fuel tank was placed behind the rear axle with insufficient crash protection. Ford's internal analyses, disclosed in subsequent litigation, showed the company had calculated the cost of redesigning the fuel system — approximately $11 per vehicle — against the projected cost of injury and death claims under assumptions about likely accident rates. The cost-benefit analysis favored not redesigning.

*Grimshaw v. Ford Motor Company* (1981) arose from a rear-end collision in which the Pinto's fuel tank ruptured and caught fire. A 13-year-old passenger died; the driver sustained severe burns. The jury awarded $2.5 million in compensatory damages and $125 million in punitive damages. The trial judge reduced the punitive award to $3.5 million. The case is taught in every product-liability course in the country because of what the internal cost-benefit documents did to the jury: when a manufacturer explicitly calculates that the cost of not fixing a defect is lower than the cost of fixing it — and the defect subsequently kills people — the punitive damages instruction becomes nearly unavoidable.

The Pinto case and the Liebeck case share a structural feature that is worth seeing clearly. In both cases, the defendant had documented internal awareness of a known risk. In both cases, the defendant chose not to act on that awareness. In both cases, the jury was permitted to see the documentation. And in both cases, the punitive award was calibrated not just to compensate the plaintiff but to communicate something about the nature of the conduct: this was not an accident in the sense of something unforeseeable. It was a choice. Choices have consequences.

---

So does the apparatus actually work?

The tort system does something important well: it places the cost of harms on parties who were in a position to prevent them. McDonald's was better positioned than Stella Liebeck to know that 185-degree coffee caused third-degree burns within seconds — it had 700 complaints documenting exactly that. Ford was better positioned than the Pinto's passengers to know the fuel-tank placement was dangerous. Strict product liability and the negligence framework both route costs toward the parties who had the information and the capacity to act on it. That is the right direction for the incentive to flow.

The punitive damages mechanism, when it fires on the right facts, does something additional: it makes the penalty commensurate with the defendant's capacity and with the nature of the conduct, not just with the specific plaintiff's harm. Two days of McDonald's coffee sales was not arbitrary. It was an attempt by the jury to impose a consequence that would actually register — to make deterrence real rather than nominal for a company large enough that any individual compensatory award would be a rounding error.

What the system does less well is predictability. Jury fact-finding varies. The line between reasonable breach and culpable disregard is drawn differently by different juries in different jurisdictions. A manufacturer trying to make rational safety investments cannot easily calculate the expected litigation cost of a given design choice, because the punitive-damages exposure varies too widely. This is a real cost, and it is worth acknowledging. A system of ex post accountability — one that evaluates conduct after the harm occurs — will always produce some uncertainty for actors trying to make decisions ex ante.

But notice what the Liebeck and Pinto cases have in common beyond the internal documents. In both cases, the company had the opportunity to make a different choice before anyone was hurt. The ex post evaluation, when it came, found a record of documented awareness and documented decision-making. The companies that manage tort exposure best are the ones that design safety in proactively — so that the ex post evaluation, if it ever comes, finds that record rather than a cost-benefit analysis in which human harm appeared as a line item.

That is not a legal observation. It is a practical one. The tort system is, among other things, a signal. It tells you what the consequences look like when the internal documentation goes against you. The signal is imprecise. But it is not ambiguous.
