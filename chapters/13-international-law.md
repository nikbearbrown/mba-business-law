# Chapter 13 — International Law
*One NASDAQ listing, five continents of bribery, and a $1 billion settlement to a government the company had no operations in.*

---

In 2016, Swedish telecommunications giant Ericsson was in the middle of paying roughly $1 billion to resolve U.S. Department of Justice charges under the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act. The company had, over more than a decade, made payments to government officials in Djibouti, China, Vietnam, Indonesia, and Kuwait — among other places — to secure telecommunications contracts. The payments moved through sham consultants, fictitious invoices, and cash couriers. The conduct happened outside the United States. The company was Swedish. The U.S. Department of Justice was the enforcement authority collecting the settlement. A U.S. federal court in New York was where the deferred prosecution agreement was filed.

How does that work?

Ericsson is incorporated in Stockholm. Its misconduct occurred in Southeast Asia and East Africa. What is the legal theory under which the United States has jurisdiction to prosecute a Swedish company for conduct in Vietnam?

The answer fits in one sentence: Ericsson had American Depositary Receipts — effectively, shares — listed on NASDAQ. That listing made the company subject to U.S. securities law. U.S. securities law incorporates the FCPA's anti-bribery provisions. The FCPA covers any issuer whose securities are registered in the United States, regardless of where the issuer is incorporated or where the corrupt conduct occurs. One U.S. stock listing. A decade of bribery across five continents. A billion-dollar settlement to a government the company had no operations in.

The Ericsson case is not an outlier. It is the clearest possible demonstration of what international law means in practice: the legal framework governing cross-border operations is a combination of treaties, domestic laws with extended reach, the laws of every country where the firm operates, and a set of international conventions and institutions governing the interactions among all of the above. Understanding how that framework is structured, and where its edges are, is what this chapter is about.

---

## The basic problem

Start with the fundamental difficulty. Law, as Chapter 1 described, is produced by sovereigns — by governments with jurisdiction over a defined territory and the people in it. The moment you cross a border, a different sovereign's law applies. The moment a transaction involves parties in two countries, you have at minimum two legal systems in play, and potentially dozens if the transaction touches multiple jurisdictions.

This creates three problems that any cross-border transaction must navigate.

The first is *which law governs*. If a U.S. company signs a supply contract with a German manufacturer for goods delivered to Brazil, and a dispute arises, which country's contract law applies? German contract law differs from U.S. contract law. Brazilian law differs from either. The parties can try to specify choice of law in their contract — most sophisticated commercial contracts do — but a contractual choice-of-law clause can be overridden by the mandatory rules of a country with a strong interest in the dispute.

The second is *where disputes are resolved*. Even knowing which law applies, you need a forum to apply it. Courts in different countries have different procedural rules, different evidentiary standards, different timelines. A U.S. judgment may be difficult or impossible to enforce in a country whose courts don't recognize it. A forum-selection clause in a contract nominates a court or arbitral body, but enforcing that clause across borders requires the nominated forum to accept jurisdiction and the losing party's home country to cooperate in enforcement.

The third is *who is subject to whose authority*. The Ericsson example is the sharp edge of this problem. Jurisdictional reach — the legal theory under which a country claims authority over a person, company, or transaction — varies by country and by statute. The United States has been historically aggressive about asserting extraterritorial jurisdiction. The FCPA is one example; U.S. sanctions law, which can subject non-U.S. companies to penalties for doing business with sanctioned countries, is another. Any U.S. connection — a listing, a U.S. employee, a transaction routed through a U.S. bank — creates exposure to legal claims not limited to where operations physically are.

## Where international law comes from

International law is not produced the way domestic law is. There is no international legislature. There is no international executive with enforcement power. There is no court with compulsory jurisdiction over all disputes. What exists instead is a set of overlapping sources, each with different authority and different enforcement mechanisms.

**Treaties** are agreements between sovereign states. The U.S. Constitution requires Senate ratification, and once ratified, a treaty has the force of federal law — supreme over conflicting state law. Treaties can be bilateral or multilateral, covering trade, investment protection, tax, extradition, mutual legal assistance, and much else. The *bilateral investment treaties* the U.S. has negotiated with approximately fifty countries are particularly important for cross-border business: a BIT typically guarantees that the host country will treat U.S. investors no less favorably than domestic investors, prohibits expropriation without compensation, and provides a dispute-resolution mechanism — usually investor-state arbitration — that allows a company to bring claims directly against a foreign government without going through diplomatic channels.

**Customary international law** is what has become binding through consistent state practice combined with *opinio juris* — the conviction among states that the practice is legally required, not merely habitual. Sovereign immunity — a state cannot be sued in another state's courts without its consent — is customary international law. Diplomatic immunity is customary international law. These norms bind even countries that never explicitly agreed to them, because they have been consistently followed long enough and universally enough to be treated as binding. Customary international law matters most in public-international contexts — relations between governments, laws of war, treatment of foreign nationals. For private commercial transactions, the more directly operative sources are the two conventions below.

**International conventions** are treaty-based regimes governing specific subject matter, developed under the auspices of international organizations and open for adoption by many countries. Two matter directly for cross-border commerce.

The *United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods* — the CISG, adopted in 1980, in force in the United States since 1988 — is the default law governing contracts for the sale of goods between parties in different signatory countries. It covers roughly 95 signatory states, including most major trading partners. Unless the parties explicitly exclude it, the CISG automatically governs their contract. Its rules differ from U.S. common law and from the UCC in ways that matter: the CISG does not require consideration; the mirror-image rule does not apply in the same form; risk-of-loss rules differ. Practitioners who draft contracts assuming UCC rules without checking whether the CISG applies will occasionally be surprised by which law their contract is actually under.

The *New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards*, adopted in 1958, is the legal infrastructure that makes international arbitration work. The New York Convention requires its 170-plus signatory states to recognize and enforce arbitral awards made in other signatory states, subject to a narrow set of exceptions. This is the mechanism that gives international arbitration practical value: a company that wins an arbitration in London against a Chinese counterparty can take that award to Chinese courts and have it enforced there. Without the New York Convention, a cross-border arbitral award would be worth little; you would need to start litigating in the debtor's home country from scratch.

**Host-country law** applies to operations within a country's territory regardless of what other law governs the underlying commercial relationship. A U.S. company's manufacturing plant in Mexico is subject to Mexican labor law, Mexican environmental law, Mexican tax law, and whatever sector-specific Mexican regulation applies — regardless of what law its supply contracts specify. Compliance with host-country law is non-optional, it varies by country, and it changes with elections, regulatory shifts, and court decisions in countries over whose legal systems the operating firm has no influence.

## The global trade framework, and its current dysfunction

The rules governing trade in goods and services between countries — tariff rates, import restrictions, subsidy limits, intellectual-property standards — are primarily set by the World Trade Organization, supplemented by bilateral and regional agreements.

The WTO, established in 1995, has 164 member states accounting for the vast majority of global trade. Its core function is maintaining agreed rules: most-favored-nation treatment (a concession given to one member must be given to all), national treatment (imported goods treated no less favorably than domestic goods after clearing customs), bound tariff schedules, and limits on non-tariff barriers. The WTO also provides a dispute-settlement mechanism for state-to-state trade disputes.

The WTO dispute-settlement system has been substantially non-functional since 2019, when the United States blocked new appointments to the Appellate Body — the second-level review tribunal — leaving it without a quorum. First-level panels continue operating, but losing parties can appeal into a void and delay enforcement indefinitely. The breakdown has accelerated the use of bilateral mechanisms, interim arrangements among subsets of WTO members, and unilateral trade measures by major economies. Supply chain decisions that assumed stable WTO-governed trade rules between specific countries now carry more disruption risk than they did a decade ago.

Regional and bilateral agreements fill in where the WTO leaves off. The United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement, replacing NAFTA in 2020, governs the trade relationship across North America — the largest free-trade zone in the world by GDP. The USMCA is significantly more comprehensive than NAFTA, covering digital trade, intellectual property, labor standards, and environmental standards. Its labor provisions include a rapid-response mechanism allowing the United States to challenge specific Mexican factory conditions directly, and that mechanism has already been used.

## The long arm: FCPA and extraterritorial jurisdiction

The FCPA is the most operationally important example of U.S. law with extended extraterritorial reach, and understanding it in detail is more useful than cataloging the other examples, because the FCPA's structure illustrates the general pattern.

The statute was enacted in 1977 after congressional investigations revealed that hundreds of U.S. companies had made payments to foreign officials. Lockheed had paid $12.5 million to Japanese officials for aircraft contracts. Banana exporters had paid bribes to Central American governments for favorable treatment. Congress criminalized it.

The statute has two operative parts. The *anti-bribery provisions* prohibit any U.S. person, any U.S. company, and any company with U.S.-listed securities from paying, offering, or authorizing a payment of value to a foreign government official for the purpose of obtaining or retaining business. The prohibition extends to payments made through third parties — consultants, agents, distributors — when the company knew or should have known the third party would pass money to an official. The *accounting provisions* require issuers to maintain books and records accurately reflecting transactions and to maintain internal controls sufficient to prevent unauthorized payments. The accounting provisions are a separate basis for liability: a company can be charged for inadequate books and records even if the underlying bribe cannot be proven.

The jurisdictional reach follows the hook. A U.S. issuer is subject to the FCPA for conduct anywhere in the world, because the U.S. securities listing creates jurisdiction. A non-U.S. company with a U.S. listing — Ericsson, Telia, Siemens, Total, and dozens of others — is similarly subject. The largest FCPA settlements have often involved non-U.S. companies precisely because that is where the extraterritorial principle bites hardest.

The practical consequence is that any company with U.S. securities, U.S. employees, U.S. banking relationships, or transactions routed through U.S. financial infrastructure should treat the FCPA as fully applicable regardless of where its operations are. The standard compliance response is a written anti-corruption policy, due diligence on third-party agents before engagement, contractual anti-corruption representations from agents, regular training, a reporting mechanism, and periodic audits. These programs are not administrative overhead; companies that have invested in them are substantially better positioned both to prevent violations and to demonstrate good faith when violations occur.

The Ericsson settlement was $1 billion for conduct that the company could credibly argue should have been handled by Swedish authorities under Swedish law. The compliance cost of a robust FCPA program is a small fraction of that. This is the cost-benefit calculation that should drive how seriously any U.S.-connected company takes the statute.

## How international disputes actually get resolved

When a cross-border commercial dispute arises — a contract breach, a joint-venture disagreement, a product liability claim — the parties face the three-part problem described at the start of this chapter. The most common answer in sophisticated international commercial contracts is international arbitration under the rules of one of the major arbitral institutions: the International Chamber of Commerce in Paris, the London Court of International Arbitration, the Singapore International Arbitration Centre, or the AAA's International Centre for Dispute Resolution.

These institutions administer procedure — appointing arbitrators when parties cannot agree, managing timelines, handling logistics — while the arbitral tribunal decides substance. The advantages over court litigation are significant. The parties choose arbitrators with relevant industry expertise. Proceedings are private. The process is generally faster than national-court litigation. And the award is enforceable under the New York Convention in 170-plus countries.

A court judgment, by contrast, may or may not be enforceable abroad depending on whether the country has a bilateral recognition treaty — and many countries lack those treaties with the United States. This asymmetry makes arbitration the preferred mechanism for cross-border dispute resolution in almost all sophisticated commercial contracts.

The arbitration clause in a cross-border contract typically specifies the arbitral institution and its rules, the seat of arbitration (which determines which courts supervise the arbitration and can set it aside on limited grounds), the number of arbitrators, the language of proceedings, and the governing substantive law. These choices matter more than most people drafting them realize. A poorly constructed arbitration clause — one that specifies an institution and a seat in ways that conflict, or that fails to specify governing law — creates ambiguity that can be litigated for years before any merits dispute is addressed.

The New York Convention permits enforcement courts to refuse recognition of an arbitral award on a narrow set of grounds: the arbitration agreement was invalid, the losing party was not given proper notice, the award exceeds the scope of the agreement, the procedure violated the agreement, the award is contrary to the public policy of the enforcing country, or the subject matter is not capable of arbitration under that country's law. Courts in most countries interpret these exceptions narrowly. The Convention was designed to maximize enforcement, and courts generally honor that design.

## What Ericsson actually teaches

Return to the opening case with the chapter's framework in hand.

The Ericsson situation is not a story about a bad company or an aggressive prosecutor. It is a demonstration of how the international legal framework actually operates. Ericsson made a decision to list its shares in the United States. That decision brought it inside U.S. jurisdictional reach for the FCPA — not because the U.S. had any particular stake in Ericsson's business in Djibouti or Vietnam, but because the listing created a legal hook sufficient to assert jurisdiction. The conduct, wherever it physically occurred, fell within the statute's reach.

The framework this chapter has described structures every stage of cross-border operations, not just enforcement actions. Before entering a new country, a firm needs to understand host-country law and any treaty protections available. During operations, FCPA compliance and accurate books and records are non-optional for any U.S.-connected firm. When structuring contracts, choice-of-law and dispute-resolution clauses are not boilerplate — they determine the practical options if the relationship breaks down. And as the WTO's current dysfunction illustrates, the tariff and regulatory environment facing a specific cross-border transaction can change faster than supply chains can be rebuilt; building flexibility into sourcing decisions has become more important than it was even ten years ago.

The international legal framework is not a subject that applies only to law departments or only when litigation looms. It is the operating context for any business with connections across borders. The Ericsson case is the extreme version of a principle that operates at every scale: the legal obligations that attach to a cross-border business are not limited to the countries where the business thinks it operates. They follow the hooks — the listing, the employee, the bank transfer, the contract clause — wherever those hooks lead.

One NASDAQ listing is enough.
