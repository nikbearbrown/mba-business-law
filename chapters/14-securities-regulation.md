# Chapter 14 — Securities Regulation
*The rules that govern what you can say when you're asking people for their money.*

---

Here is the fundamental problem that every securities market has to solve.

You want to raise capital. You have a business — or an idea for a business — and you need money you don't have. Someone else has money they'd like to put to work. In principle, the transaction is straightforward: they give you money, you give them a claim on the future returns of the enterprise, and everyone is better off if the enterprise succeeds.

The problem is information. You know everything about your business — its prospects, its risks, the quality of its management, the assumptions behind its financial projections. The investor knows almost none of this. And you have every incentive to present the best possible version of the story, to emphasize the upside, to minimize or conceal the risks. The investor, who cannot independently verify most of what you tell them, has to decide whether to trust you.

Markets that can't solve this problem don't work. If investors can't distinguish honest representations from fraudulent ones, they either demand such large discounts that honest issuers can't raise capital at fair prices, or they stop investing altogether. Either outcome destroys the mechanism by which capital flows from people who have it to enterprises that can put it to productive use.

Securities regulation is the apparatus that solves the information problem. Not by making every investment safe — it doesn't — but by requiring disclosure sufficient that an investor can make an informed decision, and by imposing liability on those who lie.

---

## Why the apparatus exists

In the 1920s, the U.S. securities markets operated with essentially no federal regulatory framework. States had their own securities laws — the so-called *blue sky laws*, named for a judge's observation that certain promoters would sell shares in the blue sky itself — but the laws were inconsistent, weakly enforced, and easily evaded by issuers who simply crossed state lines.

The result was a market in which manipulation, fraud, and disclosure failures were widespread. Promoters issued securities in companies with no meaningful assets or earnings, described in prospectuses that bore little relationship to reality. Investment banks distributed securities they knew to be overvalued. Trading syndicates manipulated prices to attract public buyers and then sold into the artificial run-up. The investing public, with no reliable way to distinguish sound investments from fraudulent ones, poured money into the market throughout the 1920s on the reasonable but incorrect assumption that rising prices reflected genuine value creation.

In October 1929, the market crashed. Over the following three years, prices declined approximately 89 percent from peak to trough. The United States entered the Great Depression. Among the structural causes the crash exposed were the information failures the unregulated market had produced — not as isolated incidents but as endemic features of the market's operation.

Congress responded with two foundational statutes. The *Securities Act of 1933* governs the issuance of new securities — what happens when a company first sells securities to the public. The *Securities Exchange Act of 1934* governs trading in already-issued securities — what happens in the secondary markets where investors buy from and sell to each other — and creates the *Securities and Exchange Commission* to administer both statutes. The two statutes, together with subsequent amendments and the body of SEC rulemaking built on top of them, constitute the operating framework of U.S. securities regulation.

The design philosophy of the framework is worth stating explicitly, because it is unusual. The statutes do not prohibit companies from issuing risky securities. They do not require the SEC to evaluate the merits of an investment or certify that a security is a good deal. What they require is *disclosure*: comprehensive, accurate, and honest information about the issuer, the securities, and the risks. A company can offer terrible securities to the public, as long as the offering documents accurately describe how terrible they are. The investor who buys a terrible security after reading accurate disclosure has received exactly what the law promises. The investor who buys a terrible security after reading false disclosure has been defrauded, and the law provides recourse.

## The 1933 Act: what you have to say before you can ask

The 1933 Act's core principle is registration. Securities offered or sold to the public must be registered with the SEC unless an exemption applies. Registration requires the issuer to file a *registration statement* — which includes the *prospectus* that will be delivered to investors — containing comprehensive disclosure about the issuer, the securities, and the planned use of the proceeds raised.

The registration process is not fast. The SEC reviews the registration statement, issues comments, and requires amendments until the disclosure meets the staff's standards. A typical initial public offering takes several months from filing to effectiveness, during which the issuer cannot sell the securities. The *Securities Act of 1933* creates three temporal zones around the offering: the *pre-filing period* (no offers or sales permitted), the *waiting period* between filing and effectiveness (oral offers and certain written communications permitted, but no sales), and the *post-effective period* (sales permitted, prospectus delivery required).

The liability framework that accompanies registration is the statute's enforcement mechanism. Section 11 imposes civil liability on the issuer — and on underwriters, directors, and certain experts who sign the registration statement — for material misstatements or omissions in the registration statement. The issuer's liability is strict: no scienter required, no due-diligence defense available. Underwriters and others can avoid liability by establishing that they conducted reasonable due diligence and had no reason to believe the registration statement contained material misstatements. Section 12 provides parallel liability for fraudulent prospectuses.

The reason the liability is calibrated this way is the information asymmetry the statute is designed to address. The issuer knows whether the registration statement is accurate; there is no excuse for a material misstatement in the document the issuer itself prepared. The underwriter doesn't know the issuer's business as well as the issuer does, but the underwriter's commercial role — vouching for the offering to its distribution network — depends on the market's trust in its due diligence. The graduated liability structure reflects those different positions.

### The exemption system

Most securities offerings in the United States are not registered. The registration process is expensive, slow, and subjects the issuer to ongoing disclosure obligations that small and early-stage companies may not be able to sustain. Congress and the SEC have built a system of exemptions that allow issuers to raise capital without registration, under conditions designed to ensure that the investors in unregistered offerings are either sophisticated enough to protect themselves or are limited in how much they can invest.

The major exemptions operate as a toolkit, with different instruments suited to different situations.

*Regulation D Rule 506(b)* is the workhorse of private capital markets. It permits issuers to raise unlimited capital from an unlimited number of *accredited investors* — individuals with net worth above $1 million (excluding their primary residence) or income above $200,000 in each of the prior two years, and institutional investors above specified size thresholds. Rule 506(b) also permits up to 35 non-accredited but sophisticated investors. No general solicitation is permitted: the offering must be made directly to investors with whom the issuer has a pre-existing relationship. Rule 506(b) offerings constitute the primary mechanism for venture capital financing, private equity, and hedge fund capital raises.

*Rule 506(c)* permits general solicitation — advertising the offering publicly — but requires that all actual investors be verified accredited investors. The general solicitation permission, added by the JOBS Act of 2012, has enabled a broader market for private placements but requires more rigorous investor verification than 506(b).

*Regulation A* permits public offerings up to $75 million annually, with a simplified registration process (Regulation A filings are reviewed by the SEC but are less comprehensive than full registration statements). Regulation A issuers can solicit investors broadly, including non-accredited investors, and are subject to ongoing reporting requirements lighter than those for registered companies.

*Regulation Crowdfunding* permits online offerings up to $5 million per year through registered crowdfunding platforms. Investment limits apply based on the investor's income and net worth, ensuring that investors of modest means cannot concentrate excessive risk in any single crowdfunding investment. Regulation Crowdfunding was created by the JOBS Act of 2012 and became operational in 2016.

*Section 4(a)(2)* is the statutory exemption for private placements — transactions not involving any public offering. The exemption predates Regulation D and remains available, though Regulation D's safe-harbor provisions make Rule 506 the preferred vehicle for most private placements because it provides clearer legal certainty.

## The 1934 Act: what you have to say while you're a public company

The 1933 Act governs the moment of issuance. Once a company is public — once its securities are trading in the secondary markets — the 1934 Act governs its ongoing relationship with its shareholders and with the SEC.

The centerpiece of the 1934 Act's ongoing obligations is the *periodic disclosure* system. Public companies must file annual reports on Form 10-K, quarterly reports on Form 10-Q, and current reports on Form 8-K for material events that occur between quarterly filings. The 10-K is the most comprehensive: it includes audited financial statements, management's discussion and analysis of financial condition and results, disclosures about the company's business, risk factors, and governance. The 10-Q provides unaudited quarterly updates. The 8-K requires prompt disclosure of specified major events — earnings releases, acquisitions, departures of key executives, material agreements.

The disclosure system is the operating context for U.S. public-company governance. Shareholders, analysts, journalists, and regulators read 10-Ks and 8-Ks; the documents are the primary mechanism by which public companies communicate with the market. A public company that fails to disclose material information, or that discloses it inaccurately, faces liability under Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5, the statute's primary anti-fraud provisions.

Section 14 of the 1934 Act governs proxy solicitations — the process by which public companies solicit shareholder votes on matters requiring shareholder approval (director elections, executive compensation, mergers). The proxy rules require substantial disclosure about the matters being voted on and about the persons soliciting proxies, ensuring that shareholders can make informed voting decisions.

Sections 13(d) and 14(d) regulate significant shareholders and tender-offer bidders. Any person or group that acquires more than 5 percent of a public company's outstanding shares must file a Schedule 13D or 13G disclosing its holdings and intentions within specified timeframes. A person launching a tender offer must comply with detailed disclosure and procedural requirements under Section 14(d). These provisions ensure that accumulations of significant ownership stakes — which can be the precursor to activist campaigns or hostile takeovers — are visible to the market and to other shareholders.

## Insider trading: when the information asymmetry is itself the fraud

Securities markets run on information. Prices incorporate information; when new information becomes public, prices adjust to reflect it. The market works — prices are informative signals about value — because information is, in principle, available to all participants.

Insider trading is the exploitation of information that isn't available to all participants. A corporate officer who knows her company is about to announce a transformative acquisition buys stock before the announcement; when the price jumps on the news, she sells at a substantial profit. She has taken money from the investors who sold to her at the pre-announcement price — investors who, had they known what she knew, would either not have sold or would have demanded a higher price.

The legal prohibition on insider trading operates under Section 10(b) of the 1934 Act and SEC Rule 10b-5, which prohibits fraud in connection with the purchase or sale of securities. The doctrine has developed through two distinct legal theories.

The *classical theory* holds that corporate insiders — officers, directors, employees — who trade on material non-public information about their own corporation breach a fiduciary duty to the corporation's shareholders. The insider who buys stock on inside information is taking an advantage that belongs to the corporation's shareholders, not to the insider personally. The theory is grounded in the fiduciary relationship: the insider's position of trust and confidence over corporate information creates an obligation not to use that information for personal gain at the expense of the people the fiduciary is supposed to serve.

The *misappropriation theory* extends insider-trading liability beyond corporate insiders. In *United States v. O'Hagan* (1997), the Supreme Court held that a person who obtains material non-public information through a breach of duty to the *source* of the information — not necessarily to the corporation whose securities are traded — violates Rule 10b-5. O'Hagan was a lawyer at a firm representing a company planning a tender offer; he traded in the target's securities using information obtained through his representation of the acquirer. He had no fiduciary duty to the target's shareholders. But he had misappropriated information belonging to his client, and that misappropriation was sufficient to establish Rule 10b-5 liability.

The misappropriation theory substantially expanded the scope of insider-trading liability. Employees of investment banks, law firms, accounting firms, and government agencies who obtain material non-public information through their professional roles — and then trade on or tip that information — are potentially liable under the misappropriation theory even when they have no direct relationship to the company whose securities they trade.

*Tippee liability* — the liability of persons who receive inside information from someone who breached a fiduciary duty — operates under standards articulated in *Dirks v. SEC* (1983) and refined in *Salman v. United States* (2016). A tippee is liable when the tipper breached a fiduciary duty by disclosing the information, the tipper received a personal benefit from the disclosure, and the tippee knew or should have known of the breach. *Salman* clarified that a personal benefit can be established when the tipper gives the information to a trading relative or friend — the benefit of the gift to the recipient is itself sufficient.

The Rajat Gupta case is the canonical recent illustration of how these theories operate in practice. Gupta was a board member of Goldman Sachs and Procter & Gamble who, in 2008 and 2009, tipped Raj Rajaratnam — the hedge fund manager who ran Galleon Group — with material non-public information about Goldman board discussions, including information about Warren Buffett's impending $5 billion investment in Goldman during the financial crisis. Rajaratnam traded on the tips; Galleon made millions. Gupta was convicted in 2012 under the classical theory — he was a corporate insider who breached his fiduciary duty to Goldman's shareholders by tipping information he had no right to disclose — and sentenced to two years in federal prison. Rajaratnam was convicted separately and sentenced to eleven years.

The Gupta case illustrates the chain of liability the insider-trading framework creates. The tipper (Gupta) is liable for breach of fiduciary duty even if he did not trade personally. The tippee (Rajaratnam) is liable because he knew the information came from a breach. The downstream trades by Galleon's fund were the profits that made the prosecution worth bringing, but the liability runs from the breach of trust at the source.

## The framework's evolution and frontier

The 1933 and 1934 Acts were designed for a securities market that operated through stock exchanges and broker-dealers. The markets they were designed to regulate were organized, visible, and dominated by institutional intermediaries with identifiable roles. The framework has been substantially modified in the decades since — primarily in response to failures that revealed gaps in the original design.

The *Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002* was the response to the Enron and WorldCom accounting frauds, which had produced two of the largest corporate bankruptcies in U.S. history and revealed systematic failures in the audit process, the independence of corporate boards, and the accuracy of financial disclosures. Sarbanes-Oxley required CEOs and CFOs to personally certify the accuracy of financial statements, imposed new independence requirements on audit committees and external auditors, created criminal liability for document destruction in connection with federal investigations, and established the *Public Company Accounting Oversight Board* to regulate the auditing profession.

The *Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010* responded to the 2008 financial crisis. Among its securities-regulation provisions: a whistleblower program that pays bounties to individuals who provide original information to the SEC leading to enforcement actions with sanctions above $1 million; executive-compensation reforms including the *say-on-pay* advisory vote; and the *Volcker Rule* restricting proprietary trading by federally insured banks.

The *JOBS Act of 2012* moved in the opposite direction — liberalizing the offering rules for smaller issuers, creating the Regulation Crowdfunding exemption, and easing the transition from private to public company status for *emerging growth companies*.

The current frontier includes SEC rulemaking on climate-related disclosure (requiring public companies to disclose material climate risks and greenhouse gas emissions), cybersecurity-incident disclosure (requiring prompt 8-K reporting of material cybersecurity incidents), and the regulatory framework for crypto-asset securities — a category the existing statutory framework was not designed to address and that has generated substantial litigation over which assets constitute *securities* subject to the 1933 and 1934 Act frameworks.

## The apparatus in full

This is the book's final chapter. The apparatus it has built — from contract formation through tort liability, criminal law, employment law, sales contracts, dispute resolution, antitrust, and now securities regulation — is not a collection of isolated rules. It is a system. The rules interact: a public company that commits securities fraud can face SEC enforcement, criminal prosecution, private securities litigation, and derivative suits by shareholders simultaneously. The constitutional protections that apply in the criminal case interact with the disclosure obligations in the civil case. The antitrust framework and the securities framework can apply to the same transaction.

The practitioner who understands the apparatus as a system — who can see which legal frameworks a given transaction or problem implicates, and what each framework requires — can do something that the practitioner who knows isolated rules cannot: navigate a complex commercial situation without missing the legal dimension that matters most.

Securities regulation, specifically, is where the information-asymmetry problem that runs through the entire apparatus is most explicit. Contracts address information asymmetry through disclosure obligations and warranties. Tort law addresses it through fraud and misrepresentation doctrine. Securities law addresses it through mandatory registration, comprehensive ongoing disclosure, and a liability framework designed to make misrepresentation expensive enough that honest disclosure is the rational choice.

The underlying insight — that markets require accurate information to function, and that the law must create the conditions under which accurate information is produced and disclosed — is not specific to securities. It is the organizing principle of much of what this book has covered. The apparatus this book has built is, at its core, an apparatus for making the information environment of commerce reliable enough that the transactions commerce requires can actually happen.

You have the apparatus. The rest of your working life is where it operates.

### Application

**Exercise 14.3.** *Tests learning objective: Rule 10b-5 / misappropriation theory.* A paralegal at a law firm is working on documents for an undisclosed merger. She tells her brother, who buys 500 shares of the target company before the announcement. The brother makes $18,000 on the trade. Apply the misappropriation theory to the paralegal and tippee liability to the brother. Identify what each party's liability turns on and what the prosecution would need to prove. Difficulty: moderate.

**Exercise 14.4.** *Tests learning objective: Section 11 liability.* A company's IPO registration statement states that its flagship product has received FDA clearance. The statement is false — clearance is pending, not received. Six months after the IPO, the misstatement is discovered and the stock price falls 40 percent. Identify who faces Section 11 liability, what the standard of proof is for each defendant, and what defense, if any, is available to the underwriters. Difficulty: moderate.

### Synthesis

**Exercise 14.5.** *Tests learning objective: framework as system.* A public company's CFO sells $2 million of company stock three days before the company announces a major product recall. The SEC opens an investigation. Identify every legal framework from this chapter — and from prior chapters of this book — that could apply to the CFO's conduct. For each framework, state the theory of liability and the remedy available. Difficulty: harder.
