# Securities Regulation

In October 1929, the U.S. stock market crashed. Over the following three years, market values declined approximately 89 percent from peak to trough; the U.S. economy entered the Great Depression. Among the structural failures the crash exposed were widespread fraud, manipulation, and disclosure failures in the securities markets. Congress responded with two foundational statutes: the *Securities Act of 1933* (governing the issuance of new securities) and the *Securities Exchange Act of 1934* (governing trading in already-issued securities and creating the *Securities and Exchange Commission*).

The two 1933-34 statutes, together with subsequent amendments and the body of SEC rulemaking, constitute the operating framework of U.S. securities regulation.

This final chapter of the book is the operating manual for that framework as it applies to contemporary U.S. business.

## Learning objectives

- **Distinguish** the Securities Act of 1933 from the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and **identify** the core requirements of each.
- **Apply** the registration framework under the 1933 Act and **identify** the major exemptions.
- **Evaluate** ongoing public-company disclosure obligations under the 1934 Act.
- **Articulate** the framework for insider trading liability under SEC Rule 10b-5.

## Concept 1: The Securities Act of 1933

The 1933 Act regulates the *issuance* of new securities — the primary market. The Act's core principle: securities offered or sold to the public must be *registered* with the SEC unless an exemption applies. Registration requires the issuer to file a *registration statement* (which includes a prospectus) containing comprehensive disclosure about the issuer, the securities, and the planned use of proceeds.

The Act's anti-fraud provisions (Sections 11 and 12) impose civil liability on issuers and others involved in the offering for material misstatements or omissions in the registration statement. The standard of liability is strict for issuers (no scienter required) and graduated for other defendants (due-diligence defense available for some).

**Major exemptions.** Most securities offerings are conducted under exemptions from registration. Major exemptions include: *Regulation D Rule 506(b)* (private offerings to accredited investors, no general solicitation), *Rule 506(c)* (similar but with general solicitation permitted), *Regulation A* (small public offerings up to $75 million), *Regulation Crowdfunding* (online offerings up to $5 million per year), and *Section 4(a)(2)* (private placements not involving any public offering).

## Concept 2: The Securities Exchange Act of 1934

The 1934 Act regulates trading in already-issued securities — the secondary market. Major components:

**Periodic disclosure.** Public companies must file annual reports (Form 10-K), quarterly reports (Form 10-Q), and current reports for major events (Form 8-K). The disclosure framework is the operating context for U.S. public-company governance.

**Proxy regulation.** Section 14 governs the solicitation of shareholder proxies, requiring substantial disclosure about matters submitted for shareholder vote.

**Tender-offer regulation.** Section 13(d) and Section 14(d) regulate disclosures by significant shareholders and bidders in tender offers.

**Anti-fraud provisions.** Section 10(b) and SEC Rule 10b-5 prohibit fraud in connection with the purchase or sale of securities. Rule 10b-5 is the basis for most contemporary securities-fraud enforcement, both civil (private litigation, SEC enforcement) and criminal (DOJ prosecution).

## Concept 3: Insider trading liability

Insider trading — the use of material non-public information for trading or for tipping others — is prohibited under multiple legal theories.

**Classical theory.** Corporate insiders (officers, directors, employees) who trade on material non-public information about their own corporation breach a fiduciary duty to the corporation's shareholders.

**Misappropriation theory.** Persons who obtain material non-public information through breach of a duty to the source of the information violate Rule 10b-5 even when they have no fiduciary duty to the corporation whose securities they trade. The theory was endorsed by the Supreme Court in *United States v. O'Hagan* (1997).

**Tippee liability.** Persons who receive material non-public information from a tipper who breached a fiduciary duty are liable if they knew or should have known of the breach. The standard is articulated in *Dirks v. SEC* (1983) and refined in *Salman v. United States* (2016).

The Rajat Gupta case (Goldman Sachs board, 2009 leak to Raj Rajaratnam, 2012 conviction, 2-year sentence) is the canonical recent insider-trading case. Bernie Madoff's Ponzi scheme — though not technically insider trading — illustrates the broader scope of Rule 10b-5 fraud liability.

## Concept 4: Ongoing developments

Securities regulation has been substantially reshaped over the past two decades. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 imposed comprehensive new corporate-governance requirements after the Enron and WorldCom failures. The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010 extended the framework with whistleblower programs, executive-compensation reforms, and the *Volcker Rule* restrictions on bank proprietary trading. The JOBS Act of 2012 substantially liberalized securities-offering rules for emerging growth companies. The 2020-2024 SEC rulemaking on climate-disclosure, cybersecurity-disclosure, and crypto-asset regulation continues to develop the framework.

## Summary

Securities regulation is the federal framework governing the issuance and trading of securities. The 1933 Act covers issuance; the 1934 Act covers trading and ongoing disclosure. Rule 10b-5 is the central anti-fraud provision. The framework has been substantially modified by SOX (2002), Dodd-Frank (2010), and subsequent legislation; it continues to evolve in response to technology and market developments.
