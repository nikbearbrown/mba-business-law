# Chapter 14 — Securities Regulation
*The rules that govern what you can say when you're asking people for their money.*

---

Here is the fundamental problem that every securities market has to solve.

You want to raise capital. You have a business — or an idea for one — and you need money you don't have. Someone else has money they would like to put to work. In principle the transaction is simple: they give you money, you give them a claim on the future returns of the enterprise, and everyone is better off if the enterprise succeeds.

The problem is information. You know everything about your business — its prospects, its risks, the quality of its management, the assumptions behind its projections. The investor knows almost none of this. And you have every incentive to present the best possible version of the story: to emphasize the upside, minimize the risks, conceal what would frighten a careful reader. The investor, who cannot independently verify most of what you tell them, has to decide whether to trust you.

Markets that can't solve this problem don't work. If investors can't distinguish honest representations from fraudulent ones, they either demand discounts so large that honest issuers can't raise capital at fair prices, or they stop investing altogether. Either way, the mechanism by which capital flows from people who have it to enterprises that can use it productively breaks down.

Securities regulation is the apparatus that solves the information problem. Not by making every investment safe — it doesn't — but by requiring disclosure sufficient that an investor can make an informed decision, and by imposing liability on those who lie.

---

In the 1920s, the U.S. securities markets operated with essentially no federal regulatory framework. States had their own *blue sky laws* — named for a judge's observation that certain promoters would sell shares in the blue sky itself — but they were inconsistent, weakly enforced, and easily evaded by crossing state lines.

The result was a market in which manipulation, fraud, and disclosure failures were endemic. Promoters issued securities in companies with no meaningful assets, described in prospectuses that bore little relationship to reality. Investment banks distributed securities they knew to be overvalued. Trading syndicates manipulated prices to attract public buyers, then sold into the artificial run-up. The investing public poured money into the market throughout the 1920s on the reasonable but incorrect assumption that rising prices reflected genuine value creation.

In October 1929, the market crashed. Over the following three years, prices declined approximately 89 percent from peak to trough. Among the structural causes the crash exposed were the information failures the unregulated market had produced — not as isolated incidents, but as endemic features of the market's operation.

Congress responded with two foundational statutes. The *Securities Act of 1933* governs the issuance of new securities — what happens when a company first sells securities to the public. The *Securities Exchange Act of 1934* governs trading in already-issued securities — what happens in the secondary markets where investors buy from and sell to each other — and creates the Securities and Exchange Commission to administer both.

The design philosophy of the framework is worth stating explicitly, because it is unusual. The statutes do not prohibit companies from issuing risky securities. They do not require the SEC to evaluate the merits of an investment or certify that a security is a good deal. What they require is *disclosure*: comprehensive, accurate, and honest information about the issuer, the securities, and the risks. A company can offer terrible securities to the public, as long as the offering documents accurately describe how terrible they are. The investor who buys a terrible security after reading accurate disclosure has received exactly what the law promises. The investor who buys a terrible security after reading false disclosure has been defrauded, and the law provides recourse.

---

The 1933 Act's core principle is registration. Securities offered or sold to the public must be registered with the SEC unless an exemption applies. Registration requires the issuer to file a *registration statement* — which includes the *prospectus* that will be delivered to investors — containing comprehensive disclosure about the issuer, the securities, the business, and the planned use of proceeds.

The registration process is not fast. The SEC reviews the statement, issues comments, and requires amendments until the disclosure meets staff standards. A typical initial public offering takes several months from filing to effectiveness. The Act creates three distinct temporal zones around the offering: the *pre-filing period*, in which no offers or sales are permitted; the *waiting period* between filing and effectiveness, in which oral offers and certain written communications are permitted but no sales may occur; and the *post-effective period*, in which sales are permitted and prospectus delivery is required.

The liability framework accompanying registration is the statute's enforcement mechanism. Section 11 imposes civil liability on the issuer — and on underwriters, directors, and certain experts who sign the registration statement — for material misstatements or omissions. The issuer's liability is strict: no intent to deceive required, no due-diligence defense available. Underwriters and others can avoid liability by establishing that they conducted reasonable due diligence and had no reason to believe the registration statement contained material misstatements. The graduated structure reflects different positions in the information hierarchy: the issuer prepared the document and knows whether it is accurate; the underwriter's commercial role — vouching for the offering to its distribution network — depends on the market's trust in its diligence.

Most securities offerings in the United States, however, are not registered. The registration process is expensive and slow, and it subjects the issuer to ongoing disclosure obligations that small and early-stage companies may not be able to sustain. The exemption system allows issuers to raise capital without registration under conditions designed to ensure that investors in unregistered offerings are either sophisticated enough to protect themselves or limited in how much they can invest.

*Regulation D Rule 506(b)* is the workhorse of private capital markets. It permits issuers to raise unlimited capital from an unlimited number of *accredited investors* — individuals with net worth above $1 million excluding their primary residence, or income above $200,000 in each of the prior two years, and qualifying institutions. Rule 506(b) also permits up to 35 non-accredited but sophisticated investors. No general solicitation is permitted: the offering must be made directly to investors with whom the issuer has a pre-existing relationship. Rule 506(b) offerings are the primary mechanism for venture capital financing, private equity, and hedge fund capital raises.

*Rule 506(c)* permits general solicitation — advertising the offering publicly — but requires that all actual investors be verified accredited investors. *Regulation A* permits public offerings up to $75 million annually through a simplified filing process, and allows non-accredited investors to participate. *Regulation Crowdfunding* permits online offerings up to $5 million per year through registered platforms, with investment limits based on the investor's income and net worth. *Section 4(a)(2)* is the original statutory exemption for private placements not involving any public offering — the statutory basis that predates Regulation D, though Rule 506 has become the preferred vehicle because it provides clearer legal certainty.

---

The 1933 Act governs the moment of issuance. Once a company is public — once its securities are trading in the secondary markets — the 1934 Act governs its ongoing relationship with its shareholders and with the SEC.

The centerpiece of that ongoing obligation is the *periodic disclosure* system. Public companies must file annual reports on Form 10-K, quarterly reports on Form 10-Q, and current reports on Form 8-K for material events occurring between quarterly filings. The 10-K is the most comprehensive: audited financial statements, management's discussion and analysis of financial condition and results, risk factors, business description, and governance disclosures. The 10-Q provides unaudited quarterly updates. The 8-K requires prompt disclosure of specified major events — earnings releases, acquisitions, departures of key executives, material agreements.

<!-- → [TABLE: Periodic disclosure forms. Columns: Form | Frequency | Key contents | Filing deadline. Rows: 10-K (annual) | 10-Q (quarterly) | 8-K (current event-driven). Source: Chapter 14] -->

The disclosure system is the operating context for public-company governance. Shareholders, analysts, journalists, and regulators read 10-Ks and 8-Ks; the documents are the primary mechanism by which public companies communicate with the market. A public company that fails to disclose material information, or discloses it inaccurately, faces liability under Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5 — the statute's primary anti-fraud provisions.

The 1934 Act also governs proxy solicitations — the process by which public companies solicit shareholder votes on director elections, executive compensation, and mergers. Section 14's proxy rules require substantial disclosure about the matters being voted on and the persons soliciting proxies. Sections 13(d) and 14(d) regulate significant shareholders and tender-offer bidders: any person or group acquiring more than 5 percent of a public company's outstanding shares must file a Schedule 13D or 13G disclosing its holdings and intentions, and a person launching a tender offer must comply with detailed disclosure requirements. These provisions ensure that accumulations of significant ownership stakes — which can precede activist campaigns or hostile takeovers — are visible to the market.

---

Securities markets run on information. Prices incorporate information; when new information becomes public, prices adjust to reflect it. The market works because information is, in principle, available to all participants simultaneously.

Insider trading is the exploitation of information that isn't available to all participants. A corporate officer who knows her company is about to announce a transformative acquisition buys stock before the announcement; when the price jumps on the news, she sells at a substantial profit. She has taken money from the investors who sold to her at the pre-announcement price — investors who, had they known what she knew, would either not have sold or would have demanded a higher price.

The legal prohibition on insider trading operates under Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5 through two distinct theories.

The *classical theory* holds that corporate insiders — officers, directors, employees — who trade on material non-public information about their own corporation breach a fiduciary duty to the corporation's shareholders. The insider who buys stock on inside information is taking an advantage that belongs to the shareholders, not to the insider personally. The position of trust creates an obligation not to use information for personal gain at shareholders' expense.

The *misappropriation theory* extends liability beyond corporate insiders. In *United States v. O'Hagan* (1997), the Supreme Court held that a person who obtains material non-public information through a breach of duty to the *source* of the information — not necessarily to the corporation whose securities are traded — violates Rule 10b-5. O'Hagan was a lawyer at a firm representing a company planning a tender offer; he traded in the target's securities using information obtained through his representation of the acquirer. He had no fiduciary duty to the target's shareholders. But he had misappropriated information belonging to his client, and that misappropriation was sufficient. The theory substantially expanded insider-trading liability: employees of investment banks, law firms, accounting firms, and government agencies who obtain material non-public information and trade on it are potentially liable even when they have no direct relationship to the company whose securities they trade.

*Tippee liability* — the liability of persons who receive inside information from someone who breached a fiduciary duty — operates under standards from *Dirks v. SEC* (1983) and *Salman v. United States* (2016). A tippee is liable when the tipper breached a fiduciary duty by disclosing the information, the tipper received a personal benefit from the disclosure, and the tippee knew or should have known of the breach. *Salman* clarified that giving inside information to a trading relative or close friend itself constitutes a personal benefit — the gift to the recipient is the benefit.

<!-- → [FIGURE: Insider trading liability chain — tipper (breach of fiduciary duty + personal benefit) → tippee (knowledge of breach) → downstream traders. Show the Gupta-Rajaratnam chain as the concrete illustration. Source: Chapter 14 Fig 14.3] -->

The Rajat Gupta case shows how the chain operates. Gupta was a board member of Goldman Sachs who, in 2008 and 2009, tipped hedge fund manager Raj Rajaratnam with material non-public information about Goldman board discussions — including information about Warren Buffett's impending $5 billion investment in Goldman during the financial crisis. Rajaratnam traded on the tips; Galleon Group made millions. Gupta was convicted in 2012 under the classical theory: he was a corporate insider who breached his fiduciary duty to Goldman's shareholders by disclosing information he had no right to disclose. He was sentenced to two years in federal prison. Rajaratnam was convicted separately and sentenced to eleven years. The tipper (Gupta) was liable even though he did not trade personally. The tippee (Rajaratnam) was liable because he knew the information came from a breach. The chain of liability runs from the breach of trust at the source.

---

The 1933 and 1934 Acts have been substantially modified in the decades since — primarily in response to failures that revealed gaps in the original design.

The *Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002* was the response to the Enron and WorldCom accounting frauds, which produced two of the largest corporate bankruptcies in U.S. history and exposed systematic failures in the audit process, board independence, and the accuracy of financial disclosures. Sarbanes-Oxley required CEOs and CFOs to personally certify the accuracy of financial statements, imposed new independence requirements on audit committees and external auditors, created criminal liability for document destruction in connection with federal investigations, and established the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board to regulate the auditing profession. The legislation moved sharply in the direction of tighter requirements and personal accountability.

The *Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010* responded to the 2008 financial crisis. Among its securities-regulation provisions: a whistleblower program paying bounties to individuals who provide original information to the SEC leading to enforcement actions with sanctions above $1 million; executive-compensation reforms including the advisory say-on-pay vote; and the Volcker Rule restricting proprietary trading by federally insured banks.

The *JOBS Act of 2012* moved in the opposite direction — liberalizing the offering rules for smaller issuers, creating the Regulation Crowdfunding exemption, and easing the transition from private to public company status for *emerging growth companies*. It represents the periodic correction in the other direction: after decades of tightening requirements following market failures, Congress lightened the regulatory burden on smaller issuers in the name of capital formation.

The current frontier includes SEC rulemaking on climate-related disclosure — requiring public companies to disclose material climate risks and greenhouse gas emissions — and cybersecurity-incident disclosure requiring prompt 8-K reporting of material cybersecurity incidents. Both rules have been contested on the question of whether they fall within the SEC's statutory mandate of investor protection through financial disclosure, or whether they represent the agency using its disclosure authority to achieve regulatory objectives outside its jurisdiction. And the regulatory framework for crypto-asset securities — a category the existing statutory framework was not designed to address — has generated substantial litigation over which digital assets constitute *securities* subject to the 1933 and 1934 Act frameworks in the first place.

---

This is the book's final chapter. The apparatus it has built — from contract formation through tort liability, criminal law, employment law, dispute resolution, antitrust, government regulation, unfair trade practices, and now securities regulation — is not a collection of isolated rules. It is a system. The rules interact: a public company that commits securities fraud can face SEC enforcement, criminal prosecution, private securities litigation, and derivative suits by shareholders simultaneously. The constitutional protections that apply in the criminal case interact with the disclosure obligations in the civil case. The antitrust framework and the securities framework can apply to the same transaction.

The practitioner who understands the apparatus as a system — who can see which legal frameworks a given transaction implicates, and what each framework requires — can do something the practitioner who knows only isolated rules cannot: navigate a complex commercial situation without missing the legal dimension that matters most.

Securities regulation, specifically, is where the information-asymmetry problem that runs through the entire apparatus is most explicit. Contracts address it through disclosure obligations and warranties. Tort law addresses it through fraud and misrepresentation doctrine. Securities law addresses it through mandatory registration, comprehensive ongoing disclosure, and a liability framework designed to make misrepresentation expensive enough that honest disclosure is the rational choice.

The underlying insight — that markets require accurate information to function, and that the law must create the conditions under which accurate information is produced and disclosed — is not specific to securities. It is the organizing principle of much of what this book has covered. The apparatus is, at its core, a mechanism for making the information environment of commerce reliable enough that the transactions commerce requires can actually happen.

You have the apparatus. The rest of your working life is where it operates.
