# Chapter 9 — Employment and Labor Law
*The locked door and the law that followed.*

---

On March 25, 1911, a fire broke out on the eighth floor of the Asch Building in lower Manhattan. The building housed the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory — a garment operation on the eighth, ninth, and tenth floors. The fire spread quickly through the cotton scraps and tissue paper that covered the floors. Workers tried to escape through the two stairwells. One door was locked from the outside. The owners locked it routinely, to prevent theft and unauthorized breaks.

One hundred and forty-six garment workers died. Most were young immigrant women. Many jumped from the upper-story windows rather than burn.

The owners were indicted for manslaughter. They were acquitted. Their insurance company paid them approximately $400 per body — $64,925 in total, slightly more than they had insured the building for. They netted roughly $400 per death.

I want you to sit with that number.

That outcome is what the legal framework of 1911 produced. There were no meaningful workplace-safety regulations. There was no worker protection worth the name. The employment relationship was, legally, an arrangement between a willing employer and a willing employee — and if both parties "agreed" to a situation with locked exits, the law had nothing to say. The owners had not done anything illegal. The door was locked; 146 people died; the owners walked out of court and collected their insurance money.

The contemporary employment-law framework is the accumulated institutional answer to that acquittal. Every statute I am going to describe in this chapter is a response to a specific, documented failure that the market did not correct and the pre-existing law did not prevent. Understanding the framework means understanding what each piece is answering.

That is the frame for this chapter.

## The statutory stack

The federal employment-law framework is not a single coherent system. It is a stack of statutes, each aimed at a specific failure mode, enacted decades apart, administered by different agencies, and enforced through overlapping mechanisms. Let me go through them in the order that matters — not chronological, but logical.

![Horizontal timeline of major federal employment-law statutes](images/09-employment-and-labor-law-fig-01.png)
*Figure 9.1 — Horizontal timeline of major federal employment-law statutes*

The **Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938** is the wage-and-hours statute. It sets the federal minimum wage, establishes the 40-hour workweek as the threshold beyond which non-exempt employees must be paid overtime at time-and-a-half, and prohibits certain child-labor practices. The FLSA was a direct response to Depression-era conditions in which employers with structural bargaining advantages could set wages at whatever desperate workers would accept. The federal minimum is currently $7.25 per hour — where it has been since July 2009 — with many states and cities operating significantly higher floors, as Chapter 1 described.

The FLSA's distinction between exempt and non-exempt employees is one of the most litigated questions in contemporary employment law. Exempt employees — executive, administrative, professional, outside-sales, and certain computer-employee categories — are not entitled to overtime, if they meet both a salary threshold and a duties test. The classification is not about job title. It is about what the employee actually does and how much they earn. Firms that classify workers as exempt to avoid overtime obligations when those workers don't meet the legal tests face substantial back-pay liability.

The **Civil Rights Act of 1964, Title VII** prohibits employment discrimination on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin, covering employers with 15 or more employees. It created the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission to investigate complaints. The Supreme Court's 2020 decision in *Bostock v. Clayton County* extended the sex-discrimination prohibition to discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity — straightforwardly, as the Court held, because discriminating against someone for being gay or transgender is discrimination "because of sex."

The **Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990** prohibits discrimination against qualified individuals with disabilities and requires employers to provide *reasonable accommodation* unless doing so would impose *undue hardship*. The operative framework runs through two key terms: a "qualified individual" can perform the essential functions of the position with or without accommodation; "reasonable accommodation" modifies the work environment without fundamentally altering the job or imposing disproportionate cost. The ADA Amendments Act of 2008 substantially broadened the definition of disability, reversing a line of Supreme Court decisions that had narrowed the Act's reach.

The **Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967** prohibits discrimination against workers 40 and older, at firms with 20 or more employees.

The **Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970** requires employers to provide a workplace free of recognized hazards and authorizes OSHA to inspect facilities, issue citations, and impose penalties. This is Triangle's most direct legislative descendant. It created the federal mechanism for requiring what the Asch Building's owners had not been required to provide: a workplace workers could actually exit in an emergency.

The **Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993** provides eligible employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per year for specified family and medical reasons, with continuation of group health coverage. It covers employers with 50 or more employees. Its existence reflects the fact that the workforce of 1993 was not the workforce of 1938 — a framework built on the assumption that most workers were male breadwinners with a non-employed spouse at home did not address the reality most working families actually faced.

The **National Labor Relations Act of 1935** is different in kind from all the others. It does not prohibit a specific harmful practice. It establishes a structure: the right of employees to organize, to bargain collectively, and to engage in *protected concerted activity*. Section 7 protects employees who act together "for mutual aid or protection." The Act created the National Labor Relations Board to administer the framework. The Taft-Hartley Act of 1947 added union unfair labor practices to the framework and is the source of the right-to-work laws that 27 states have enacted, prohibiting required union membership or dues as a condition of employment.

## The at-will doctrine and why the exceptions are not optional

The United States is unusual among developed economies in the breadth of its employment-at-will doctrine. The rule is simple: either party can end the employment relationship at any time, for any reason or no reason, with or without notice. No cause required. Approximately 85 percent of U.S. private-sector workers operate under it. Forty-nine states follow the doctrine by default; Montana is the exception, requiring good cause for termination after a probationary period.

The doctrine made intuitive sense when it was established in the late nineteenth century, if you believed the employment relationship was a contract between equals. Freedom to quit implies freedom to fire. The problem is that the premise — equal bargaining power — was not and is not generally accurate. An employer typically faces less severe consequences from losing one employee than that employee faces from losing the job. The structural asymmetry is exactly the kind of condition that produces the failure modes the regulatory framework was built to address.

Three exceptions chip away at the doctrine's scope, and understanding them is necessary to understand how the at-will rule and the federal statutes work together.

The **public-policy exception**, recognized in approximately 43 states, protects employees from termination for refusing to violate the law, for exercising a legal right (filing a workers' compensation claim, serving on a jury), or for performing a public obligation (reporting a crime, testifying in court). The exception exists because without it, the at-will doctrine creates a mechanism for nullifying legal protections. If an employer can fire a worker for filing a workers' compensation claim, the workers' compensation system does not, in practice, protect workers. The exception prevents the at-will doctrine from consuming the statutory rights that sit alongside it.

The **implied-contract exception**, recognized in approximately 38 states, allows employees to challenge terminations as breaches of an implied contract when the employer's words or conduct created a reasonable expectation of continued employment. Employee handbooks promising progressive discipline, supervisor assurances that employment will continue "as long as you do your job," established practices of long tenure — each can create the implied contract. The practical implication is direct: firms drafting handbooks need to be careful about language that can be read as limiting at-will employment, unless they intend to limit it.

The **covenant-of-good-faith exception**, recognized in approximately 11 states, protects against terminations made in bad faith — most commonly, terminations timed to deprive an employee of compensation already earned. Firing a salesperson the week before a large commission vests. Terminating a long-tenured employee a few months before her pension becomes guaranteed. The exception is narrow; it does not prohibit termination without cause. It prohibits terminations that are themselves a form of theft.

These exceptions operate alongside the federal anti-discrimination statutes, not instead of them. A termination that falls cleanly within the at-will doctrine — no implied contract, no public-policy violation, no bad faith — can still be an illegal termination if the actual reason was the employee's race, age, disability, or any other protected characteristic. The at-will doctrine does not permit discriminatory termination.

## The worker-classification problem

The most contested question in contemporary U.S. employment law is also one of the most economically consequential: is a given worker an employee or an independent contractor?

The classification determines nearly everything. Employees are entitled to minimum wage, overtime, employer FICA contributions, unemployment insurance, workers' compensation coverage, and the anti-discrimination protections of federal law. Independent contractors are entitled to none of those things. The employer pays roughly 7.65 percent of each employee's wages into Social Security and Medicare; for contractors, no employer contribution is required. Multiply that across a large workforce and the classification produces enormous cost differences.

The gig-economy business model — Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, and dozens of others — is built on independent-contractor classification. The companies provide the platform; the workers provide the labor; the companies argue that the workers are independent businesses, not employees. Whether that argument is legally correct has been litigated in courts across the United States and in multiple other countries, without final resolution.

Three different tests apply to the classification question.

The **IRS test** examines three broad categories. Behavioral control: does the firm direct how the work is performed — not just the outcome, but the manner and means? Financial control: does the worker bear the financial risk? Does she invest in her own equipment, work for multiple clients, face the risk of profit and loss? Relationship of the parties: is the work integral to the firm's core business? Is the relationship indefinite or project-specific?

The **DOL economic-realities test** under the FLSA focuses on a single overriding question — is the worker economically dependent on the firm? — approached through multiple factors: opportunity for profit or loss, investment in equipment, degree of skill required, permanence of the relationship, and the extent to which the work is integral to the employer's core business. The test tries to identify the structural reality of the relationship, not its formal label.

The **ABC test**, used in California and several other states, is the most protective of workers. It presumes employee status and requires the firm to establish all three of: (A) the worker is free from the firm's control in how she performs the work; (B) the work is outside the usual course of the firm's business; and (C) the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade. The B prong is the difficult one for platform companies. It is hard to argue that driving passengers is outside the usual course of Uber's business when driving passengers is Uber's entire business.

California's 2019 AB 5 codified the ABC test for most workers, making most gig-economy workers employees rather than contractors. Proposition 22, passed in 2020 after a roughly $200 million campaign funded primarily by Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, and Instacart, carved app-based transportation and delivery companies out of AB 5's reach. The fight between AB 5 and Proposition 22 is a compressed version of the broader structural dispute: the classification question is not primarily a doctrinal puzzle. It is a question about who bears the cost of labor conditions.

The historical precedent is worth knowing. In 2000, Microsoft settled a class action for $97 million brought by long-tenured contractors — workers classified as independent contractors who had worked alongside Microsoft employees doing identical work for years and had been excluded from the company's stock option plan. The structural condition exposed — firms using contractor status to exclude workers from benefit programs while integrating those workers fully into the business — is precisely what the multi-factor tests are designed to identify.

## Retaliation: the protection that makes all the others work

One of the most important provisions in employment law is not the discrimination prohibition itself but its companion: the prohibition on retaliation against employees who invoke the protections.

Here is why it is not just a detail. Suppose Title VII prohibits discrimination but has no anti-retaliation provision. An employee who believes she has been discriminated against can file a charge with the EEOC. But if her employer can immediately fire her for filing that charge, the anti-discrimination protection only applies to employees willing to lose their jobs to invoke it. That is not a meaningful protection. The retaliation prohibition is what makes the underlying rights real rather than theoretical.

Anti-retaliation provisions exist in Title VII, the ADA, the ADEA, the FMLA, OSHA, and most other major employment statutes. The protection covers two categories of activity. *Participation activity* — filing a charge, participating in an EEOC investigation — is protected absolutely. *Opposition activity* — complaining internally about what the employee reasonably believes is illegal discrimination — is protected even if the underlying complaint turns out to be legally incorrect, provided the belief was reasonable.

The retaliation claim is analytically independent of the underlying claim. A plaintiff who cannot prove the underlying harassment occurred can still prevail on a retaliation claim by showing that she made a complaint, the employer knew about it, and an adverse employment action followed. Temporal proximity — being fired two weeks after filing an EEOC charge — is circumstantial evidence supporting the retaliation claim even when direct evidence of motive is unavailable.

Consider what this looks like in practice. A 52-year-old employee files an internal harassment complaint. Two weeks later she is terminated. She has eleven years of positive performance reviews and no documented performance issues. The combination — protected characteristic (age), protected activity (harassment complaint), adverse action (termination), no independent legitimate reason on the record — is the fact pattern that employment-discrimination cases are built from. The at-will doctrine is irrelevant to that analysis. The right to terminate at will does not include the right to terminate in retaliation for invoking a federal statutory protection.

## What concerted activity actually covers

The NLRA framework works differently from the anti-discrimination statutes. It establishes a structure rather than prohibiting a specific practice — and the structure protects something broader than most people realize.

Section 7 protects employees who act together "for mutual aid or protection." The protection extends well beyond union organizing and formal collective bargaining. Two employees who discuss their salaries and discover a pay disparity are engaged in protected concerted activity. Three employees who complain collectively to their supervisor about an unsafe working condition are engaged in protected concerted activity. Employees who post on social media about working conditions — if the posts relate to workplace conditions and involve more than one employee, or are intended to initiate group action — may be engaged in protected concerted activity.

This breadth is surprising to most people who have not studied the Act. A firm with no union, no organizing drive underway, and no interest in collective bargaining is still subject to Section 7 protection for its employees' concerted activity about working conditions. Firing two employees because they complained together to HR about a supervisor's conduct is an unfair labor practice under the NLRA, union or no union.

The Act also prohibits specific employer unfair labor practices: interfering with, restraining, or coercing employees in their Section 7 rights; dominating or interfering with a labor organization; discriminating against employees for union activity; retaliating for filing NLRA charges; and refusing to bargain in good faith with a certified union.

## Back to the locked door

Return to the Asch Building. The owners were acquitted because, under the law of 1911, they had done nothing illegal. No statute required the door to be unlocked. No agency had authority to inspect the building. No legal framework prohibited the labor conditions that made the fire fatal rather than dangerous.

The contemporary framework is the accumulated answer to that acquittal. OSHA is the locked-door statute — the mechanism for requiring what the Asch Building's owners were not required to provide. The FLSA is the answer to wages set by employers with structural bargaining advantages over workers with no alternatives. Title VII is the answer to systematic exclusion from employment based on characteristics unrelated to job performance. The NLRA is the answer to the absence of any institutional mechanism for workers to address structural power imbalances through collective action.

The framework has real limits. It does not reach the smallest employers — firms below the statutory thresholds operate largely outside the federal anti-discrimination regime. The gig-economy classification dispute is an active example of a failure mode the existing framework addresses imperfectly: the multi-factor tests were designed for a world in which the question of who controls your work was clearer than platform-mediated labor makes it. The framework is enforced unevenly. It is contested constantly.

But these limits are best understood against the baseline. The baseline is 1911. The baseline is a locked door, an acquitted owner, and a net profit of $400 per death.

Every workplace protection in contemporary law is the distance between that baseline and where the law has moved since. The question the framework keeps asking — in worker-classification litigation, in accommodation disputes, in wage-and-hour cases, in collective-bargaining negotiations — is how far that distance should be, and who should bear the cost of increasing it.

That is not an abstract question. It is what the Triangle fire posed, and what the law has been answering, imperfectly and incompletely, ever since.
