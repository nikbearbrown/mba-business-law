# Employment and Labor Law

In April 1911, in lower Manhattan, the *Triangle Shirtwaist Factory* — a garment-manufacturing operation occupying the eighth, ninth, and tenth floors of the Asch Building near Washington Square — caught fire. The fire spread quickly through the cotton fabric and tissue paper that filled the floors. Workers attempted to escape through the building's two stairwells, but one of the stairwell doors had been locked from the outside — a routine practice the factory's owners had used to prevent worker theft and unauthorized breaks. One hundred and forty-six garment workers, most of them young immigrant women, died. Many of the deaths occurred when workers jumped from upper-story windows rather than burn alive.

The fire was the proximate cause of a substantial transformation of U.S. employment law. New York State enacted comprehensive workplace-safety regulation. The federal government, over the next several decades, built the modern framework of employment law — the National Labor Relations Act of 1935, the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938, the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970, the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993. The contemporary U.S. employment-law framework is the residue of more than a century of accumulated regulatory response to specific workplace failures.

This chapter is the operating manual for that framework as it applies to contemporary U.S. employment.

## Concept 1: The federal employment-law framework

Several major federal statutes do most of the work in U.S. employment law.

**The Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (FLSA)** sets minimum wage, overtime pay, and child-labor restrictions. The federal minimum wage has been $7.25 per hour since July 2009; many states and cities operate higher minimums. Overtime — generally time-and-a-half pay for hours over 40 per week — applies to *non-exempt* employees; *exempt* employees (executive, administrative, professional, computer, and outside-sales categories meeting specified salary and duties tests) are not entitled to overtime.

**Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964** prohibits discrimination on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. The Act created the *Equal Employment Opportunity Commission* (EEOC), which investigates and litigates discrimination complaints. The Act covers employers with 15 or more employees. The Supreme Court's *Bostock v. Clayton County* (2020) extended Title VII's sex-discrimination prohibition to discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity.

**The Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA)** prohibits discrimination against qualified individuals with disabilities and requires *reasonable accommodation* unless it would impose *undue hardship*. The ADA Amendments Act of 2008 substantially broadened the definition of disability.

**The Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA)** prohibits discrimination against workers 40 and older. Covered employers: those with 20 or more employees.

**The National Labor Relations Act of 1935 (NLRA, the *Wagner Act*)** establishes the right to organize, bargain collectively, and engage in *protected concerted activity*. The Act created the *National Labor Relations Board* (NLRB), which administers the framework. Section 7 of the Act protects employees who act *for mutual aid or protection* — which includes union organizing, collective bargaining, and concerted complaints about working conditions.

**The Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 (FMLA)** provides eligible employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid leave per year for specified family and medical reasons, with continuation of group health insurance coverage. Covered employers: those with 50 or more employees.

**The Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970 (OSHA)** requires employers to provide a workplace free of recognized hazards and authorizes the Occupational Safety and Health Administration to inspect, cite, and penalize violations.

## Concept 2: Employment at will and its exceptions

The U.S. *employment-at-will* doctrine — under which approximately 85 percent of U.S. private-sector workers operate — provides that either party can terminate the employment relationship at any time, with or without cause, with or without notice. The doctrine is the default rule in 49 of the 50 U.S. states (Montana is the exception, where employees who have completed a probationary period can only be terminated for *good cause*).

Three major exceptions modify the at-will doctrine.

**Public-policy exception.** An employee cannot be terminated for refusing to violate the law, for exercising a legal right (filing a workers' compensation claim, serving on a jury), or for performing a public obligation. The exception is recognized in approximately 43 states.

**Implied-contract exception.** When an employer's words or conduct create an implied contract — through employee handbook language, oral assurances, or established practice — the employee can challenge a termination as a breach of that implied contract. The exception is recognized in approximately 38 states.

**Covenant-of-good-faith exception.** When an employer terminates an employee in bad faith — for example, to avoid paying a commission the employee has earned — the termination can be challenged. The exception is recognized in approximately 11 states.

In addition, federal anti-discrimination statutes (Title VII, ADA, ADEA) and state analogs prohibit terminations based on protected characteristics. A terminated employee whose termination involved protected characteristics has a federal or state anti-discrimination claim independent of the at-will doctrine.

## Concept 3: Worker classification and contemporary disputes

The classification of workers as *employees* or *independent contractors* is one of the most contested questions in contemporary U.S. employment law. The classification determines whether minimum wage, overtime, employer-provided health insurance, employer FICA contribution, unemployment insurance, workers' compensation, and most anti-discrimination protections apply.

### The multi-factor tests

Several different tests apply depending on context.

**The IRS test** focuses on three broad categories: behavioral control (does the firm have the right to direct how the work is performed?), financial control (does the worker bear the financial risk of the work?), and relationship of the parties (is the work integral to the firm's business; what does the contract say?). The IRS uses the test for tax-purposes classification.

**The DOL economic-realities test** under the FLSA looks at the worker's economic dependence on the firm, with multiple factors: opportunity for profit or loss, investment in equipment, skill required, permanence of the relationship, control by the firm, and integration into the firm's business.

**The common-law agency test** for various state-law purposes focuses heavily on the right to control the manner of performance.

**The ABC test**, used in California (after the 2018 *Dynamex* decision and AB 5) and several other states, presumes employee status unless the firm can establish (A) freedom from control, (B) work outside the firm's usual course of business, and (C) the worker's independent trade or business.

The tests overlap but are not identical. A worker can be classified differently for different legal purposes. The contemporary gig-economy fights — Uber, Lyft, DoorDash — turn substantially on which test applies.

### Worker-classification cases

The Microsoft 2000 settlement ($97 million for stock options denied to long-tenured contractors classified as independent contractors despite working alongside employees on identical work) is the leading historical precedent. California's AB 5 (2019) and Proposition 22 (2020), the U.K. Supreme Court's 2021 *Uber B.V. v. Aslam* decision, and the EU's Platform Work Directive of 2024 are the contemporary frontier.

## Integration: a workplace dispute through the framework

A mid-sized firm — call it Hartcourt — terminates a 52-year-old employee after she files a complaint about her manager's harassment. The employee has worked at the firm for 11 years, has consistent positive performance reviews, and has no documented performance issues. She files a charge with the EEOC.

Apply the framework.

*Title VII (sex)* — sex-based harassment claim. *ADEA* — age-discrimination claim if the termination correlates with the employee's age. *Title VII retaliation* — a separate claim if the termination was in response to her harassment complaint, regardless of whether the underlying harassment claim succeeds.

*Public-policy exception to at-will* — additional claim under state law in many states.

*Implied contract* — fact-specific based on Hartcourt's handbook and policies.

The integration verdict: a termination after a protected complaint produces multiple overlapping claims under federal and state law. Settlements in such cases are typically substantial. The firm's risk management should include clear documentation of independent legitimate reasons for any termination decision near a protected activity.
