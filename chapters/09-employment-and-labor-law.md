# Chapter 9 — Employment and Labor Law
*The locked door and the law that followed.*

---

Here is where the contemporary framework of U.S. employment law comes from.

On March 25, 1911, a fire broke out on the eighth floor of the Asch Building in lower Manhattan. The building housed the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory — a garment operation occupying the eighth, ninth, and tenth floors. The fire spread quickly through the cotton fabric scraps and tissue paper that covered the floors. Workers tried to escape through the building's two stairwells. One stairwell door was locked from the outside. The owners had locked it routinely — to prevent theft and unauthorized breaks.

One hundred and forty-six garment workers died that day. Most were young immigrant women. Many jumped from the upper-story windows rather than burn.

The owners were indicted for manslaughter. They were acquitted. Their insurance company paid them $400 per body killed — $64,925 in total — approximately $400 more than they had paid to insure the building. They collected a net profit of $400 per death.

That outcome is what the legal framework before 1911 produced. There were no meaningful workplace-safety regulations. There was no meaningful worker protection. The employment relationship was, in legal terms, an arrangement between a willing employer and a willing employee — and if both parties "agreed" to a situation in which doors were locked and exits were blocked, the law had little to say about it.

The Triangle fire is where the modern framework begins. Not because one fire changed everything overnight, but because it was the event visible enough, and the injustice documented enough, to make the costs of the existing system impossible to ignore. New York enacted comprehensive workplace-safety legislation within two years. Over the following half century, Congress built the federal structure that governs employment today: the National Labor Relations Act of 1935, the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938, Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970, the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993.

Every statute in that list is a response to a documented failure of the employment relationship — a failure that the market did not correct on its own and that the pre-existing legal framework did not prevent. The contemporary employment-law regime is not abstract regulation imposed on an otherwise-functional system. It is the accumulated institutional memory of what happens when the system operates without it.

That is the frame I want you to carry through this chapter.

## The structure of the framework: what each statute actually does

The federal employment-law framework is not a single coherent system designed from scratch. It is a stack of statutes, each aimed at a specific failure mode, enacted decades apart, administered by different agencies, and enforced through overlapping mechanisms. Understanding it requires knowing what each piece does and why it exists.

**The Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938** is the wage-and-hours statute. It sets the federal minimum wage (currently $7.25 per hour, where it has been since July 2009; many states and cities operate significantly higher minimums), establishes the 40-hour workweek as the threshold beyond which *non-exempt* employees must be paid overtime at time-and-a-half, and prohibits certain child-labor practices. The Act was a direct response to the conditions of the Depression-era labor market, in which employers with significant bargaining leverage could set wages at whatever the market — desperate workers with few alternatives — would bear.

The FLSA's distinction between exempt and non-exempt employees is one of the most litigated questions in contemporary employment law. *Exempt* employees — executive, administrative, professional, computer, and outside-sales categories — are not entitled to overtime, provided they meet both a salary threshold and a duties test. The classification is not about job title or how the employee is compensated; it is about what the employee actually does and how much they earn. Firms that classify workers as exempt to avoid overtime obligations, when those workers do not meet the legal tests, face substantial back-pay exposure and liquidated damages.

**Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964** prohibits discrimination in employment on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. The Act covers employers with 15 or more employees and created the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to investigate and enforce discrimination complaints. The statute was one of the central legislative achievements of the civil rights movement; its passage, over sustained filibuster, marked a fundamental shift in the legal relationship between employers and employees.

The Supreme Court's 2020 decision in *Bostock v. Clayton County* extended Title VII's sex-discrimination prohibition to discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity. The decision was straightforward as a matter of statutory text: discriminating against an employee because they are gay or transgender is, as the Court held, discrimination "because of sex." The decision was significant as a matter of effect: it extended federal anti-discrimination protection to a category of workers who had been explicitly excluded from most state analogs.

**The Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA)** prohibits discrimination against *qualified individuals with disabilities* and requires employers to provide *reasonable accommodation* unless doing so would impose *undue hardship* on the employer. The ADA's operative framework runs through two key terms. A "qualified individual" is someone who can perform the *essential functions* of the position, with or without reasonable accommodation. "Reasonable accommodation" is a modification that enables the qualified individual to perform those functions without fundamentally altering the nature of the job or imposing disproportionate cost. The ADA Amendments Act of 2008 substantially broadened the statute's definition of disability, largely reversing a series of Supreme Court decisions that had narrowed the Act's coverage.

**The Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA)** prohibits discrimination against workers 40 and older, covering employers with 20 or more employees. The statute is the employment-specific application of a principle that runs through much of civil rights law: categories that have been systematically used as proxies for hiring and firing decisions, in ways that bear no relationship to actual job performance, are the categories that deserve legal protection.

**The Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970 (OSHA)** requires employers to provide a workplace free of recognized hazards and authorizes the Occupational Safety and Health Administration to inspect facilities, issue citations, and impose penalties for violations. The Act was Triangle's most direct legislative descendant: it created the federal mechanism for requiring what the Asch Building's owners had not been required to do — maintain a workplace that workers could actually exit in an emergency.

**The Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 (FMLA)** provides eligible employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per year for specified family and medical reasons — the birth or adoption of a child, a serious health condition of the employee or a close family member — with continuation of group health insurance coverage. The Act covers employers with 50 or more employees. It is a partial response to a structural fact: the workforce changed significantly between 1938 and 1993, and a framework built when most workers were assumed to be male heads of household with a non-employed spouse at home did not adequately address the reality of a workforce in which that assumption had long since ceased to describe most working families.

**The National Labor Relations Act of 1935** is different in kind from the others. It does not prohibit a specific harmful practice. It establishes a structure: the right of employees to organize, to bargain collectively with their employer, and to engage in *protected concerted activity*. Section 7 of the Act protects employees who act together "for mutual aid or protection" — which includes union organizing, collective bargaining, and collective complaints about working conditions, whether or not a formal union is involved. The Act created the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) to administer the framework. The tension built into the Act — between employer prerogatives and employee organizing rights — has been contested in every decade since the Act's passage.

## The employment-at-will doctrine and why the exceptions matter

The United States is unusual among developed economies in the breadth of its *employment-at-will* doctrine. The rule is simple: either party can terminate the employment relationship at any time, for any reason or no reason, with or without notice. The employer does not need cause. The employee does not need to give notice. The default is pure freedom of exit — and approximately 85 percent of U.S. private-sector workers operate under it.

Forty-nine states follow this doctrine by default. Montana is the exception: employees who complete a probationary period can be terminated only for good cause.

The at-will doctrine made intuitive sense when it was established in the late nineteenth century. If you believe the employment relationship is a contract between equals, freely entered and freely exited, then freedom to terminate is the logical correlate of freedom to quit. The problem is that the premise — equality of bargaining power between employer and employee — was not and is not generally accurate. An employer typically faces less severe consequences from losing a single employee than that employee faces from losing the job. The bargaining asymmetry is exactly the kind of structural condition that produces the failure modes the regulatory framework was designed to address.

Three major exceptions chip away at the doctrine's scope.

**The public-policy exception**, recognized in approximately 43 states, protects employees from termination for refusing to violate the law, for exercising a legal right (filing a workers' compensation claim, serving on a jury), or for performing a public obligation (reporting a crime, testifying). The exception exists because a system of at-will employment, without this carve-out, creates perverse incentives: employers could effectively nullify legal protections by firing workers who invoked them. If you can fire a worker for filing a workers' compensation claim, the workers' compensation system does not, in practice, protect workers.

**The implied-contract exception**, recognized in approximately 38 states, allows employees to challenge terminations as breaches of an implied contract when the employer's words or conduct created a reasonable expectation of continued employment. Employee handbooks that promise progressive discipline, oral assurances from supervisors that employment will continue "as long as you do your job," established practices of long tenure — each can create the implied contract. The practical implication is that firms drafting employee handbooks need to be careful about language that can be read as limiting the at-will doctrine, unless they intend to limit it.

**The covenant-of-good-faith exception**, recognized in approximately 11 states, protects against terminations made in bad faith — most commonly, terminations timed to deprive an employee of compensation she has earned (firing a salesperson the week before a large commission vests, or terminating a long-tenured employee a few months before her pension becomes guaranteed). The exception is narrow and fact-specific. It is not a general prohibition on termination without cause; it is a prohibition on terminations that are themselves a form of theft.

These three exceptions operate alongside the federal anti-discrimination statutes. A termination that falls within the at-will doctrine — no implied contract, no public-policy violation, no bad faith — can still be an illegal termination under Title VII if the employer's actual reason was the employee's race, sex, religion, national origin, or any other protected characteristic. The at-will doctrine does not permit discriminatory termination; it just does not, on its own, prohibit non-discriminatory termination.

## The worker-classification problem

The most contested question in contemporary U.S. employment law is also one of the most consequential: is a given worker an *employee* or an *independent contractor*?

The classification determines nearly everything. Employees are entitled to minimum wage, overtime, employer contributions to Social Security and Medicare, unemployment insurance, workers' compensation coverage, and the anti-discrimination protections of federal law. Independent contractors are entitled to none of those things. The employer pays roughly 7.65 percent of each employee's wages into FICA (Social Security and Medicare); for independent contractors, no employer contribution is required. Multiply that across a large workforce and the classification produces enormous differences in cost.

The gig-economy business model — Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, and dozens of others — is built on independent-contractor classification. The companies provide the platform; the workers provide the labor; the companies' argument is that the workers are independent businesses using their platform, not employees of the company. Whether that argument is legally correct is a live dispute that has been litigated in courts across the United States and in multiple other countries.

Three different tests apply to the classification question, depending on context.

**The IRS test** looks at three broad categories. *Behavioral control*: does the firm have the right to direct how the work is performed — not just the outcome, but the manner and means of performing it? *Financial control*: does the worker bear the financial risk of the work? Does the worker invest in her own equipment, work for multiple clients, bear the risk of profit and loss? *Relationship of the parties*: is the work integral to the firm's core business? Is the relationship indefinite or project-specific?

**The DOL economic-realities test** under the FLSA focuses on a single question — is the worker economically dependent on the firm? — approached through multiple factors: opportunity for profit or loss, investment in equipment or materials, degree of skill required, permanence of the relationship, and the extent to which the work is integral to the employer's business. The test is designed to identify the structural reality of the relationship, not just its formal label.

**The ABC test**, used in California and several other states, is the most worker-protective. It presumes employee status and requires the firm to establish all three of: (A) the worker is free from the firm's control in performing the work; (B) the work is performed outside the usual course of the firm's business; and (C) the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade or occupation. The B prong is the difficult one for platform companies: it is hard to argue that driving passengers is outside the usual course of Uber's business when driving passengers is Uber's business.

California's 2019 AB 5 codified the ABC test for most workers and produced exactly the conflict its proponents intended: it meant most gig-economy workers in California were employees, not contractors. Proposition 22, passed by California voters in 2020 after an approximately $200 million campaign funded primarily by Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, and Instacart, carved app-based transportation and delivery companies out of AB 5's reach. The fight between AB 5 and Proposition 22 is a compressed version of the broader structural dispute: the classification question is not primarily a doctrinal puzzle. It is a question about who bears the cost of labor conditions.

The leading historical precedent is worth knowing. In 2000, Microsoft settled a class action for $97 million brought by long-tenured contractors — workers classified as independent contractors who had worked alongside Microsoft employees doing identical work for years — who had been denied participation in the company's stock option plan. The structural condition the settlement exposed — firms using contractor status to exclude workers from benefit programs while integrating those workers fully into the business — is precisely what the multi-factor tests are designed to identify.

## Retaliation: the protection that makes all the others work

One of the most important provisions in U.S. employment law is not the discrimination prohibition itself but its companion: the prohibition on *retaliation* against employees who invoke the discrimination protections.

Here is why it matters. Suppose Title VII prohibits discrimination but contains no anti-retaliation provision. An employee who believes she has been discriminated against can file a charge with the EEOC. But if her employer can immediately fire her for filing that charge, the practical effect is that the anti-discrimination protection only applies to employees who are willing to lose their jobs to invoke it. That is not a meaningful protection.

Anti-retaliation provisions exist in Title VII, the ADA, the ADEA, the FMLA, OSHA, and most other major employment statutes. The protection covers two categories of activity. *Participation activity* — filing a charge, participating in an EEOC investigation — is protected absolutely. *Opposition activity* — complaining internally about what the employee reasonably believes to be illegal discrimination — is protected even if the underlying complaint turns out to be legally incorrect, provided the belief was reasonable.

The retaliation claim is analytically independent of the underlying discrimination claim. A plaintiff who cannot prove the underlying harassment occurred can still prevail on a retaliation claim if she can show that she made a complaint, the employer knew about the complaint, and an adverse employment action followed. Temporal proximity — being fired two weeks after filing an EEOC charge — is circumstantial evidence supporting the retaliation claim even when direct evidence of motive is unavailable.

Consider what the termination pattern looks like in practice: a 52-year-old employee files a harassment complaint. Two weeks later, she is terminated. She has eleven years of consistent positive performance reviews and no documented performance issues. The combination — protected characteristic (age), protected activity (harassment complaint), adverse action (termination), no independent legitimate reason on the record — is the fact pattern that employment-discrimination cases are made of. The at-will doctrine is irrelevant to that analysis. The employer's right to terminate at will does not include the right to terminate in retaliation for invoking a federal statutory protection.

## Collective bargaining and what concerted activity actually covers

The NLRA framework operates differently from the anti-discrimination statutes. It does not prohibit a specific harmful practice. It establishes a structure — and the structure protects something that is broader than most people realize.

Section 7 of the Act protects employees who act together "for mutual aid or protection." The protection extends well beyond union organizing and formal collective bargaining. Two employees who discuss their salaries and discover a gender pay disparity are engaged in protected concerted activity. Three employees who complain collectively to their supervisor about an unsafe working condition are engaged in protected concerted activity. Employees who post on social media about working conditions — if the posts relate to workplace conditions and involve more than one employee, or are intended to initiate group action — are potentially engaged in protected concerted activity.

This is worth pausing on, because the breadth is surprising to most people who have not studied the Act carefully. A firm that has no union, has no organizing drive underway, and has no interest whatsoever in the collective-bargaining framework is still subject to Section 7 protection for its employees' concerted activity about working conditions. Firing two employees because they complained together to HR about a supervisor's conduct is an unfair labor practice under the NLRA, union or no union.

The NLRA also prohibits specific unfair labor practices by employers: interfering with, restraining, or coercing employees in their Section 7 rights; dominating or interfering with the formation of a labor organization; discriminating against employees for union activity; retaliating against employees for filing NLRA charges; and refusing to bargain in good faith with a union that has been certified as the employees' representative.

The Taft-Hartley Act of 1947 added unfair labor practices by unions to the framework and is the source of the *right-to-work* laws that 27 states have enacted — laws that prohibit requiring union membership or dues payment as a condition of employment. The practical effect of right-to-work laws on union strength and organizing is substantial; they are among the most contested provisions in contemporary labor law.

## What the framework is actually doing

Return to the Triangle fire. The owners were acquitted because, under the legal framework that existed in 1911, they had not done anything illegal. There was no statute requiring the door to be unlocked. There was no regulatory agency with authority to inspect the building. There was no prohibition on the labor practices that produced the conditions in which the fire was fatal rather than merely dangerous.

The contemporary framework is the accumulated answer to that acquittal. Each statute represents a category of harm that the market did not correct and the pre-regulatory framework did not prevent. OSHA is the locked-door statute. The FLSA is the response to wages set at starvation levels by employers with structural bargaining advantages. Title VII is the response to systematic exclusion from employment based on characteristics unrelated to job performance. The NLRA is the response to the absence of any institutional mechanism for workers to address structural power imbalances through collective action.

The framework has limits. It does not reach employers below the statutory thresholds — firms with fewer than 15 employees operate largely outside the federal anti-discrimination regime. It is enforced unevenly. The gig-economy classification dispute is an active example of a failure mode that the existing framework addresses imperfectly: the tests were designed for a world in which the question of who controls your work was clearer than platform-mediated labor makes it.

But the framework's limitations are best understood against the baseline. The baseline is 1911. The baseline is a locked door, an acquitted owner, and a net profit of $400 per death.

Every workplace protection that exists today is the distance between that baseline and where the law has moved since. The question the framework keeps asking — in worker-classification litigation, in accommodation disputes, in wage-and-hour cases, in collective bargaining negotiations — is how far that distance should be, and who should bear the cost of increasing it.

## What you can now do

You can identify the major federal employment-law statutes and explain what each one prohibits, whom it covers, and which agency administers it. You know the FLSA from the NLRA from Title VII from the ADA from the ADEA from the FMLA from OSHA — not as a list of acronyms but as a set of responses to specific documented failures.

You can apply the employment-at-will doctrine with precision. You know the default rule, the three major exceptions, and why the exceptions are necessary to prevent the at-will doctrine from swallowing the statutory protections that exist alongside it.

You can analyze a worker-classification question using the IRS, DOL, and ABC tests, explain where they differ, and identify the structural fact — economic dependence, control over the manner of work, integration into the firm's core business — that drives the analysis under each.

You can trace a retaliation claim from the underlying protected activity through the adverse action to the legal framework that makes the claim viable even when the underlying discrimination cannot be proven.

And you can explain what the NLRA's protection of concerted activity actually covers — which is broader than most people assume and more practically applicable to most workplaces than union organizing.

The single idea that matters most is that the employment-law framework is not background noise. It is the answer to the question the Triangle fire posed: what should the law require of the employment relationship, given what happens when it requires nothing?
