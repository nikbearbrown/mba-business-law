# Business Ethics and Social Responsibility

In April 2015, the founder and chief executive of a Seattle-based credit-card processing company called Gravity Payments — Dan Price, then thirty years old — announced at an internal company meeting that the firm's minimum salary would rise to $70,000 per year, phased in over three years, and that he would cut his own salary from approximately one million dollars to the same $70,000 to fund part of the increase. The decision was framed by Price as an ethical commitment to the firm's employees. The decision was, simultaneously, a substantial commercial bet — the firm's revenue at the time was approximately $20 million per year, and the wage increase committed the firm to a substantial increase in payroll cost.

Three years later, Gravity Payments had grown its revenue substantially, retained more than 90 percent of its workforce through the wage transition, and become one of the most-cited contemporary examples of an ethical-business commitment producing improved commercial performance.

The Gravity Payments case is the clean version of a question that the broader landscape of business ethics keeps asking. *Are ethical commitments compatible with commercial performance?* The contemporary evidence — McKinsey's *Diversity Matters* studies, Corporate Knights' Global 100 returns, the Edelman Trust Barometer's annual surveys, the documented performance of B-Corp-certified firms — supports an affirmative answer with substantial qualifications. Ethical commitments are compatible with commercial performance when they are operationally implemented; they fail when they are stated in mission documents but not enforced in compensation, promotion, and accountability structures.

This chapter is the legal-context overview of business ethics. The companion textbook *Business Ethics* covers the territory in much greater depth — the four major ethical traditions (virtue, utilitarianism, deontology, contractualism), the stakeholder map, the special stakeholders (society, environment, government), the cross-cultural framework, the employer/employee duties, the rights-of-all framework, the industry-specific pressures, and the future-of-work analysis. The reader for whom business ethics is a substantial professional concern should consult the companion book. The chapter here provides the legal-system framing that the law-textbook reader needs.

## Why this chapter matters

Most business-ethics failures produce legal consequences — settlements, fines, regulatory actions, criminal prosecutions of executives. The chapter is about the legal apparatus through which ethics violations become legal violations and about the operational frameworks the firm can adopt to reduce the probability of either kind of failure.

## Concept 1: Compliance is the floor; ethics is what is built above the floor

Chapter 1 of the companion *Business Ethics* textbook anchors the entire field on a single distinction: legal compliance is the floor of obligation, and ethics is what the firm chooses to do above the floor. The distinction is not academic. It is the operating frame inside which most business-ethics decisions happen.

A specific firm operates under a federal regulatory regime (Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, dozens of others) and a parallel state regulatory regime. The regimes specify the minimum conduct the firm must perform to avoid legal liability. The firm has two broad strategic choices: operate at the regulatory minimum, or operate above it. Most successful firms operate above it, in part because the operative-culture costs of operating at the minimum (employee turnover, customer dissatisfaction, reputational risk) typically exceed the financial cost of operating above it.

### The mechanism: the operative culture above the floor

Several specific concepts anchor the operative-culture discussion.

**Tone at the top.** The operative culture of a firm tracks, more closely than any other variable, the operative culture of its senior leadership. The mechanism operates through observation and imitation: employees observe what their senior leaders actually do (rather than what the firm's stated values say) and adjust their own conduct accordingly. The firm whose senior leadership operates ethically produces an ethical operative culture; the firm whose senior leadership operates unethically produces an unethical one, regardless of what the printed code of conduct says.

**The Gravity Payments case.** Dan Price's decision to raise the firm's minimum salary to $70,000 was a tone-at-the-top decision. The decision aligned the firm's stated commitment to employee welfare with its operative compensation structure. The post-decision retention rate (over 90 percent) and the post-decision revenue growth are consistent with the framework's prediction.

**The contrasting cases.** When senior leadership operates outside the firm's stated values, the operative culture tracks the operating behavior, not the stated values. The McDonald's funding of the Nixon campaign in the 1970s (in support of legislation that would have lowered fast-food workers' minimum wages); the 2017 sequence of internal harassment disclosures at Uber under Travis Kalanick; the Wells Fargo unauthorized-account scandal that surfaced in 2016 — each is an instance of senior leadership operating at variance with the firm's stated values, and each produced operative-culture consequences that exceeded the immediate financial cost of the underlying misconduct.

### The tools: codes of conduct, whistleblower policies, third-party investigation

Three tools are now standard in most large U.S. firms.

**Codes of conduct.** A written enumeration of the daily behavioral expectations the firm imposes on employees, covering: legal compliance with applicable statutes; conflicts of interest and disclosure obligations; care of company property; cybersecurity and data handling; social-media conduct; workplace respect and harassment prohibition; gift policies. The Exxon Mobil Code of Conduct is widely cited as a representative example. Codes are operationally useful when the firm enforces them; they are decorative when the firm does not.

**Whistleblower policies.** A formal procedure by which employees who observe misconduct can report it without retaliation. Effective whistleblower policies specify: confidential reporting channels (typically including a hotline operated by a third party), protection against retaliation, escalation procedures when initial reports do not produce action, and documentation requirements. The federal Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 imposes specific whistleblower-protection requirements on publicly-traded firms; the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010 extends protection and adds specific reward provisions for disclosures to the SEC.

**Third-party investigation.** When ethics complaints reach a level requiring formal investigation, the firm typically retains an outside law firm or specialized investigative consultant to conduct the investigation. The use of third parties reduces the perceived conflict of interest in internal investigations; it also produces investigative work that is, in subsequent litigation, more likely to be defensible.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that legal compliance is sufficient. It is, on a strict legal reading. It is not, on the operative-culture reading that determines the firm's long-run performance. The companion *Business Ethics* textbook argues this in detail.

The second is that ethical commitments are charity. The Gravity Payments case and the broader McKinsey *Diversity Matters* findings are the standing rebuttals; ethical commitments, when operationally implemented, correlate with improved commercial performance.

The third is that codes of conduct produce ethical cultures. They do not, on their own. The operative culture is produced by what is rewarded, what is punished, and what senior leadership models. The code of conduct is one input; the compensation, promotion, and accountability structures are the others.

## Concept 2: The history of corporate governance and the post-1985 ethics-office regime

The contemporary U.S. corporate-governance regime — the formal institutional apparatus through which firms attempt to constrain unethical behavior — has a documented historical origin that is substantially more recent than most readers assume.

In 1985, in response to a sequence of defense-procurement scandals during the Reagan-administration military buildup, the defense contractor *General Dynamics* established what is now considered the first formal corporate ethics office in U.S. business. The office had a documented charter, a reporting structure independent of operational management, and the authority to investigate and act on ethics complaints. The model was adopted, over the following decade, by other major defense contractors and gradually by other industries.

The Defense Industry Initiative on Business Ethics and Conduct (DII), established in 1986, was the cross-firm institutional partner of the General Dynamics ethics-office model. DII brought together major defense contractors to share best practices and to commit, in writing, to specific ethics standards. The DII commitments became the template for the broader corporate-ethics movement of the 1990s and 2000s.

### The mechanism: the post-Enron compliance regime

The 2001 Enron bankruptcy and the 2002 WorldCom accounting failures produced the largest single legislative response to corporate-ethics failure in U.S. history: the *Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002*, often abbreviated as SOX. The Act, signed by President George W. Bush in July 2002, imposed comprehensive new requirements on publicly-traded firms.

**SOX Section 302** requires the chief executive officer and chief financial officer to personally certify the accuracy of the firm's financial reports. The certification requirement creates personal criminal liability for executives who knowingly certify inaccurate reports.

**SOX Section 404** requires the firm to maintain documented internal controls over financial reporting and to obtain an independent auditor's attestation that the controls are operating effectively. The compliance cost of Section 404, particularly for small public companies, has been substantial; subsequent legislation (the Jumpstart Our Business Startups Act of 2012) has provided partial relief for smaller firms.

**SOX Section 806** prohibits retaliation against whistleblowers in publicly-traded firms and provides specific remedies (reinstatement, back pay, special damages) for employees who experience retaliation.

**SOX Section 906** establishes criminal penalties — up to ten years of imprisonment, up to twenty years for willful violations — for executives who knowingly certify false financial reports.

The post-SOX compliance regime has, on the available evidence, substantially reduced the incidence of the specific kinds of accounting fraud the Enron and WorldCom failures exemplified. It has not eliminated corporate-ethics failures more broadly; the post-SOX era has produced its own set of failures, in different categories (Wells Fargo's account-creation scandal of 2016; Volkswagen's diesel emissions fraud of 2015; the ongoing opioid-industry liability under the various MDL settlements).

### The Dodd-Frank extensions

The 2010 Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act extended the post-SOX framework to financial-services-specific concerns. The Act's whistleblower provisions, administered by the SEC, have produced substantial recoveries — over $1.7 billion in awards to whistleblowers through 2024. The Act's *clawback* provisions require firms to recover executive compensation when subsequent restatement reveals that the original compensation was based on financial reports later found to be materially inaccurate.

## Concept 3: Specific contemporary categories of business-ethics failure

The contemporary business-ethics literature identifies several recurring categories of failure that are worth carrying as the chapter's working taxonomy.

**Conflicts of interest.** The classical category. An executive whose personal financial interest is misaligned with the firm's interest, and who acts on the personal interest at the firm's expense. The post-SOX regime has substantially tightened disclosure requirements; conflicts have not disappeared but they are more visible than they were.

**Insider trading.** The use of material non-public information for personal trading or for tipping others. The Rajat Gupta case (Goldman Sachs board, 2009 leak to Raj Rajaratnam, 2012 conviction, 2-year sentence and $5 million fine) is the standing recent example. The companion *Business Ethics* textbook treats Gupta in detail in its epilogue chapter.

**Bribery, including under the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act.** The FCPA of 1977 (substantially amended in 1988 and 1998) prohibits U.S. persons and U.S.-listed firms from paying or offering anything of value to foreign government officials to obtain or retain business. Recent FCPA settlements have included BHP Billiton (2014), GlaxoSmithKline (2014), and Telia (2017, $965 million for Uzbekistan bribery). The Act's enforcement is active and the penalties substantial.

**Accounting fraud.** The Enron-WorldCom category. Post-SOX enforcement has reduced the incidence of the most aggressive forms; subtler forms (revenue recognition timing, off-balance-sheet structures, related-party transactions inadequately disclosed) continue.

**Discrimination and harassment.** The post-2017 #MeToo wave produced a substantial wave of disclosures and settlements. The legal framework has been in place since the Civil Rights Act of 1964; the operative-culture failures the wave revealed had been longstanding.

**Environmental and product-safety violations.** The Volkswagen diesel emissions fraud (2015 disclosure, $30+ billion in fines and settlements), the BP Deepwater Horizon spill (2010, $65+ billion in total cost), the Boeing 737 MAX failures (2018-19, two crashes producing 346 deaths and substantial subsequent regulatory and financial consequences) — each illustrates the category.

**Data privacy and cybersecurity.** The Equifax breach of 2017 (147 million U.S. consumers exposed, $700 million settlement), the Facebook-Cambridge Analytica matter of 2018, the recurring ransomware-driven disruptions of major firms — the category has grown substantially in importance over the past decade.

### The structural conditions for ethics failure

Across the categories, the documented structural conditions that make ethics failure more likely are recognizable.

**Misaligned compensation incentives.** When senior executives are compensated primarily on short-term financial metrics (quarterly earnings, share-price performance over short horizons), they are more likely to make decisions that produce short-term metric improvements at the cost of longer-term ethical violations.

**Weak independent oversight.** Boards of directors that are insufficiently independent of management, audit committees with insufficient technical expertise, internal audit functions reporting to management rather than to the board — each is a structural condition for ethics failure.

**Founder-culture-bureaucracy paradox.** Rapidly-scaling firms with strong founder cultures often produce operative cultures that diverge from formal compliance structures (the Uber 2017 case is exemplary). The companion *Business Ethics* textbook treats this in Chapter 9.

**Industry-specific pressure structures.** Financial-services firms facing intense quarterly performance pressure, healthcare firms facing third-party-payer pricing pressure, defense contractors facing single-customer (Department of Defense) dependency — each industry's specific pressure structure produces specific failure modes that the firm's compliance regime should be designed to address.

## Integration: a single firm through the chapter's apparatus

A mid-sized publicly-traded U.S. firm — call it Westchester — operates in a regulated industry (specifically, it is a regional health-insurance carrier) with approximately 3,200 employees and approximately $1.8 billion in annual revenue. The firm's chief ethics officer has just delivered the annual compliance report to the audit committee. The report identifies several findings:

(1) Twelve whistleblower complaints filed during the year, of which seven were investigated and resolved; (2) two pending matters under outside-counsel investigation; (3) a documented gap between the firm's stated commitment to *fair handling of claim disputes* and the operative metrics that compensation incentivizes (specifically, claims-handlers are compensated on resolution speed, which can produce premature claim denials); (4) a recommendation that the firm restructure its compensation incentives to reduce the gap.

Apply the chapter's apparatus.

*Concept 1 — compliance vs. ethics.* The legal compliance question is settled — the firm is in compliance with applicable insurance regulation and labor law. The ethical question is alive — the operative incentive structure produces outcomes the firm's stated values would not endorse.

*Concept 2 — corporate governance.* The audit committee, the chief ethics officer, the third-party investigation framework, the whistleblower policy — the apparatus is functioning. The firm's compliance regime is identifying the gaps the operative culture is producing.

*Concept 3 — failure-mode taxonomy.* The case fits the *misaligned compensation incentives* category. The remedy is operational: restructure the compensation to reward fair-handling outcomes rather than (or alongside) resolution speed.

The integration verdict: the firm has the apparatus to identify the gap and to act on the recommendation. Whether the firm acts on it is the test of the operative culture rather than the formal apparatus. The chapter's framework predicts that the firm whose senior leadership endorses the recommendation produces a better long-run outcome than the firm whose senior leadership receives the report and shelves it.

The firms that operate at the floor produce, on the documented evidence, weaker long-run performance than the firms that operate visibly above it. The chapter has been one extended case for the *operate above the floor* posture; the companion *Business Ethics* textbook makes the case in much greater depth.
