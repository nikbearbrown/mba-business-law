# Chapter 8 — Warranties and Sales Contracts
*When the Deal Is Bigger Than the Document.*

*Source: OpenStax, Business Law I Essentials, sections 8.1–8.2, CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. Derivative use credits OpenStax and preserves noncommercial/share-alike terms.*

---

Here is a question that sounds simple until you press on it: when you say you sold something, what exactly did you promise?

Not the price. The price is easy — it is the number on the invoice. Not the goods themselves. The goods are the thing in the box. What I mean is something more slippery: what did you commit to about the nature of those goods? What did your words imply? What did the trade expect? What does the law supply even when you said nothing at all?

The answer, it turns out, is not simple, and it is not left to chance. It is governed by Article 2 of the Uniform Commercial Code — a body of law that essentially asks, every time goods change hands for money, a series of questions the parties often never thought to ask themselves.

---

## What Article 2 Actually Governs

The UCC was created in 1952 precisely because commercial activity had outrun the patchwork of state laws that governed it. Every state had its own rules, and interstate commerce was choking on the inconsistency. The Code harmonized the most important rules and has since been adopted, with modifications, in all fifty states.

Article 2 of the Code governs transactions in *goods* — and this word matters more than it seems. Goods, under Section 2-105, are things that are movable and tangible at the time they are identified to the contract. A refrigerator is a good. A consulting engagement is not — it is a service, and Article 2 does not generally govern it. Land is not a good — it is realty. These distinctions matter because different legal regimes apply to each.

Where it gets interesting is at the edges. A cell phone tower looks like it might be realty — it is bolted to the ground — but courts have held it to be a good because it can be detached and moved. Soil and clay are goods even when they are part of immovable land, because they can be extracted. Growing crops are goods. Software licenses have been treated variously as goods, as mixed goods-and-services, and as pure services, depending on the facts and the jurisdiction. The line is not always clean, and when a transaction mixes goods with services, courts typically ask which predominates: is this mainly a sale of a thing, or mainly the engagement of a skill?

Article 2 also draws a distinction between merchants and non-merchants that runs through almost everything. A merchant is someone who regularly deals in goods of the kind being sold, or who holds themselves out as having expertise in those goods. The distinction matters because merchants are held to a higher standard — the Code imposes obligations on them that do not apply to casual sellers — and because several of the most important warranty rules apply only when the seller is a merchant.

---

## The Obligation Underneath the Transaction

Before getting to warranties specifically, there is a foundational obligation that Article 2 places on every contract: good faith. Under UCC 1-304 (formerly 1-203 in older editions), every contract or duty under the Code imposes an obligation of good faith in its performance and enforcement. For merchants, the Code adds commercial reasonableness — a requirement to behave in the sensible, prudent way that trade standards would expect.

Good faith is not just a platitude. It limits opportunistic behavior: manufactured surprise, bad-faith rejection of conforming goods, sham demands under requirements contracts, unreasonable cover. It is the legal system's way of saying that the parties are bound not merely by the words of the deal but by its spirit. A seller who technically complied with a purchase order while deliberately delivering goods the buyer could not use has not necessarily satisfied the obligation.

---

## What You Said: Express Warranties

Now we arrive at the heart of the matter. An express warranty, governed by UCC 2-313, is exactly what the name implies: a warranty you actually made. The seller either affirmed a fact, made a promise, described the goods, showed a sample, or produced a model — and that assertion became part of the basis of the bargain.

Notice what the Code does not require. It does not require the word "warranty" or "guarantee." It does not require a formal document. It does not even require the seller to intend to create a warranty. What it requires is that the seller made a factual commitment, and the buyer relied on it in entering the transaction.

<!-- → [TABLE: Examples of express warranty language — two columns: statement and whether it creates a warranty (yes/no/depends) — rows: "wrinkle-free shirt," "lifetime guarantee," "Made in the USA," "not from concentrate," "24k gold," "best sandwich in the world," technical performance specification] -->

This is where business people sometimes get into trouble without realizing it. The express warranty is not just what is written in the document labeled "Warranty." It is in the product label. It is in the sales email. It is in the demo. It is in the product data sheet. It is in the catalog description. It is in the sample you passed across the table. Every factual claim your organization makes about the goods is potentially part of the legal architecture of the sale.

The Code does draw one important line: puffery. Sellers are allowed to engage in promotional enthusiasm, and buyers are expected to apply common sense to what they hear. "Best in the world" is puffery. "Guaranteed to improve your marathon time by twenty percent" is likely a warranty. The test is whether the statement was a specific, verifiable factual claim, or whether it was the kind of extravagant boasting that no reasonable buyer would treat as a literal commitment.

When an express warranty is breached — when what was delivered does not match what was said — the seller's defenses are narrow. It does not matter that the seller believed the warranty was true. It does not matter that the seller exercised care in producing the goods. It does not matter that there was no reason to think the warranty was false. The seller made a commitment, the goods did not satisfy it, and the Code gives the buyer remedies.

One more important point: a seller generally cannot make a specific factual promise and then erase it with contradictory boilerplate. UCC 2-316 governs disclaimers, and it tries to read warranty language and disclaimer language consistently where possible. Where they cannot be reconciled, the disclaimer often gives way. Standard terms cannot always rescue an overpromising sales process.

## What the Trade Expects: Implied Warranty of Merchantability

Now consider a different scenario. No claims were made. No warranties were given. The seller simply sold the goods. Does the buyer have any protection?

Yes — if the seller is a merchant with respect to goods of that kind. Under UCC 2-314, every sale by such a merchant carries an implied warranty of merchantability. The goods must be fit for the ordinary purposes for which goods of that kind are used.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: What "merchantable" means — a simple visual showing the warranty as automatic, arising from the merchant relationship and the sale itself, not from any statement; contrasted with express warranty arising from affirmation] -->

The implied warranty of merchantability is not a guarantee of perfection. It asks a specific question: can this thing do the ordinary job that goods like this are supposed to do? A commercial freezer must hold temperature. A wrench must turn bolts without snapping. A jacket must provide the warmth and protection appropriate to garments of its type. The standard is calibrated to what the trade expects of goods sold under that description — not to what the buyer hoped for, but to what any reasonable person would expect.

Used goods carry this warranty too, though the expected standard adjusts to the condition appropriate for used goods of that kind. Food and drink sold commercially carries the implied warranty of being fit for human consumption — no express promise needed.

If a buyer believes this warranty has been breached, the burden falls on them to show three things: the good was defective, that defect made the good unfit for ordinary use, and the defect caused the harm. Typical defects include design flaws, manufacturing flaws, and inadequate instructions or warnings.

Merchantability warranties can be disclaimed, but the Code is strict about how. A disclaimer of merchantability must use the word "merchantability." If it is in writing, it must be conspicuous — not buried in unreadable small print. "As is" or "with all faults" language can also exclude implied warranties, provided it is presented in a way that reasonably alerts the buyer to what they are giving up.

---

## What the Buyer Told You: Implied Warranty of Fitness for a Particular Purpose

There is a third warranty, different from merchantability, and its logic is worth understanding precisely because it is easy to confuse the two.

Under UCC 2-315, if a seller has reason to know that the buyer has a particular purpose in mind for the goods, and if the buyer is relying on the seller's skill or judgment to select the right goods for that purpose, then the seller warrants that the goods will be fit for that particular purpose.

The key distinction: merchantability is about ordinary use. Fitness for a particular purpose is about special use plus reliance. A pair of boots must be fit for ordinary wear — that is merchantability. If the buyer tells the salesperson that they need boots for underwater cave diving, and they rely on the salesperson's recommendation, and the salesperson selects and recommends a specific model — that is fitness for a particular purpose.

<!-- → [TABLE: Merchantability vs. fitness for a particular purpose — side-by-side comparison — rows: trigger, UCC section, who must be a merchant, what the buyer must show, disclaimer requirements, ordinary example, special-use example] -->

Three conditions must all be present: the seller must have reason to know the buyer's particular purpose, the seller must have reason to know the buyer is relying on the seller's skill or judgment, and the buyer must actually rely. A buyer who specifies exact technical requirements and essentially designs the goods may have difficulty establishing reliance on the seller's judgment — though this does not automatically eliminate other warranties.

Fitness disclaimers must be in writing and conspicuous.

---

## What Comes With the Deal: Warranty of Title

There is one more warranty built into every sale, and it operates differently from the others. Under UCC 2-312, by the mere act of selling goods, the seller warrants that the title is good, that the transfer is lawful, and that the goods are free from undisclosed liens or security interests the buyer did not know about.

Merchant sellers who regularly deal in goods of a particular kind add a further warranty: that the goods are free from rightful claims of patent, trademark, or other intellectual property infringement — unless the buyer supplied the specifications that caused the infringement problem.

Modern transactions make this warranty consequential in ways earlier commercial law did not anticipate. Equipment resold under an undisclosed security interest, counterfeit branded goods, gray-market inventory, a custom part manufactured from buyer-supplied drawings that infringe a patent — these are all title and infringement problems. The buyer who purchases without knowing about the encumbrance is protected, but the legal machinery can take time to unwind.

## When the Goods Fail: Buyer Remedies Under Article 2

Warranties create obligations. Breached obligations need remedies. Article 2 gives buyers a toolkit.

The most direct remedy is cancellation. Under UCC 2-711, a buyer who receives nonconforming goods can cancel the contract and demand return of any price paid, while also pursuing further damages. The key word is "nonconforming" — the goods must actually fail to meet the contract's requirements.

<!-- → [TABLE: Buyer remedies under Article 2 — columns: remedy, UCC section, when it applies — rows: cancel contract, cover, market-price damages, damages for accepted goods, incidental and consequential damages, specific performance] -->

Cover, under UCC 2-712, allows the buyer to go buy substitute goods from another source and charge the seller for the difference in price. Cover must be reasonable, must happen without unreasonable delay, and must be done in good faith. The buyer cannot engineer an inflated replacement purchase and then bill the original seller for the premium.

If the goods are unique — or if a legal remedy would simply be inadequate — a court can order specific performance under UCC 2-716: the seller must actually deliver the thing they promised. This is uncommon in commercial transactions involving fungible goods, but it matters for specialized equipment, unique items, or situations where the market has no real substitute.

Consequential damages — losses flowing from the breach that were foreseeable when the contract was made — are available under UCC 2-715. A buyer whose spoiled inventory losses were foreseeable at the time of contracting can recover those losses, not merely the purchase price of the defective goods.

One caution about punitive damages: they are not a standard Article 2 remedy. Ordinary breach of a sales contract does not generate punitive damages. They can arise if there is fraud, tortious conduct, bad faith in a special context, or some independent statutory basis — but the default rule is compensatory, not punitive.

## The International Dimension

One final boundary worth marking: when the sale crosses borders, the governing law may shift. The United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods — the CISG — is the primary legal framework for international commercial transactions between parties in signatory countries. It broadly covers similar ground to the UCC but preempts it when an international sale falls within its scope. Business operating across borders should not assume UCC rules apply by default.

---

## The Manager's Lesson

What this chapter amounts to, from a business perspective, is the following: the legal obligations in a sale of goods do not begin and end with the written contract. They begin with every factual claim anyone in your organization makes about the goods — in ads, emails, labels, demos, samples, data sheets, and conversations. They are supplied automatically by the Code when you are a merchant, even if you said nothing. They follow from the buyer's disclosed purposes when you help them select the goods. And they attach to the title itself, guaranteeing that what you sold is actually yours to sell.

The risk architecture of a transaction is larger than the four corners of the purchase order. Knowing that is the beginning of managing it.

## Exercises

### Warm-Up

**1.** A hardware store sells a claw hammer. No statements are made about it during the sale. The head separates from the handle on first use, injuring the buyer's hand. The store regularly sells tools of this kind. Which warranty, if any, has been breached? What must the buyer demonstrate to prevail?

**2.** A seller's product page states: "Our industrial pump delivers 200 GPM at 40 PSI — certified for continuous duty cycle." The buyer purchases the pump, which delivers only 140 GPM under identical conditions. Identify the type of warranty created and explain why it applies regardless of whether a separate warranty document exists.

**3.** A used-equipment dealer sells a commercial espresso machine described as "operational." It breaks down within a week. Does the implied warranty of merchantability apply? How, if at all, does the used-goods context change the standard?

---

### Application

**4.** A restaurant equipment supplier tells a buyer over the phone: "This walk-in cooler is perfect for storing dairy — it'll hold 34–38°F without issue." The written invoice contains a clause stating: "Seller makes no warranties, express or implied." The cooler consistently runs at 45°F, spoiling the buyer's dairy inventory. Analyze whether the phone statement created an express warranty and whether the invoice disclaimer can negate it under UCC 2-316.

**5.** A building contractor tells a plumbing supply house that she needs pipe fittings rated for high-pressure steam lines in an industrial retrofit. The supply house selects and recommends a specific product. The fittings fail under pressure, causing damage. Identify which warranty or warranties are potentially at issue. What must the contractor prove to establish the implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose?

**6.** A company purchases 200 branded promotional items from a vendor. The items turn out to be counterfeit — the brand's trademark is reproduced without authorization, and the brand owner sends a cease-and-desist. What UCC warranty is implicated? Who bears the risk, and does it matter whether the buyer supplied the design?

---

### Synthesis

**7.** A small manufacturer orders custom-machined parts to specifications it provided. The parts are delivered but fail to meet dimensional tolerances. The seller argues: (a) the buyer designed the parts, so there is no warranty of fitness for a particular purpose; (b) the invoice disclaimed all implied warranties. Evaluate both defenses. Does either succeed? Are there remaining warranty theories the buyer can still pursue?

**8.** Return to the MBA classroom scenario from the chapter: a restaurant buys a used commercial freezer from a restaurant-equipment dealer. The salesperson says, "It has been tested and is ready for commercial kitchen use." The invoice includes a small-print disclaimer of all warranties. The freezer fails after two days, ruining $8,000 of inventory. Apply the full warranty analysis: express warranty, implied warranty of merchantability (including the used-goods calibration), disclaimer validity under UCC 2-316, and available remedies including whether the spoiled inventory qualifies as consequential damages.

---

### Challenge

**9.** A software company licenses a machine-vision system to a manufacturer for inspecting circuit boards. The system is delivered as firmware embedded in a physical device. The manufacturer claims the system fails to detect a class of defects it was marketed as able to catch, causing $600,000 in downstream product recalls. The seller argues Article 2 does not apply because software is a service, not a good. Construct the strongest argument on each side. What factors would a court weigh under a predominant-purpose analysis? If Article 2 does apply, identify the relevant warranties and remedies.

**10.** A buyer and seller negotiate a purchase order for 1,000 industrial valves. The purchase order is silent on warranties. After the order is signed, the seller emails its standard terms, which include: (a) a disclaimer of all implied warranties, (b) a limitation of remedies to repair or replacement, and (c) an exclusion of consequential damages. Sixty valves fail, causing a production shutdown costing the buyer $250,000 in lost output. Analyze whether the post-order standard terms are part of the contract under UCC 2-207, whether the disclaimer and remedy limitations are effective, and whether the consequential-damage exclusion can survive if the limited remedy fails of its essential purpose.

## LLM Exercise

A small electronics manufacturer orders 500 specialized circuit boards for $42,000. The purchase order states that the boards must meet IPC-A-610 Class 3 standards. The supplier ships 480 boards, and 60 fail incoming inspection. After the purchase order but before shipment, the supplier emailed standard terms disclaiming all implied warranties and limiting remedies to repair or replacement at the supplier's option.

Apply UCC Article 2. Identify express warranties, implied warranties, disclaimer issues, battle-of-the-forms issues, buyer notice, and available remedies.

**Evaluation rubric:**

- Does the model identify the product specification as a likely express warranty?
- Does it distinguish merchantability from fitness for a particular purpose?
- Does it analyze whether the disclaimer is conspicuous and whether it can negate express warranties?
- Does it flag UCC 2-207 if the standard terms arrived after the purchase order?
- Does it identify rejection, acceptance with damages, cover, incidental damages, and consequential damages?
- Does it avoid treating every product failure as strict product liability?
