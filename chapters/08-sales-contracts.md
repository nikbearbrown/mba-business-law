# Sales Contracts

In 1952, the U.S. legal community completed work on the first version of what is now the most important commercial-law statute in the United States: the *Uniform Commercial Code* (UCC). The UCC was a project of the *American Law Institute* and the *National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws*, and its purpose was to bring uniform rules to commercial transactions across the fifty U.S. states. The drafting was led primarily by Karl Llewellyn of Columbia Law School. The UCC has been adopted, with state-specific variations, in every U.S. state (with the partial exception of Louisiana, which has adopted some articles but not others).

The UCC contains nine substantive articles. Article 2 — *Sales* — governs the sale of *goods* (movable, tangible items) between buyers and sellers. Article 2 is the framework inside which the bulk of U.S. commercial transactions are now conducted; it modifies the common-law rules of contract in ways calibrated to the realities of commercial practice between merchants.

This chapter is the operating manual for UCC Article 2 as it applies to sales contracts in U.S. commerce.

## Why this chapter matters

Sales of goods constitute the bulk of U.S. commercial transaction volume by dollar value. The UCC Article 2 framework governs them. Practitioners involved in any meaningful volume of goods transactions — manufacturers, wholesalers, retailers, distributors, consumers — operate inside the framework whether they realize it or not.

## Concept 1: The scope of UCC Article 2 — what counts as *goods*

UCC § 2-105 defines *goods* as *all things... which are movable at the time of identification to the contract for sale, other than the money in which the price is to be paid, investment securities, and things in action*. The definition includes manufactured products, agricultural commodities, livestock, vehicles, equipment, and any other tangible movable item.

The definition does *not* include real estate (governed by separate state real-property law), services (governed by common-law contract doctrine), intellectual property (governed by federal IP statutes and state contract doctrine), or financial instruments (governed by other UCC articles).

### Mixed contracts and the predominant purpose test

Many contemporary contracts include both goods and services components. A heating-system installation contract, for example, includes goods (the heating equipment) and services (the installation labor). The applicable framework — UCC Article 2 or common-law contract — depends on the *predominant purpose* test: is the transaction predominantly for goods (with services incidental) or predominantly for services (with goods incidental)?

The test is fact-specific. Courts look at the relative dollar values of the two components, the language of the contract, the way the transaction was negotiated, and the nature of the underlying transaction. The doctrine is articulated in *Bonebrake v. Cox* (1974) and has been widely followed.

### Merchant vs. non-merchant transactions

UCC Article 2 distinguishes between transactions involving *merchants* (parties who deal in goods of the kind being sold or who hold themselves out as having particular knowledge of the relevant trade) and transactions involving non-merchants. Several Article 2 provisions impose stricter requirements on merchants — including the *firm offer* rule under UCC § 2-205, which requires a merchant's signed written offer to remain open for the time stated (or for a reasonable time, up to three months), even without consideration for the option.

## Concept 2: Battle of the forms and UCC § 2-207

In modern commercial practice, sellers and buyers typically exchange standardized forms — purchase orders from buyers, sales acknowledgments from sellers — that contain detailed terms drafted in advance for repeated use. The forms often contain conflicting terms. Under common-law mirror-image doctrine, no contract would be formed: each form would be a counter-offer, and no acceptance would match the original offer's terms exactly. The result would have been a substantial part of U.S. commerce operating without binding contracts.

UCC § 2-207 modifies the mirror-image rule for sales of goods. The provision is one of the most-litigated in U.S. commercial law and one of the most operationally consequential.

### The mechanism

Under § 2-207(1), an *expression of acceptance* operates as an acceptance even if it states terms different from or additional to the offer, *unless* acceptance is expressly made conditional on assent to the new terms. Under § 2-207(2), additional terms in the acceptance become part of the contract between merchants if the additional terms (a) do not materially alter the offer, (b) are not objected to within a reasonable time, and (c) are not objected to in advance. Under § 2-207(3), if the writings do not establish a contract, performance by both parties — shipping and accepting the goods — establishes a contract on the terms on which the writings agree, plus default UCC terms for any matters on which the writings disagree.

The provision is intentionally complex. Its operational effect is that contracts get formed in commercial-form-exchange contexts even when the forms do not match exactly, and the specific terms that govern depend on the specific facts of the form exchange.

### Worked example

A buyer sends a purchase order specifying delivery within 30 days, payment within 60 days of delivery, no warranties beyond the seller's standard terms. The seller sends a sales acknowledgment specifying delivery within 45 days, payment within 30 days of delivery, an express warranty disclaimer, and a forum-selection clause requiring litigation in the seller's home state. The seller ships the goods. The buyer accepts and pays.

Under § 2-207(1), the seller's acknowledgment operates as an acceptance because it does not expressly condition acceptance on assent to its different terms. Under § 2-207(2), the additional terms (the warranty disclaimer, the forum-selection clause) are *between merchants* — both parties. Whether they become part of the contract depends on whether they materially alter the offer. The warranty disclaimer typically does materially alter and is excluded. The forum-selection clause is fact-specific. The delivery and payment timing provisions, where the writings disagree, are resolved by reference to default UCC rules and reasonable commercial practice.

The chapter's framework: § 2-207 produces enforceable contracts in commercial-form contexts but produces those contracts on terms that may differ from what either party intended. The practitioner's discipline is to read the forms before signing them.

## Concept 3: Warranties, risk of loss, and remedies

UCC Article 2 creates several warranties that, by default, accompany sales of goods. The warranties can be disclaimed under specified conditions; absent effective disclaimer, they apply.

**Express warranty** (§ 2-313). Any affirmation of fact or promise made by the seller relating to the goods becomes part of the basis of the bargain and creates an express warranty. *Mere puffery* (subjective opinions like "this is the best product on the market") does not create a warranty; statements of fact ("this engine produces 250 horsepower") do.

**Implied warranty of merchantability** (§ 2-314). When the seller is a merchant in goods of the kind, the goods sold are warranted to be merchantable — fit for ordinary purposes, adequately packaged and labeled, meeting industry standards. The warranty is implied unless effectively disclaimed.

**Implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose** (§ 2-315). When the seller has reason to know the buyer's particular purpose for the goods and that the buyer is relying on the seller's skill and judgment, the goods are warranted to be fit for that particular purpose. The warranty is implied unless effectively disclaimed.

**Disclaimer.** Implied warranties can be disclaimed under § 2-316. Disclaimer of merchantability requires the term *merchantability* be conspicuously stated. Disclaimer of fitness for particular purpose requires conspicuous written disclaimer. The phrase *as is* effectively disclaims most implied warranties. Disclaimers in standard-form consumer contracts are subject to additional unconscionability scrutiny.

### Risk of loss

UCC § 2-509 allocates the *risk of loss* — the risk that the goods will be damaged or destroyed in transit — between buyer and seller. The default allocations:

- **Shipment contract** (seller required to ship but not to deliver to a particular destination): risk passes to buyer when goods are delivered to the carrier.
- **Destination contract** (seller required to deliver to a particular destination): risk passes to buyer when goods are tendered at destination.
- **Bailee transactions** (goods held by a third-party warehouse): risk passes to buyer on transfer of negotiable document of title.

The default allocations can be modified by contract. The shipment-vs-destination distinction is often determined by the shipping terms specified in the contract — *FOB origin* (shipment) vs. *FOB destination* (destination), under common Incoterms-influenced practice.

### Remedies for breach

UCC Article 2 provides specific remedies for breach by either party.

*Buyer's remedies for seller's breach:* cover (purchase substitute goods and recover the price differential), reject the goods, accept and recover damages, recover incidental and consequential damages, specific performance for unique goods.

*Seller's remedies for buyer's breach:* withhold delivery, resell the goods, recover the contract price for goods accepted but not paid for, recover damages for non-acceptance, cancel the contract.

The remedies are calibrated to make the non-breaching party whole. Consequential damages — losses to the non-breaching party from the breach beyond the immediate transaction — are recoverable when the breaching party reasonably should have foreseen them at the time of contracting.

## Integration: a sales transaction through the framework

A small manufacturer — call it Caraway — orders a batch of specialized industrial sensors from a supplier (call it Northbeam) for incorporation into Caraway's products. The contract specifies delivery in 30 days, FOB Northbeam's facility (shipment contract). The sensors arrive on time but, on inspection, fail Caraway's incoming-quality testing. Caraway notifies Northbeam and rejects the shipment.

Apply the chapter's framework.

*UCC Article 2 governs* — sale of goods between merchants.

*Implied warranty of merchantability applies* unless effectively disclaimed. The contract did not contain an effective disclaimer (Caraway should check the contract terms carefully). The defective sensors breach the warranty.

*Risk of loss* — under FOB origin terms, risk passed to Caraway when the sensors were delivered to the carrier. Caraway bears the risk during transit, but the breach of warranty is independent of the risk-of-loss allocation.

*Remedies* — Caraway can reject the goods (which it has done), recover the contract price plus incidental damages (cost of inspection and return), and pursue cover (purchase substitute sensors elsewhere and recover the price differential from Northbeam). If Caraway's downstream production is delayed because of the defective sensors, consequential damages may be available if reasonably foreseeable.

The integration verdict: the framework produces the structured analysis. The remedy is operational — prompt notification of rejection, careful documentation, qualified counsel for any claim.

## Chapter summary

You can now do five things you could not do before. You can distinguish UCC Article 2 contracts from common-law contracts. You can apply UCC § 2-207 to commercial form-exchange transactions. You can evaluate implied warranties and the conditions for disclaimer. You can analyze the available remedies for buyer's and seller's breach. And you can identify risk-of-loss allocations under specific shipping arrangements.

The single idea that matters most is that *the UCC is the operating system of U.S. commercial transactions for goods*. The reader who knows the UCC operates with substantially more precision in goods-transaction work than the reader who relies on common-law contract intuitions.

You should now be able to teach UCC § 2-207 and the implied-warranty framework to a colleague who has not read this chapter, in fifteen minutes.
