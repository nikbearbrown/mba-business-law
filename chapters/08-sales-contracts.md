# Chapter 8 — Contract Law
*Why the law enforces some promises and ignores the rest.*

---

Here is the puzzle I want you to sit with before we get into any of the machinery.

In November 1891, a London company called the Carbolic Smoke Ball Company placed an advertisement in several British newspapers. The advertisement offered £100 — roughly £15,000 in today's money — to anyone who used their smoke-ball product as directed for two weeks and then contracted influenza. To signal seriousness, the company announced it had deposited £1,000 in a specific bank against such claims. You could walk past the bank. The money was there.

A woman named Louisa Carlill took the advertisement at face value. She bought the smoke ball, used it as directed, and contracted influenza. She wrote to the company requesting her £100. The company declined. Their position: the advertisement was promotional puffery, a flourish of advertising language, not a binding commitment. No reasonable person would treat a newspaper advertisement as a legal offer.

Mrs. Carlill sued. The company's lawyers were creative. There was no offer to a specific person. The terms were too vague. An offer to the whole world is not an offer at all. A contract requires two parties, and the company had not contracted with every reader of a newspaper.

The English Court of Appeal, in 1893, rejected every argument. The £1,000 deposit was not puffery — it was a visible, verifiable demonstration of intent. The terms were definite: use the product as specified, contract influenza, collect £100. Mrs. Carlill had accepted by performing the conditions. She had given consideration by purchasing the product and complying with the terms. The contract was valid. The company owed £100.

The case has been taught in law schools in every common-law jurisdiction for 130 years. Not because it is dramatic — though it is, a little — but because it forces you to confront the basic question: what is a contract, what makes a promise binding, and why does the law enforce some agreements and ignore the rest?

That question has a clean answer, and the answer has structure. Let me show you the structure.

---

People make promises constantly. Almost none of them are contracts. Your friend promises to meet you for lunch; if she doesn't show up, you have no legal claim. Your employer promises to promote you someday; if the promotion never materializes, you generally cannot sue. The legal system does not enforce all promises. It enforces a specific subset, and the five requirements that identify that subset are not arbitrary — each one is doing a job.

The first requirement is an *offer*: one party must make a proposal demonstrating serious intent to enter an agreement. Seriousness is the operative word, and it is what the Carbolic company tried to defeat. Jokes are not offers. Advertisements are generally not offers — they are invitations to negotiate, preliminary signals of interest. But an advertisement specific enough in its terms, backed by a visible commitment, can cross the line. The court's reasoning was that no company deposits £1,000 in a bank as a joke. The deposit was evidence of intent, and intent is what the law is actually looking for.

The offer must also be definite. A promise to pay "a reasonable amount" for work performed occupies uncertain legal ground; a promise to pay "$75,000 for the following services, delivered by the following date" is clear enough that a court can evaluate whether performance occurred and calculate the remedy if it didn't. The definiteness requirement is practical: courts need to know what was promised before they can determine whether it was delivered.

The second requirement is *acceptance*: the offeree must agree to be bound by the offer's terms, without modification. This is the mirror-image rule. If you offer to sell me a car for $20,000 and I say I'll take it for $18,000, I haven't accepted — I've made a counter-offer, and your original offer is extinguished. If you walk away, I cannot come back and accept the $20,000 offer. It no longer exists. Acceptance that adds or changes terms is rejection plus counter-offer, and the original offer dies.

The mirror-image rule is strict under common-law contract doctrine. The Uniform Commercial Code, which governs sales of goods between merchants, modifies it — the exercises at the end of this chapter work through the UCC in detail. But for services, real estate, employment, and most contracts not involving the sale of goods, the rule applies precisely: acceptance must match the offer.

The third requirement is *consideration*: each party must give something of value. The something need not be equal — courts do not generally ask whether the deal was a good one for both sides. What consideration requires is mutuality: both parties must be getting something, and both parties must be giving something. A promise to make a gift is unenforceable precisely because the gift-giver is giving something and getting nothing in return. The law treats exchange promises differently from gift promises, and consideration is the mechanism that draws the line between them.

Notice what this means for Mrs. Carlill. The Carbolic company argued she gave no consideration — she just used the smoke ball and got sick. The court disagreed. She purchased the product. She used it as directed, which required her effort and compliance. Both counted. She gave something; the company promised something in return. Consideration satisfied.

The fourth requirement is *capacity*: both parties must have the legal ability to enter a contract. Minors — generally those under 18 — lack full capacity, with specific exceptions for necessities like food, clothing, and shelter. People who are mentally incompetent lack capacity. People who were substantially intoxicated at the moment of contracting may lack capacity. A contract signed with someone who lacked capacity is *voidable* at the option of the party who lacked it. The minor can walk away; the adult party generally cannot. This asymmetry is deliberate: the capacity doctrine protects people who may not fully understand what they are agreeing to.

The fifth requirement is *legality*: the contract's purpose must be legal. A contract to commit a crime is unenforceable. A non-compete clause so broad that it prevents someone from ever working in their field again may be unenforceable as against public policy. Courts will sometimes sever the illegal portions and enforce the rest; sometimes the illegality defeats the whole contract.

Run Mrs. Carlill through all five. Offer: the advertisement was a definite, serious proposal backed by a £1,000 deposit. Acceptance: she performed the advertised conditions. Consideration: she purchased the product and complied with the directions; the company promised £100. Capacity: no issue raised. Legality: no issue raised. All five satisfied. Contract enforceable. The company pays.

---

But the five elements establish only formal validity. A contract that satisfies all five may still be invalid if something was wrong with the quality of the agreement — if one party's consent was obtained by deception, pressure, or mistake.

*Fraud* voids consent entirely. If one party intentionally misrepresents a material fact — a fact the other party would have found important — knowing the representation is false, intending reliance, and the other party relies and is harmed, the contract is voidable at the defrauded party's option. Voidable means the injured party can choose to rescind it. The defrauding party cannot rescind on the basis of their own fraud.

*Innocent misrepresentation* works similarly but without the intent element. If a statement was made honestly but turned out to be false, and the other party relied on it, rescission may still be available even though nobody was lying. The remedies are typically narrower than in fraud.

*Mutual mistake* is the case where both parties are wrong about the same thing. The classic case involves a cow that both parties believed to be infertile; it turns out the cow is pregnant, which substantially changes its value. Neither party lied. Both were wrong about the basic substance of what they were exchanging. When the mistake goes to the essence of the agreement, the contract may be voidable by either party.

*Unilateral mistake* — only one party is wrong — generally does not produce voidability. You took a risk when you entered the contract; that you miscalculated does not entitle you to escape. The narrow exception is when the other party knew or should have known of your mistake and said nothing. When the other party exploited your error, the equities shift.

*Duress* means agreement obtained by threat. Physical threats are the obvious case. Economic duress — I will cut off your essential supply unless you agree to new terms, and you have no reasonable alternative — is harder to establish but recognized. The key question is whether the threatened party had a genuine choice. If they did, the agreement stands. If they genuinely had no reasonable alternative, duress may void it.

*Undue influence* is improper pressure by a party in a position of trust or dominance — a caretaker, a financial advisor, a lawyer who uses that position to extract an agreement the other party would not have freely made. The mechanism: the dominated party's judgment is substituted by the dominant party's will.

And then there is the broader backstop: *unconscionability*. A contract whose terms are so one-sided that no reasonable person in possession of their faculties would have agreed to them can be refused enforcement, especially when lopsidedness was accompanied by procedural unfairness — take-it-or-leave-it form contracts, incomprehensible fine print, a party with no meaningful bargaining leverage. The doctrine is applied most often in consumer contracts. Between sophisticated commercial parties, courts rarely find unconscionability, on the theory that people who know what they are doing are held to what they agreed.

---

There is a problem with the consideration requirement that I want to spend a moment on, because it illustrates something important about how contract law actually operates.

Suppose a company recruits you aggressively, extends a job offer, and tells you to start in sixty days. You turn down a competing offer. You give notice to your current employer. You sell your house in Cincinnati and buy an apartment in Boston near the new office. Sixty days later, the company calls and says the position is eliminated.

Under strict consideration analysis, your position is uncomfortable. The employment relationship hadn't started. Under many states' employment-at-will doctrine, even a signed offer letter may not create an enforceable promise beyond the first day of employment. The formal consideration structure may not help you.

But you sold your house. You turned down a competing offer. You bought an apartment. You did all of this because of a promise.

The doctrine of *promissory estoppel* was built for exactly this situation. The rule: a promise that a reasonable person should expect to induce significant action or forbearance in another party, and that does induce it, is enforceable to the extent necessary to prevent injustice — even without consideration.

The elements are fact-specific: a promise was made; reliance on the promise was reasonable; the reliance produced action that would not otherwise have occurred; the relying party suffered real, measurable harm; and allowing the promisor to walk away without consequences would be unjust. The recovery under promissory estoppel is typically *reliance damages* — what you lost by relying on the promise — rather than *expectation damages* — what you would have gained if the promise had been kept.

In the job example: the company is probably not liable for your full expected salary under the employment agreement. That is expectation damages, and the contract may not have been binding in that way. The company is probably liable for your actual out-of-pocket losses from relying on the promise — the apartment costs, the value of the competing offer you turned down, the differential costs of the relocation. Promissory estoppel closes the gap between the strict consideration doctrine and what fairness requires.

---

A contract is a promise about the future. The future doesn't always cooperate. When a party fails to perform as promised, the law provides remedies calibrated not to punish the breaching party but to restore the injured party to an appropriate position.

*Expectation damages* are the standard remedy. The goal is to place the non-breaching party in the position it would have been in if the contract had been fully performed. If you contracted to buy equipment for $50,000 and the seller delivers nothing, and you have to buy the same equipment elsewhere for $65,000, your expectation damages are $15,000 — the gap between what you were promised and what you had to pay to get it elsewhere.

Expectation damages also include *consequential damages* — losses that flow from the breach beyond the direct cost differential, when those losses were foreseeable at the time of contracting. The rule from *Hadley v. Baxendale* (1854), still good law today: a breaching party is liable for losses that arise naturally from the breach, and for losses that were within the reasonable contemplation of both parties when the contract was made. Unusual downstream losses that neither party could have anticipated are not recoverable. The breaching party is responsible for foreseeable harm, not for every conceivable chain of consequences.

*Reliance damages* are smaller. The goal is to restore the non-breaching party to the position it was in before the contract was made. You recover what you actually spent in reliance, not what you expected to gain. Reliance damages appear most often in promissory estoppel cases and in cases where expectation damages are too speculative to calculate.

*Restitution* addresses unjust enrichment. If you paid money under a contract that turned out to be unenforceable, or if you partially performed before the other party breached, restitution requires the breaching party to return any benefit they received. The principle: you should not be allowed to keep what you received under an agreement you refused to honor.

*Specific performance* is the exceptional remedy — a court order directing the breaching party to actually perform. Courts are reluctant to issue such orders because they require ongoing judicial supervision and because, in most commercial contexts, money damages are adequate. The exceptions: real estate, because each piece of land is unique and money is not a perfect substitute, and contracts for genuinely unique goods or services. You cannot sue for specific performance on a contract for standard-grade wheat; you can, arguably, sue for specific performance on a contract for a specific antique.

*Liquidated damages* are damages the parties specified in advance, in the contract itself, for the event of breach. They are enforceable when they represent a reasonable forecast of likely actual losses at the time of contracting. They are unenforceable when they are so disproportionate to likely actual losses that they function as penalties — ways to punish breach rather than compensate for it. The distinction between a reasonable forecast and a penalty is, predictably, sometimes litigated.

One principle cuts across all of these: the *duty to mitigate*. A non-breaching party cannot sit back and let damages accumulate. Once breach occurs, the injured party is required to take reasonable steps to reduce the loss. The rule does not require heroic effort — only reasonable steps. But it means damages are measured from what the injured party suffered after taking reasonable steps, not from what they chose to suffer by doing nothing.

---

Stand back from the machinery for a moment and look at what it is actually doing.

Contract law, examined carefully, is not primarily about punishment. There is no criminal prosecution for breach of contract. The entire remedial apparatus — expectation damages, reliance damages, restitution — is calibrated to restoration, not retribution. The law's position is roughly this: if you breach a contract, you owe the other party whatever it takes to put them in the position your promise created, or, if that is not calculable, to put them back where they started. You owe them compensation for what your breach cost them. You generally do not owe them more.

This has an implication that is worth taking seriously. The law does not prohibit breach of contract. It prices it. If you can breach a contract, pay the other party's expectation damages, and still come out ahead — because the value of the performance to you fell below its cost — the law generally allows that. Economists call this *efficient breach*: it is sometimes more valuable for a resource to go somewhere other than where a contract directed it, and the damages system permits that reallocation while compensating the party who gave up their contractual expectation.

Whether this is morally satisfying depends on how you feel about promise-keeping as a matter of principle. What it means practically is that contract law is designed to make commercial activity reliable and predictable, not to guarantee that every promise is honored. The smoke-ball company had to pay Mrs. Carlill because the machinery said so — because the conditions of a valid contract were satisfied and she performed them. If the machinery had produced the opposite result — if advertisements could never be binding, if there were no way to make a credible public commitment — the world would contain fewer credible public commitments, and commercial life would be harder to navigate.

The doctrine's job is to create the conditions under which people can trust each other enough to transact. The five-element framework, with its genuine-agreement qualifications and its calibrated remedies, is the specific mechanism by which that job gets done. Mrs. Carlill's £100 is not just an old English judgment. It is the demonstration that when you make a promise clearly enough, visibly enough, and back it with enough evidence of serious intent, the legal system will hold you to it. That is the foundation on which every commercial agreement you have ever signed rests.
