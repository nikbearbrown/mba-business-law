# Chapter 7 — Contract Law
*Why the law enforces some promises and ignores the rest.*

In November 1891, a London company called the Carbolic Smoke Ball Company placed an advertisement in several British newspapers. The advertisement offered £100 — a substantial sum, roughly £15,000 in today's money — to anyone who used their smoke-ball product as directed for two weeks and then contracted influenza. To demonstrate sincerity, the company announced it had deposited £1,000 in a specific bank against such claims.

A woman named Louisa Carlill took the advertisement seriously. She purchased the smoke ball, used it exactly as directed, and contracted influenza. She wrote to the company requesting her £100. The company declined to pay. Their position: the advertisement was mere puffery, a promotional flourish that no reasonable person would take as a binding offer.

Mrs. Carlill sued. The company's lawyers developed several defenses. There was no offer to any specific person. There were no consideration terms. The promise was too vague to enforce. An offer to the whole world is not an offer at all.

The English Court of Appeal, in 1893, rejected every argument. The company had deposited £1,000 in a bank — that was not puffery, that was a demonstration of serious intent. The terms were definite: use the product as directed, contract influenza, collect £100. Mrs. Carlill had accepted by performing. She had given consideration by buying the product and complying with the conditions. The contract was valid. The company owed £100.

The case has been taught in law schools in every common-law jurisdiction for the past 130 years. The reason is not that it is dramatic — though it is, a little — but that it forces you to think through what a contract actually is, what makes a promise binding, and why the law will enforce some agreements and not others. Those questions are the subject of this chapter.

## What makes a promise a contract

Here is the basic problem. People make promises constantly. Most promises are not contracts. Your friend promises to meet you for lunch; if she doesn't show up, you have no legal claim. Your employer promises to promote you someday; if the promotion never comes, you generally cannot sue. The legal system does not enforce all promises. It enforces a specific subset.

The doctrine that identifies which promises the law will enforce has, in U.S. common law, five requirements. All five must be satisfied. Miss one and you have a moral obligation, perhaps, but not a legal one.

**Offer.** One party must make a proposal demonstrating serious intent to enter an agreement. The seriousness requirement is what the Carbolic Smoke Ball Company tried to defeat, and what the £1,000 deposit sank. Jokes are not offers. Advertisements are generally not offers — they are invitations to negotiate — but the Carbolic case establishes that an advertisement specific enough in its terms, backed by a visible commitment, can cross the line.

The offer must also be definite. A promise to pay "a reasonable amount" for work performed may or may not be enforceable; a promise to pay "$75,000 for the following services, delivered by the following date" is. Courts need terms specific enough that they can tell when performance has occurred and calculate the remedy if it hasn't.

**Acceptance.** The offeree must agree to be bound by the offer's terms, without modification. This is the *mirror image* rule: acceptance has to match the offer exactly. If you offer to sell me a car for $20,000 and I say "I'll take it for $18,000," I haven't accepted — I've made a counter-offer, and your original offer is now extinguished. If you then walk away, I cannot come back and accept the original $20,000 offer. It no longer exists.

The mirror-image rule is strict in common-law contract doctrine. The Uniform Commercial Code, which governs sales of goods between merchants, modifies it — a topic Chapter 8 addresses. But for services, real estate, employment, and most contracts not involving the sale of goods, the rule applies: acceptance that adds or changes terms is rejection plus counter-offer.

**Consideration.** Each party must give something of value. The something need not be equal — courts do not generally inquire into whether the deal was a good one for both sides. A peppercorn is, famously, sufficient consideration if both parties agree. What consideration requires is *mutuality*: both parties must be getting something, and both parties must be giving something. A promise to make a gift is not enforceable precisely because the gift-giver is getting nothing in return. The law treats gift promises differently from exchange promises, and consideration is the line between them.

**Capacity.** Both parties must have the legal ability to enter the contract. Minors — generally those under 18 — lack capacity, with specific exceptions for necessities like food, clothing, and shelter. People who are mentally incompetent lack capacity. People who were substantially intoxicated at the moment of contracting may lack capacity. The practical implication is that a contract signed with someone who lacked capacity is *voidable* at the option of the party who lacked it — the minor can walk away; the adult party generally cannot.

**Legality.** The contract's purpose must be legal. A contract to commit a crime is unenforceable. A contract in restraint of trade — say, a non-compete clause so broad that it prevents someone from ever working in their industry again — may be unenforceable to the extent it violates public policy. Courts will sometimes sever the illegal portions and enforce the rest; sometimes the illegality defeats the whole contract.

Run Mrs. Carlill through these five. Offer: yes, the advertisement was a definite, serious proposal backed by a £1,000 deposit. Acceptance: yes, she performed the advertised conditions. Consideration: she purchased the product and complied with the directions; the company promised £100. Capacity: no issue. Legality: no issue. All five satisfied. Contract enforceable. The company pays.

## When the agreement isn't genuine

The five elements establish formation. But a contract that formally satisfies all five may still be invalid if something was wrong with the *quality* of the agreement — if one party's consent was obtained by deception, pressure, or mistake.

**Fraud.** If one party intentionally misrepresents a material fact — a fact that the other party would have found important to the decision — with knowledge that the representation is false, intending the other party to rely on it, and the other party does rely on it and is harmed, the contract is *voidable* at the defrauded party's option. Voidable means the injured party can choose to rescind it. The defrauding party cannot rescind on the basis of their own fraud.

**Innocent misrepresentation.** The same mechanism — material false statement, reliance, harm — can produce voidability even without the intent element. If the statement was made honestly but turned out to be false, and the other party relied on it, rescission may still be available. The remedies are typically narrower than in fraud.

**Mutual mistake.** Both parties are mistaken about a fact essential to the agreement. The classic case is a contract for the sale of a cow that both parties believe to be infertile; it turns out the cow is pregnant, which substantially changes its value. Neither party was lying. Both were wrong about the same thing. When the mistake goes to the basic substance of the agreement, the contract may be voidable by either party.

**Unilateral mistake** — only one party is wrong — generally does not produce voidability. You took a risk when you entered the contract; that you miscalculated does not let you escape. The narrow exception is when the other party knew or had reason to know of your mistake and said nothing, in which case the equities shift.

**Duress.** Agreement obtained by threat is not genuine agreement. Physical threats are the obvious case. Economic duress — I will cut off your essential supply unless you agree to these new terms, and you have no reasonable alternative — is harder to establish but recognized. The key question is whether the threatened party had a reasonable choice. If they did, the agreement stands. If they genuinely had no reasonable alternative, duress may void it.

**Undue influence.** Agreement obtained through improper pressure by a party in a position of trust or dominance. The doctrine arises most often where someone in a fiduciary relationship — a caretaker, a financial advisor, a lawyer — exercises that position to extract an agreement the other party would not have freely made. The mechanism: the dominated party's judgment is substituted by the dominant party's will.

There is also a broader backstop: *unconscionability*. A contract whose terms are so one-sided that no reasonable person in possession of their faculties would have agreed to them can be refused enforcement, especially when the lopsidedness was accompanied by procedural unfairness — a take-it-or-leave-it form contract, incomprehensible fine print, a party with no bargaining leverage at all. The doctrine is applied most often in consumer contracts. Between sophisticated commercial parties with real bargaining power, courts rarely find unconscionability, on the theory that both parties knew what they were doing.

## The problem with consideration

The consideration requirement, applied strictly, produces some results that common sense finds unsatisfying.

Suppose a company recruits you aggressively, extends a job offer, and tells you to start in sixty days. You turn down another offer. You give notice to your current employer. You sell your house in Cincinnati and buy an apartment in Boston near the new office. Sixty days later, the company calls and says it has changed its mind; the position is eliminated.

Under strict consideration analysis, your position may be weaker than you'd expect. The employment relationship hadn't started. Whether there was a binding contract depends on the specific terms of the offer and acceptance, and under many states' employment-at-will doctrine, even a signed offer letter may not create an enforceable promise beyond the first day of employment.

But you sold your house. You turned down a competing offer. You bought an apartment. You relied on the promise to your substantial detriment.

The doctrine of *promissory estoppel* addresses this. The rule: a promise that a reasonable person should expect to induce significant action or forbearance in the other party, and that does induce it, is enforceable to the extent necessary to prevent injustice — even without consideration.

The elements are fact-specific: a promise was made; reliance on the promise was reasonable; the reliance produced action that would not otherwise have occurred; the relying party suffered real, measurable harm; and allowing the promisor to walk away without consequences would be unjust. Under promissory estoppel, the measure of recovery is typically *reliance damages* — what you lost by relying on the promise — not *expectation damages* — what you would have gained if the promise had been kept.

In the job example: the company is probably not liable for your full expected salary under the employment agreement (that is expectation damages, and the contract may not have been binding). The company is probably liable for your actual losses from relying on the promise — the apartment costs, the lost competing offer, the cost differential of the new lease. Promissory estoppel closes the gap between the strict consideration doctrine and what justice seems to require.

## What happens when performance fails

A contract is a promise about the future. The future doesn't always cooperate. When a party fails to perform as promised, the law provides a set of remedies designed to restore the situation — not to punish the breaching party, but to put the injured party in an appropriate position.

**Expectation damages** are the standard remedy. The goal: place the non-breaching party in the position it would have been in if the contract had been fully performed. If you contracted to buy a piece of equipment for $50,000 and the seller delivers nothing, and you have to buy the same equipment elsewhere for $65,000, your expectation damages are $15,000 — the difference between what you were promised and what you had to pay to get it.

Expectation damages also include *consequential damages* — losses that flow from the breach beyond the direct cost differential, when those losses were foreseeable at the time of contracting. The rule from *Hadley v. Baxendale* (1854), still good law in the U.S.: a breaching party is liable for losses that arise naturally from the breach, and for losses that were within the reasonable contemplation of both parties when the contract was made. Unusual downstream losses that neither party could have anticipated are not recoverable.

**Reliance damages** are smaller. The goal: restore the non-breaching party to the position it was in before the contract was made. You don't recover what you expected to gain; you recover what you actually spent in reliance on the contract. Reliance damages appear most often in promissory estoppel cases, where expectation damages aren't available, and in cases where expectation damages are too speculative to calculate.

**Restitution** addresses the different problem of unjust enrichment. If you paid money under a contract that turned out to be unenforceable, or if you partially performed before the other party breached, restitution requires the breaching party to return any benefit they received. The principle is simple: you should not be able to keep what you received under an agreement you refused to honor.

**Specific performance** is the exceptional remedy — a court order directing the breaching party to actually perform the contract. Courts are reluctant to issue such orders because they require ongoing judicial supervision and because, in most commercial contexts, money damages are adequate. The exceptions: real estate (each piece of land is unique; money isn't a perfect substitute) and contracts for the sale of goods that are genuinely unique. You cannot sue for specific performance of a contract for the delivery of a thousand tons of standard-grade wheat; you can, arguably, sue for specific performance of a contract for the sale of a specific antique.

**Liquidated damages** are damages the parties specified in advance, in the contract itself, for the event of breach. Liquidated-damages clauses are enforceable when they represent a reasonable forecast of likely actual losses at the time of contracting. They are unenforceable when they are so disproportionate to likely actual losses that they function as penalties — ways to punish breach rather than compensate for it. The distinction between a reasonable forecast and a penalty is sometimes litigated.

One more principle that cuts across all of these: the *duty to mitigate*. A non-breaching party cannot simply sit back and let damages accumulate. Once breach occurs, the injured party is required to take reasonable steps to reduce the loss. The seller who breaches a real estate contract is not liable for damages the buyer could have avoided by promptly finding a comparable property. The rule does not require heroic effort — only reasonable steps. But it means that damages are measured from what the injured party suffered after mitigation, not from what they chose to suffer by doing nothing.

## The purpose of the machinery

Contract law, when you look at it carefully, is not primarily about punishment. There is no criminal prosecution for breach of contract. The remedies — expectation damages, reliance damages, restitution — are calibrated to restoration, not retribution. The law's position is, roughly: if you breach a contract, you owe the other party whatever it takes to put them in the position your promise created or, if that's not calculable, to put them back where they started. You owe them compensation for what your breach cost them. You generally do not owe them more than that.

This has an implication worth taking seriously. The law does not prohibit breach of contract. It prices it. If a party can breach a contract, pay the other party's expectation damages, and still come out ahead — because the value of the performance to them fell below its cost — the law generally allows that. Economists call this *efficient breach*: it is sometimes more valuable for a resource to go somewhere other than where a contract directed it, and the damages system permits that reallocation while compensating the party who had to give up their contractual expectation.

Whether you find this morally satisfying depends on your intuitions about promise-keeping. What it means practically is that contract law is designed to make commercial activity reliable, not to guarantee that every promise is kept. The smoke-ball company had to pay Mrs. Carlill because the machinery said so. If the machinery had produced the opposite result — if advertisements could never be binding, if there were no way to make a credible public commitment — the world would contain fewer such commitments, and commercial life would be harder to navigate. The doctrine's job is to create the conditions under which people can trust each other enough to transact, and the five-element framework, with its genuine-agreement qualifications and its calibrated remedies, is the specific mechanism by which that job gets done.
