# American Law, Legal Reasoning, and the Legal System

In June 2018, the U.S. Supreme Court decided a case captioned *South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc.* The case arose from a 2016 South Dakota statute requiring out-of-state online retailers to collect and remit sales tax on goods sold to South Dakota residents, even when the retailer had no physical presence in the state. The statute was a deliberate challenge to a 1992 Supreme Court precedent — *Quill Corp. v. North Dakota* — that had held the opposite: that states could not require sales-tax collection from retailers without an in-state physical presence. South Dakota knew the statute violated *Quill*. South Dakota wanted the Supreme Court to reconsider *Quill*. The Court did. By a 5-4 vote, it overruled *Quill* and upheld the South Dakota statute.

The Wayfair decision changed the operating economics of e-commerce overnight. Approximately ten thousand state and local taxing jurisdictions in the United States, each with its own sales-tax rate and set of taxable categories, became potentially relevant to any online retailer with sales above specified thresholds in those jurisdictions. The compliance infrastructure that emerged in the years that followed — automated tax-calculation services, multi-state filing platforms, certified state-tax-rate databases — is now part of the standard operating cost of any U.S. online retail business of meaningful scale.

The Wayfair case is a useful starting point for this chapter because it illustrates, in compressed form, several features of American law that the chapter is built around. *American law operates through specific cases producing specific decisions, with the decisions becoming the precedent for subsequent cases.* *American law derives from multiple sources — constitutional, statutory, regulatory, judicial — that interact in specific ways.* *American law is layered across federal, state, and local jurisdictions, with rules for which layer governs in which circumstances.* And *American law affects business operations directly, often in ways that reshape entire markets in a single decision.*

## Why this chapter matters

Every business decision happens inside a legal framework. The framework is built from rules that have specific origins, that operate at specific layers of government, and that interact with each other in specific ways. The framework is not optional. It is the structural condition of doing business in the United States. The chapter is the operating manual for the framework.

## Concept 1: The purposes of American law and its British roots

The American legal system has structural origins in the British legal system, from which it inherits, among other things, the doctrine of *common law* — judge-made law produced by accumulated rulings on specific cases over centuries. All U.S. states except Louisiana have *reception statutes* that explicitly incorporate British common law as it stood at independence into the state's legal foundation. Louisiana's exception traces to its French and Spanish colonial heritage and the resulting *civil-law* tradition that contemporary Louisiana law preserves in modified form.

The contemporary American legal system has, on the available consensus account, five functional purposes.

**Establishing standards.** The legal system specifies the minimum conduct required of members of the society. Federal laws apply to all U.S. citizens; state laws apply to citizens within the state's jurisdiction; local laws (city, county, township) apply to citizens within the local jurisdiction. The standards are not negotiable in the way that ethical commitments may be; they are enforceable through the apparatus of the courts.

**Promoting consistency.** The system uses the doctrine of *precedent* — *stare decisis*, *to stand by things decided* — to require that courts decide similar cases in similar ways. The doctrine produces consistency over time and across jurisdictions, making the system's outcomes more predictable than a system that decided each case in isolation would be.

**Maintaining order.** Law substitutes orderly procedures for the chaotic alternatives. Disputes that would, in a stateless society, be settled by force are settled by procedure.

**Resolving disputes.** Beyond the formal court system, the legal apparatus permits *alternative dispute resolution* — mediation, arbitration, settlement negotiation — that resolves the bulk of commercial disputes without litigation. Concept 3 in Chapter 2 will return to this.

**Protecting liberties and rights.** The U.S. Constitution and its state-level analogs articulate specific rights of citizens against state action and against private action where the state has chosen to extend protection. The legal system enforces those rights when they are violated.

The five purposes operate together. A specific law (say, a state statute on the recording of consumer credit transactions) typically serves several of them simultaneously — establishing a standard, promoting consistency across transactions, maintaining order in the consumer-credit market, providing a dispute-resolution framework when transactions go wrong, and protecting borrower rights against predatory practices.

### Common law and the *Wayfair* example

The case from the chapter cold open is a useful illustration of how common law operates.

The 2016 South Dakota statute took effect. Wayfair, an online furniture retailer with no physical presence in South Dakota, did not collect the sales tax. The state filed a declaratory-judgment action. The South Dakota Supreme Court ruled, in *State v. Wayfair Inc.* (2017 SD 56, 901 N.W.2d 754), that the state statute violated the U.S. Supreme Court's 1992 *Quill* precedent and was unconstitutional. South Dakota appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court. The U.S. Supreme Court granted *certiorari* (the formal acceptance of an appeal) and, in June 2018, overruled *Quill* and reversed the South Dakota Supreme Court. The 1992 precedent that had governed e-commerce taxation for twenty-six years was no longer good law.

The *Wayfair* case illustrates that common-law precedent is durable but not permanent. A precedent stands until an appellate court with appropriate jurisdiction reconsiders it. The *Wayfair* Court held that the *Quill* rule had been a creation of an earlier era — the era of mail-order catalog sales in which the physical-presence requirement was a sensible proxy for the substantive issue (whether the retailer had enough connection to a state to be fairly subject to its tax authority) — and that the rule had become outdated as e-commerce had grown into a substantial fraction of retail sales. The doctrine of *stare decisis* counsels following precedent; it does not require following precedent that has been superseded by changed circumstances.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that common law is *unwritten* in the sense that it does not exist in any written form. It does. Common law is written in the published opinions of appellate courts. The published opinions are aggregated into reporters (legal publishing series), digitized in legal databases (Westlaw, LexisNexis, public-access services like CourtListener and Justia), and cited by lawyers and judges in every legal proceeding.

The second is that civil-law and common-law systems are radically different. They are different in important ways — civil-law systems (most of continental Europe, Japan, much of Latin America) rely more heavily on codified statutes and less on judicial precedent; common-law systems (the U.K., U.S. except Louisiana, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, India) rely more heavily on judicial precedent. The systems operate with different default postures but converge substantially in practice on most commercial questions.

The third is that the doctrine of precedent prevents legal change. It does not. The doctrine produces continuity between case and case while permitting deliberate change through appellate decisions like *Wayfair*. Change is slower in a common-law system than in a system that permits legislative override of judicial precedent, but it is not absent.

## Concept 2: The four sources of American law

American law derives from four primary sources, and any meaningful legal question typically engages more than one of them.

**Constitutional law.** The U.S. Constitution is the foremost law of the land. The first ten amendments — the *Bill of Rights*, ratified in 1791 — articulate specific protections of individual liberty against federal action; the Fourteenth Amendment, ratified in 1868, extends most of those protections against state action through its Due Process and Equal Protection clauses. The Constitution's *Commerce Clause* (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3) gives Congress the power to regulate interstate commerce; that single clause, read broadly, has produced most of the modern federal regulatory state. The *Spending Clause* in the same section gives Congress the power to attach conditions to federal funds, producing additional federal leverage over policy areas that Congress could not directly regulate. State constitutions parallel the federal Constitution at the state level, with some states' constitutions providing additional rights beyond the federal floor.

**Statutory law.** Federal statutes are enacted by Congress and signed by the President (or enacted over presidential veto). State statutes are enacted by state legislatures and signed by governors (or enacted over gubernatorial veto). Local ordinances are enacted by city councils, county commissions, and similar local bodies. The federal *United States Code* (USC) is the codified compilation of federal statutes; the state codes serve the analogous function at the state level. When a bill becomes a federal law, it is assigned a public-law number, prepared for publication by the Office of the Federal Register, and incorporated into the USC.

**Administrative regulation.** Federal agencies — the EPA, the SEC, the FDA, OSHA, the FCC, dozens of others — are granted rulemaking authority by Congress through specific enabling statutes. Within the scope of that authority, the agencies promulgate *regulations* that have the force of law. The *Administrative Procedure Act* of 1946 establishes the rulemaking process: notice-and-comment, public hearings, judicial review, codification in the *Code of Federal Regulations* (CFR). Agency regulations are subject to constitutional and statutory limits; an agency that promulgates a regulation outside its statutory authority can have the regulation vacated by a reviewing court.

**Case law.** Judicial decisions, interpreting and applying the constitutional, statutory, and regulatory law described above, constitute the fourth source. Trial-court decisions resolve specific disputes between specific parties. Appellate decisions, in addition to resolving the dispute, articulate rules of law that bind subsequent decisions in the same jurisdiction (the doctrine of precedent). U.S. Supreme Court decisions on federal-law questions bind all U.S. courts. State supreme court decisions on state-law questions bind all state courts within the state.

### How the sources interact: a worked example

The Environmental Protection Agency's regulation of motor-vehicle emissions illustrates how the four sources interact in operation.

The *Constitution*'s Commerce Clause gives Congress power to regulate interstate commerce, including the manufacture and sale of motor vehicles.

The *Clean Air Act of 1970* (and its 1990 amendments) is a federal *statute* that authorizes the EPA to regulate motor-vehicle emissions and to set national emissions standards. The Act preempts most state regulation in the area, with a specific exception for California (which has a long-standing waiver permitting stricter state-level emissions standards under specified conditions, with other states permitted to follow California's standards rather than the federal floor).

The EPA's *regulations* promulgated under the Clean Air Act establish specific emissions limits for specific vehicle categories, the testing protocols by which compliance is measured, and the enforcement mechanisms by which violations are penalized. The regulations are codified in Title 40 of the CFR.

The *case law* interpreting the regulations includes, among many others, *Massachusetts v. EPA* (2007), in which the Supreme Court held that greenhouse gases are *air pollutants* within the meaning of the Clean Air Act and that the EPA was required to regulate them or to provide a reasoned explanation for not doing so. The decision substantially expanded the scope of EPA authority and produced a sequence of subsequent regulatory actions on vehicle and stationary-source greenhouse-gas emissions.

A specific business decision — say, a vehicle manufacturer's decision about emissions controls in a 2026 model year — operates inside the framework all four sources jointly produce. The Constitution authorizes the regulation; the statute creates the program; the regulations specify the requirements; the case law shapes how the requirements are interpreted and enforced.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that statutes always trump common law. They do, on the topics they address. They do not, on topics they do not address. Common law continues to govern in any area not preempted by statute. Most contract law in U.S. states is still primarily common law, supplemented by statutes (the Uniform Commercial Code's Article 2 governs sales of goods) but not replaced by them.

The second is that federal law always trumps state law. It does, when both apply. It does not, on topics outside Congress's enumerated powers. State regulation of family law, of most criminal law, of most tort law, and of most professional licensing is unaffected by federal statutes (with limited exceptions for areas where Congress has acted under specific constitutional authority).

The third is that regulations are *less* binding than statutes. They are not. Regulations promulgated within an agency's statutory authority have the force of law and are enforceable through the same mechanisms as statutes themselves.

## Concept 3: Federal, state, and local — the layers and their interactions

The U.S. operates with fifty-one parallel governments — the federal government and the fifty states — each with its own constitution, legislature, executive, and judiciary. Local governments (counties, cities, towns) operate under authority delegated from the states. The interaction among the three layers — federal, state, local — produces the operating texture of U.S. law.

### The mechanism: enumerated federal powers, reserved state powers, the doctrine of preemption

**Enumerated federal powers.** The federal government's authority is, on the constitutional structure, *limited*. Congress can enact laws only on subjects within its enumerated powers — most importantly, the Commerce Clause, the Spending Clause, the Necessary and Proper Clause (which permits Congress to enact laws *necessary and proper* for executing its other powers), and the specific powers granted in Articles I and III. Subject matters outside those enumerated powers are reserved to the states.

**Reserved state powers.** The Tenth Amendment to the Constitution provides that *the powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people*. The Amendment is the textual basis for the *reserved-powers* doctrine that defines the boundary between federal and state authority. The doctrine has been substantially reshaped over time by Supreme Court interpretations of the Commerce Clause, with the Court's reading of federal Commerce Clause authority broadening through the New Deal era and contracting modestly in subsequent decades.

**Federal preemption.** When federal law and state law conflict, federal law generally prevails — under the *Supremacy Clause* of Article VI. Preemption can be *express* (the federal statute explicitly states that it preempts state law on the topic), *implied* (the federal scheme is so comprehensive that no room for state regulation is left), or *conflict* (the federal and state requirements cannot both be satisfied). The doctrine has been substantially developed in case law; specific preemption questions are often the substance of major federal litigation.

**Concurrent jurisdiction.** Some areas — antitrust, securities regulation, employment discrimination — are subject to both federal and state law operating concurrently. A defendant in such a case may face both federal and state enforcement; the two systems coexist with rules for coordination.

### Worked example: state minimum wages

The U.S. federal minimum wage has been $7.25 per hour since July 2009. Twenty-nine states (as of 2026) operate minimum wages above the federal floor. Many cities (San Francisco, Seattle, New York City, others) operate minimum wages above the state floor. The layered system permits each level of government to set a higher floor than the level above; it does not permit a lower floor.

The mechanism: the federal Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (FLSA) sets a national floor. The Act does not preempt state law; it permits states to set higher floors. State law similarly permits local floors above the state floor (in states that grant local-government wage authority). The operative wage in any given workplace is the highest applicable floor — federal, state, or local.

The pattern is the standard one for areas of concurrent jurisdiction: a federal floor with state and local discretion to go above it. Most U.S. employment law, much of consumer protection law, and most environmental law follow this pattern.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that state law is always weaker than federal law. It is, on the questions where federal law applies. On the many questions where federal law does not apply, state law is the operative regime, and state law can be substantially more demanding than the federal default.

The second is that local law is decorative. It is not. Local zoning, taxation, business licensing, and operating regulations are often the most directly consequential law affecting a small business's daily operations. The federal regime sets the perimeter; the local regime determines what can happen on a specific piece of property.

The third is that preemption is automatic. It is not. Specific preemption questions are often litigated, and the outcomes are sometimes counterintuitive. The chapter recommends that businesses operating across multiple states consult specific legal counsel on preemption questions rather than assuming the federal default.

This chapter has built the legal apparatus the rest of the textbook will operate inside. The book is, after this chapter, a sequence of applied chapters covering the specific legal disciplines most directly relevant to business practice.

**Disputes and dispute settlement** (Chapter 2). The mechanisms — court litigation, arbitration, mediation, settlement — by which commercial disputes are resolved.

**Business and the U.S. Constitution** (Chapter 4). The specific constitutional provisions most directly affecting business — the Commerce Clause, the Takings Clause, the First Amendment as it applies to commercial speech, due process and equal protection in the business context.

**Criminal liability** (Chapter 5). The principles by which corporations and corporate officers can be held criminally responsible for actions taken within the firm's operations.

**Torts** (Chapter 6). The body of civil-law liability for harms outside the scope of contract — negligence, intentional torts, product liability, defamation.

**Contract law** (Chapter 7). The formation, performance, breach, and remedy of agreements.

**Sales contracts** (Chapter 8). The Uniform Commercial Code's Article 2 framework for the sale of goods.

**Employment and labor law** (Chapter 9). The federal and state framework governing the employment relationship.

**Government regulation** (Chapter 10). The administrative-state apparatus through which much of contemporary business law operates.

**Antitrust law** (Chapter 11). The Sherman Act, Clayton Act, and FTC Act framework for preventing anti-competitive market practices.

**Unfair trade practices and the FTC** (Chapter 12). The consumer-protection apparatus.

**International law** (Chapter 13). The framework for cross-border business operations.

**Securities regulation** (Chapter 14). The federal regime governing public capital markets.

The chapter sequence is not exhaustive of business law — corporate-formation law, intellectual property, real estate, bankruptcy, and several other major areas are not given full chapters. The sequence does cover the areas most directly relevant to the operating manager. The reader who has worked through the chapters that follow will have a working competence in the legal framework inside which U.S. business operates.

## Chapter summary

You can now do five things you could not do before. You can identify the four sources of American law and articulate the relationship among them. You can distinguish the federal, state, and local layers of U.S. law and apply the federal preemption doctrine to specific cases. You can apply *stare decisis* to a real case and explain how case law functions as binding precedent. You can evaluate administrative regulation as the operationalization of statutory authority. And you can articulate the scope of business law as a body of legal disciplines that the rest of the book will work through in detail.

The single idea that matters most is that American law is *layered* — constitutional, statutory, regulatory, judicial; federal, state, local — and that any specific legal question typically engages multiple layers and multiple sources simultaneously. Practitioners who work with the legal apparatus daily develop the habit of asking, of any specific question, *which sources govern, which jurisdictions apply, and what the controlling precedent is*. The habit is not optional; it is the operating discipline of competent business operation.

You should now be able to teach the four sources, the three jurisdictional layers, and the doctrine of *stare decisis* to a colleague who has not read this chapter, in fifteen minutes, with one specific case for each.
