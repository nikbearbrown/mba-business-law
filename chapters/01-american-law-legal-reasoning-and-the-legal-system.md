# Chapter 1 — American Law, Legal Reasoning, and the Legal System
*Nine unelected judges, fifty sovereign states, and why the answer is never just "look it up."*

Here is something I want you to think about.

You run a furniture company in Boston. You have a warehouse in Boston, employees in Boston, a truck that makes deliveries around New England. One day someone in South Dakota — a state you have never visited, where you have no warehouse, no employees, no office — finds your website, likes a couch, and orders it. You box it up and hand it to a carrier. Done.

Now: do you owe South Dakota anything?

Your intuition probably says no. You did nothing in South Dakota. The package just went there. South Dakota is, as far as your business is concerned, just a place the couch ended up.

And yet since 2018, the answer has been yes. South Dakota can legally require you to collect sales tax on that transaction, file returns with their revenue department, and track their rules about which product categories are taxable — all for a state where you have never set foot. If you sell enough couches there, you have to do this. Same for potentially ten thousand other state and local jurisdictions across the United States.

How is this possible? Where does this obligation come from? Nobody passed a law specifically about your couch business. No South Dakota official called you. And yet the obligation is real, it is enforceable, and a federal court will back it up.

That is what this chapter is about. Not the answer to the couch question specifically — you already have that — but the machinery underneath it. The structure that makes a question like that answerable at all, that tells you where to look, what counts as an authority, and why the answer might be different next decade than it is today.

## Where law comes from

Most people, when they think about law, think of it as a thing that exists — a set of rules filed somewhere that you look up when you need them. This is not wrong, exactly, but it misses something important. Law is *produced*. It has sources. And the source matters, because it tells you something you actually need to know: how hard this rule is to change, who can override it, and what happens when two rules conflict.

There are four sources of American law, and they sit in a hierarchy.

<!-- → INFOGRAPHIC: Vertical hierarchy of the four sources of American law — Constitution at top, then Statutory, then Administrative Regulation, then Case Law — with a one-sentence description of each and an arrow indicating that each lower layer must be consistent with the layers above it. Reader should see at a glance that the hierarchy is about override authority, not volume of rules. -->

At the top is the Constitution. It is not the most detailed document in the legal system — it is actually quite short — but it has a special property: everything else has to be consistent with it. A federal statute that contradicts the Constitution is void. A state regulation that violates the Constitution is void. The Constitution is not a law among laws; it is the constraint that all other laws must satisfy. Think of it less like a rulebook and more like the axioms in a mathematical system. Everything downstream has to follow from it.

One sentence in that document turns out to matter enormously for business. The Commerce Clause gives Congress the power to regulate commerce "among the several states." Seven words. Those seven words are the constitutional authority behind most of the modern federal regulatory apparatus — environmental rules, workplace safety, financial markets, e-commerce taxation. Two centuries of courts have been arguing about what those seven words mean and how far they reach. They are still arguing.

Below the Constitution sits statutory law — what legislatures produce. Congress passes a bill; the President signs it; it becomes federal statute. State legislatures do the same at the state level. The result is an enormous body of written rules covering everything from bankruptcy to food safety. Statutes are the primary mechanism by which political choices become law. Notice what that implies: a statute reflects a particular moment in time, a particular political configuration, a particular set of assumptions about the world. Those assumptions can become obsolete. The statute does not automatically update itself.

Below statutes sits administrative regulation. A statute like the Clean Air Act says the EPA shall regulate air emissions. It does not say what the limit should be on a specific truck engine in a specific year. That detail is left to the agency. The agency writes regulations through a formal process — propose a rule, take public comments, respond to those comments, finalize — and the result goes into the Code of Federal Regulations. These regulations have the force of law. Not because Congress passed each one, but because Congress gave the agency the authority to make them.

This is commonly misunderstood. Regulations are not suggestions. They are as binding as any statute, within the scope of what the authorizing statute permits. The important qualifier is that last phrase. Courts occasionally decide that an agency went further than Congress actually authorized — that the regulation covers ground the statute didn't give the agency power over. When that happens, the regulation fails. The question of how much authority Congress actually delegated to any given agency is actively contested in courts right now.

At the bottom of the hierarchy — though "bottom" is misleading, because in practice it is vast — is case law. When a court decides a dispute, it writes an opinion explaining its reasoning. That opinion becomes precedent. Future courts facing similar questions are expected to follow it. The doctrine is called *stare decisis*, Latin for "stand by things decided." It is the mechanism that makes the legal system's outputs somewhat predictable over time. Without it, you could lose a lawsuit on a set of facts that would have gotten your competitor off the hook the previous year, with no principled explanation for why.

Predictability, it turns out, is not just an aesthetic preference. It is the foundation of commercial activity. If you cannot forecast legal consequences with some reliability, you cannot write contracts, plan investments, or build a business around reasonable assumptions. *Stare decisis* is what makes the future somewhat legible from the present.

## A twenty-six-year experiment in unintended consequences

Now I can tell you how we got to the South Dakota couch problem. It starts in 1992 with a mail-order catalog company called Quill.

Quill was based in Illinois. North Dakota wanted Quill to collect sales tax on goods shipped to North Dakota customers. The Supreme Court said no. The reasoning was sensible for 1992: if a company has no physical presence in a state — no office, no warehouse, no employees — the connection is too thin to justify forcing it to navigate that state's tax rules. The *physical presence rule* became law.

For twenty-six years, it stayed law. Companies — including, famously, Amazon in its early years — deliberately avoided establishing physical presence in large states precisely to avoid triggering sales-tax obligations. This was not a loophole in any sneaky sense; it was rational behavior in response to a known legal rule. The rule was a feature of the operating environment, and entire logistics and tax strategies were built around it.

Then the world changed in a way the 1992 Court had not anticipated. E-commerce scaled. A company could now have $100,000 in annual sales to South Dakota customers, reaching thousands of South Dakota households, with zero physical presence in the state. The original intuition behind the rule — that no physical presence meant a thin connection — had inverted. The connection was substantial. The physical absence was incidental.

South Dakota decided to force the question. In 2016, the state legislature passed a statute requiring out-of-state retailers above certain sales thresholds to collect sales tax — knowing, explicitly, that the statute violated the existing precedent. The state filed suit against Wayfair, Overstock, and Newegg specifically to engineer a case the Supreme Court would have to take.

The South Dakota Supreme Court struck down the statute. It was following *Quill*; that was the correct thing to do under the rules of precedent. South Dakota appealed. The Supreme Court accepted the case, and in 2018, by a vote of 5 to 4, it overruled *Quill*.

The majority opinion makes an argument you should understand, because it shows you something real about how the legal system works. Justice Kennedy's point was not that *Quill* was badly reasoned in 1992. It was reasonable for its time. His point was that the conditions underlying the rule had changed so fundamentally that the rule no longer made sense. The physical presence test was invented as a proxy for "has this company formed a real connection with this state." By 2018, a company with $100,000 in South Dakota sales had obviously formed a real connection, physical presence or not. The proxy had become misleading.

This is the mechanism of legal change in a common-law system, and it is worth sitting with. *Stare decisis* is a strong presumption — courts are expected to follow precedent, not relitigate it every generation. But it is not an absolute rule. The presumption can be overcome when a court concludes that a prior decision was wrong from the start, or that conditions have changed enough to undermine the reasoning behind it. The doctrine is *durable*. It is not *permanent*.

What this means for business, and for anyone who has to operate inside the legal system, is that legal rules are not geological features. They look permanent until they change, and when they change, they change quickly. The *Wayfair* decision took effect immediately. Every company selling significant volumes online suddenly had new obligations in states where they'd had none the previous morning. That is a real economic event, caused entirely by the accumulated reasoning of nine people who were not elected by anyone.

## Fifty-one governments, one country

Here is another way to see the structure. The United States does not have one legal system. It has fifty-one.

The federal government. Fifty state governments. Each has its own constitution, its own legislature, its own executive, its own court system. Counties and cities layer on top of the states. The three levels interact constantly — sometimes cooperating, sometimes in conflict, always negotiating over who has authority over what.

The original constitutional design starts with enumerated powers. The federal government is not a general government; it can act only on subjects Congress is specifically authorized to regulate. The Commerce Clause is one. Taxing and spending is another. Coining money, raising armies, running the postal system. Everything outside those enumerated subjects is, under the Tenth Amendment, reserved to the states or to the people.

In practice, the Commerce Clause has expanded so broadly that this constraint is less limiting than it sounds. The New Deal-era Court, in the 1930s and 40s, began reading "commerce among the several states" broadly enough to cover almost any economic activity that affects markets across state lines. That reading has been trimmed at the edges — in 1995, the Court said Congress could not criminalize gun possession near schools under the Commerce Clause, because that is not, without more, interstate commerce — but the federal regulatory state built on the broad reading remains intact.

When federal law and state law conflict, federal law wins. That is the Supremacy Clause of Article VI, and it is the mechanism that keeps the fifty-one-government system from fracturing. The doctrine that implements this is called preemption, and it comes in three varieties.

<!-- → TABLE: Three-column table distinguishing the three types of preemption — Express, Implied, Conflict. Columns: Type | Definition | Example | How courts identify it. Reader should be able to use this as a reference when encountering preemption arguments in subsequent chapters. -->

Express preemption is the clearest: the federal statute says outright that state law on this subject is overridden. Implied preemption is trickier: the federal regulatory scheme is so comprehensive that there is no room left for state law to operate alongside it, even if Congress didn't say so explicitly. Conflict preemption is the most common source of litigation: the federal and state requirements are incompatible, either because you literally cannot comply with both, or because the state law stands as an obstacle to what the federal law was trying to accomplish.

But here is what is interesting, and what the minimum wage example illustrates nicely. Federal preemption is not the default. Many federal statutes explicitly *permit* states to exceed the federal standard. The Fair Labor Standards Act sets a federal minimum wage — $7.25 per hour, unchanged since 2009 — and explicitly allows states to set it higher. Twenty-nine states have. Many cities have gone higher still. The operative minimum in any workplace is the highest applicable floor, whichever level of government set it.

Three colors per bar: federal floor, state addition above federal, local addition above state. Reader should see at a glance how the layered structure produces different effective minimums in different places — all within the same federal framework. -->

This pattern — a federal floor, with states and localities free to go higher — appears all over employment law, environmental regulation, and consumer protection. The federal government sets the minimum acceptable standard. Above that line, states compete and experiment. Some observers call this laboratories of democracy: different states trying different policies, with the results visible to anyone who wants to learn from them.

## What the British have to do with this

One more piece of foundation, because it explains something that otherwise seems arbitrary.

American law is built on British law. When the colonies declared independence, they did not erase the legal tradition they had been operating under. They inherited it. All states except Louisiana have *reception statutes* that explicitly incorporated British common law as it stood at independence. Louisiana is the exception because its legal heritage runs through France and Spain rather than England; its law retains features of the civil-law tradition descended from the Napoleonic Code.

The civil-law tradition governs most of continental Europe, Japan, and much of Latin America. The structural difference from common law is this: civil-law systems rely on comprehensive written codes. Judges apply the code. They do not, in the same sense that common-law judges do, *make* law. In a common-law system, the accumulated reasoning of appellate courts over centuries is itself a source of legal rules — not merely interpretation of statutory rules, but an independent body of doctrine that emerges from the decisions themselves.

<!-- → IMAGE: World map shaded to distinguish common-law jurisdictions (UK, US except Louisiana, Canada, Australia, India, etc.) from civil-law jurisdictions (continental Europe, Latin America, Japan, etc.) from mixed jurisdictions (Louisiana, Quebec, South Africa, etc.). Caption should note that the distinction affects how precedent functions in each system and where U.S.-trained lawyers will encounter unfamiliar legal logic when working internationally. -->

In practice, the two traditions have converged substantially on most commercial questions. The differences are more pronounced in family law, property law, and constitutional structure. But if you ever work with lawyers or courts in a civil-law country, you will notice that they look for rules differently — more code, less precedent — and that the authority structure feels different as a result.

## The machinery underneath everything

The structure this chapter has laid out — four sources of law, three jurisdictional layers, the doctrine of precedent, the preemption framework — is not just background. It is the operating environment for every business decision with legal consequences, which is to say most business decisions.

Contract law governs whether the agreements you make are binding and what happens when they are not honored. Tort law governs your liability for harms you cause outside of contract. Employment law governs the relationship between employer and worker. Securities law governs how you raise capital from the public. Antitrust law governs whether your competitive behavior crosses from aggressive to illegal. Each of these domains has the same architecture: constitutional authority at the top, statutes in the middle, regulatory detail at the granular level, and case law interpreting all of it.

There is a temptation, when confronted with this much structure, to look for shortcuts — to find the rule that applies and treat the surrounding machinery as irrelevant overhead. I want to push back on that temptation, because it leads to exactly the kind of error the *Wayfair* situation illustrates. The companies that were caught flat-footed in 2018 were not operating illegally. They were following the existing rule. What they had not accounted for was that the rule could change overnight, that the mechanism for changing it was already in motion, and that the conditions underlying the rule had been deteriorating for years.

The legal system the chapter has described is not optimized for simplicity. It is optimized for something different — resolving disputes among parties with conflicting legitimate interests, across fifty sovereign states, with a long history of skepticism toward centralized authority. The complexity is not incidental. It is the inevitable output of designing a legal system for a society with those specific features.

Understanding why the system is complex the way it is — not just experiencing the complexity as an obstacle — is the starting point for working inside it effectively. The South Dakota couch question has an answer, and the answer is not arbitrary. It follows from a structure that is, once you see it clearly, internally coherent. The couch ends up in South Dakota; the connection is real; the obligation follows. The machinery that gets you to that conclusion is what the rest of this book works through.
