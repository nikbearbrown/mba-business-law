# Chapter 1 — American Law, Legal Reasoning, and the Legal System
*Nine unelected judges, fifty sovereign states, and why the answer is never just "look it up."*

Here is something that seems simple until you think about it: why does a company in Boston, selling furniture over the internet to someone in South Dakota, have to follow South Dakota's tax law?

The answer is not obvious. The company has no office in South Dakota. No warehouse. No employees. No trucks. It has a website and a shipping contract with a carrier. By any ordinary intuition, South Dakota is just a place the packages go.

And yet, since June 2018, South Dakota can require that company to collect and remit sales tax on every transaction over a specified threshold — roughly $100,000 in sales or 200 separate transactions per year. The company has to track South Dakota's rates, file returns with South Dakota's revenue department, and stay current on South Dakota's rules about which product categories are taxable. It has to do the same thing for up to ten thousand other state and local jurisdictions in the United States.

How did we get here? The answer runs through the structure of American law itself — where it comes from, how it changes, how the federal government and the fifty states negotiate authority with each other, and why a single decision by nine unelected judges can reshape the operating economics of an entire industry overnight. Understanding those mechanisms is what this chapter is about.

## Where law comes from

There is a temptation to treat law as a thing that just exists — a set of rules you look up when you need them. That is not a useful way to think about it. Law is produced. It has sources. And the sources matter, because the source of a legal rule tells you something important: how hard it is to change, who has authority to override it, and what happens when rules from different sources conflict.

American law has four sources.

**The Constitution** is the highest. Not highest in the sense that it contains the most detailed rules — it doesn't — but highest in the sense that everything else has to be consistent with it. A federal statute that violates the Constitution is void. A state regulation that violates the Constitution is void. The Constitution is the structural logic the whole system runs inside.

The Constitution's Commerce Clause — the clause giving Congress power to regulate commerce *among the several states* — turns out to be one of the most consequential sentences in the document. That one clause, interpreted broadly, is the authority behind most of the modern federal regulatory apparatus. It is the constitutional sentence that authorized Congress to regulate motor-vehicle emissions, workplace safety, financial markets, and, eventually, e-commerce taxation. The meaning of that sentence has been disputed, litigated, and elaborated in hundreds of cases over two centuries. It still is.

**Statutory law** is what legislatures produce. Congress passes a bill; the President signs it (or Congress overrides a veto); it becomes a federal statute. State legislatures do the same at the state level; city councils and county commissions at the local level. The result is an enormous body of written rules — the United States Code at the federal level, state codes at the state level — covering everything from bankruptcy procedures to food-safety standards to the conditions under which a contract is enforceable.

Statutes are the primary mechanism by which the political process translates policy choices into law. When Congress passes the Clean Air Act, it is making a choice: that air-quality regulation is a federal responsibility, that the executive branch will administer it, and that the specific standards will be set by an agency rather than by Congress itself. Every statute is a design decision, and the design reveals what the legislature was optimizing for.

**Administrative regulation** is where the detail lives. The Clean Air Act tells the EPA to regulate emissions. It does not tell the EPA what the specific emission limits should be for a 2026 pickup truck. That is left to the agency, which promulgates regulations through a process established by the Administrative Procedure Act of 1946: publish a proposed rule, take public comment, respond to significant comments, finalize the rule, codify it in the Code of Federal Regulations. The final rule has the force of law — not because Congress passed it, but because Congress gave the agency authority to make it.

This is important and often misunderstood. Regulations are not suggestions. They are not recommendations. Within the scope of the agency's statutory authority, they are as binding as any statute. The distinction matters in litigation, where parties sometimes argue that an agency exceeded its statutory authority — that it promulgated a rule on a subject Congress did not actually authorize — and courts have to decide whether that argument is right.

**Case law** is what courts produce. Courts interpret statutes; courts interpret constitutional provisions; courts evaluate regulations. When an appellate court interprets a statute and reaches a conclusion, that conclusion becomes precedent — a decision that subsequent courts in the same jurisdiction are expected to follow when they face the same question.

The principle is called *stare decisis*, which is the short form of the Latin *stare decisis et non quieta movere*: to stand by things decided and not disturb what is settled. The doctrine is the mechanism by which the legal system produces consistency over time. If courts decided every case from scratch, applying no precedent, a company could be held liable in one case and not liable on nearly identical facts in the next, with no principled basis for the difference. *Stare decisis* prevents that. It makes the system's outputs somewhat predictable, which turns out to be enormously important for commercial activity — contracts, transactions, long-term planning — all of which depend on being able to forecast legal consequences with some reliability.

## Why South Dakota won

The *Wayfair* case is a useful place to watch these mechanisms in operation.

In 1992, the Supreme Court decided *Quill Corp. v. North Dakota*. The question was whether North Dakota could require a mail-order catalog company — Quill, based in Illinois — to collect and remit North Dakota sales tax on goods shipped to North Dakota customers. The Court said no. Under the dormant Commerce Clause doctrine (the principle that states cannot impose undue burdens on interstate commerce even when Congress has not acted), requiring an out-of-state retailer with no physical presence in the state to collect taxes on behalf of that state was an unconstitutional burden. The *physical presence rule* became law.

For twenty-six years, *Quill* governed mail-order and then e-commerce sales taxation. States could not require collection from retailers without in-state physical presence. Amazon, for most of its early history, deliberately avoided establishing physical presence in large states precisely to avoid triggering sales-tax obligations. The *Quill* rule was a feature of the operating environment, and companies built their logistics and tax strategies around it.

Then South Dakota decided to force a reconsideration. In 2016, the state legislature passed a statute requiring sales-tax collection from out-of-state retailers meeting the threshold criteria — knowing, explicitly, that the statute violated *Quill*. The state filed a declaratory-judgment action against Wayfair, Overstock, and Newegg to get the question before the courts.

The South Dakota Supreme Court struck down the statute, correctly applying *Quill*. South Dakota appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court, which granted *certiorari* — the formal acceptance of an appeal — and, in 2018, overruled *Quill* by a 5-4 vote.

Justice Kennedy, writing for the majority, made an argument that is worth understanding directly. The *physical presence rule*, he wrote, had been invented before e-commerce existed. In 1992, the rule was a reasonable proxy: if a company had no physical presence in a state, its connection to that state was likely too thin to justify the compliance burden of collecting taxes for it. By 2018, a company with $100,000 in annual sales to South Dakota customers had a substantial economic connection to South Dakota, even with no physical presence. The rule had outlived the conditions that made it sensible.

*Stare decisis* counsels following precedent. It does not require following precedent indefinitely when the conditions underlying the precedent have fundamentally changed. The Court held that the e-commerce economy had changed the relevant conditions sufficiently to warrant departure from *Quill*.

This is the mechanism of legal change in a common-law system. Precedent is durable. It is expected to govern until an appellate court with appropriate jurisdiction reconsiders it. But it is not permanent. The doctrine of *stare decisis* is a strong presumption, not an absolute rule, and the presumption can be overcome when a court concludes that a prior decision was wrong or has been superseded by changed circumstances.

## The architecture of federalism

The *Wayfair* case is a federal constitutional case — a case about the limits the Constitution places on state taxing authority. It illustrates something about the architecture of American federalism that is worth stating directly.

The United States operates with fifty-one parallel governments. The federal government. Fifty state governments. Each has its own constitution, legislature, executive branch, and judiciary. Local governments — counties, cities, towns, townships — operate under authority delegated from the states. The three layers interact constantly, and the interaction is governed by rules about which layer has authority over what.

The constitutional structure starts with enumerated federal powers. The federal government is a government of specific powers; Congress can enact law only on subjects within those enumerated powers. The Commerce Clause is one. The Spending Clause is one. The power to raise armies, coin money, and operate a postal system are others. Subject matters outside those enumerated powers are, under the Tenth Amendment, reserved to the states or to the people.

In practice, the Commerce Clause has expanded the scope of federal authority substantially. The New Deal-era Supreme Court, under political pressure after Franklin Roosevelt's failed court-packing threat, began reading the Commerce Clause broadly enough to authorize most federal economic regulation. That reading has been somewhat constrained in subsequent decades — the Court held, in 1995, that Congress could not criminalize possession of guns near schools under the Commerce Clause because possession of a gun near a school is not, without more, economic activity in interstate commerce — but it remains broad enough to authorize most of the regulatory state.

The mechanism that resolves conflicts between federal and state law is the Supremacy Clause of Article VI: when federal and state law conflict, federal law prevails. The doctrine of *preemption* gives this specific operational content. Express preemption means the federal statute explicitly says it overrides state law. Implied preemption means the federal scheme is comprehensive enough that no room for state law is left. Conflict preemption means the federal and state requirements are genuinely incompatible — compliance with both is impossible, or state law stands as an obstacle to the federal objective.

The minimum wage illustrates the layered structure without preemption. The federal Fair Labor Standards Act sets a national minimum wage — $7.25 per hour since 2009. The Act does not preempt states from setting higher minimums; it explicitly permits them. Twenty-nine states have done so. Many cities have gone higher than their states. The operative minimum in any given workplace is the highest applicable floor — federal, state, or local. The federal floor is the floor below which no jurisdiction can go. Everything above it is state and local discretion.

This pattern — a federal floor with state and local discretion above it — appears across much of American employment law, environmental regulation, and consumer protection. The federal government sets the minimum acceptable standard; states and localities can be more demanding if they choose.

## What British common law has to do with any of this

American law inherits its basic structure from British law, and one feature of that inheritance is the doctrine of common law itself.

Common law is judge-made law. Not made arbitrarily — made through the accumulated reasoning of appellate decisions on specific disputes, over centuries. When a court decides a case and writes an opinion explaining its reasoning, that opinion becomes available as precedent. Future courts facing similar questions are expected to reason from that precedent. Over time, the accumulation of precedent produces a body of legal rules — the common law — that is written, consistent, and authoritative, even though it emerged from individual decisions rather than from legislative acts.

All U.S. states except Louisiana have *reception statutes* that explicitly incorporated the British common law as it stood at the time of independence into the state's legal foundation. Louisiana is the exception because of its French and Spanish colonial heritage; Louisiana law retains features of the civil-law tradition inherited from the Napoleonic Code.

The civil-law tradition, which governs most of continental Europe, Japan, and much of Latin America, differs from the common-law tradition in a structural way: civil-law systems rely primarily on comprehensive codes — codified statutes that cover the field — rather than on accumulated judicial precedent. Judges in civil-law systems apply the code; they do not make law in the same sense that common-law judges do. In practice, the systems converge substantially on most commercial questions; the differences are more pronounced in family law, property law, and constitutional structure.

## The scope of business law

The machinery the chapter has described — four sources of law, three jurisdictional layers, the doctrine of precedent, the preemption framework — is the structural context inside which business operates. Business law is the body of legal doctrine most directly relevant to commercial activity, and it spans a wide range of subjects.

Contract law governs the formation, performance, breach, and remedy of agreements — the legal machinery that makes it possible for parties who do not fully trust each other to make binding commitments. Tort law governs civil liability for harms outside the scope of contract — negligence, product liability, defamation, intentional wrongs. Employment law governs the relationship between employer and employee, from hiring to termination to collective bargaining. Securities regulation governs the public capital markets, including the disclosure obligations of public companies and the prohibition on insider trading. Antitrust law governs market structure and competitive behavior, prohibiting agreements to fix prices or divide markets and acquisitions that substantially lessen competition.

Each of those bodies of law has the same structure: constitutional authority at the top, statutory enactments in the middle, regulatory detail at the granular level, and case law interpreting all of it. The chapters that follow work through each domain in detail. But the structural logic is constant — it is the logic this chapter has laid out.

One observation worth making before moving on: the legal system the chapter has described is not a system optimized for simplicity. It is a system optimized for something different — for the resolution of disputes among parties with conflicting legitimate interests, in a country with fifty sovereign states and a long history of skepticism toward centralized authority. The complexity is not a bug. It is the inevitable result of designing a legal system for a society with those characteristics. Understanding why the system is complex the way it is — rather than just experiencing the complexity as an obstacle — is the starting point for working inside it effectively.
