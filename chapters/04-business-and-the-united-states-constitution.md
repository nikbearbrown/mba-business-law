# Chapter 4 — Business and the United States Constitution
*How eleven words in Article I became the permission slip for the modern regulatory state.*

---

Here is a puzzle worth sitting with: eleven words.

Article I, Section 8, Clause 3 of the United States Constitution gives Congress the power to *regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States, and with the Indian Tribes.* On the domestic side — the side that matters for business regulation — eleven words. That is the entire constitutional text from which the Clean Air Act, the Civil Rights Act, the Americans with Disabilities Act, federal workplace-safety law, and financial regulation all derive their authority. Not analogously. Directly. If you trace any of those statutes back to the constitutional provision that permits Congress to enact them, you land on those eleven words.

How does that work? How do eleven words in a document written in 1787 — when the largest business enterprises in the country were mercantile trading houses — authorize a regulatory apparatus that covers the employment practices of a steel mill in Pennsylvania, the wheat production of a farmer who never sells a bushel, and the admission policies of a motel in Atlanta? That is the question this chapter is about. The answer is not a legal trick. It reflects something real about how economies are structured, and understanding it is one of the most practically useful things a business practitioner can know.

---

Start with the problem the Constitution was designed to solve.

Under the Articles of Confederation — the arrangement that preceded the Constitution — each state was essentially a sovereign trading nation. States taxed goods from other states. States imposed tariffs on out-of-state commerce to protect local producers. New York taxed New Jersey ferries. Connecticut taxed Massachusetts goods. The result was the kind of economic fragmentation that stunted growth and made the union functionally unworkable. The Commerce Clause was written to fix this: to give Congress authority over commerce between states so that the national economy could function as a single market rather than thirteen competing ones.

But fixing that problem created another one. The same clause that authorized Congress to regulate interstate commerce also, implicitly, raised the question of what *wasn't* interstate commerce. The boundary between what Congress could regulate and what was reserved to state authority — the constitutional line between "among the several States" and everything else — had to be drawn somewhere. For most of American history before 1937, courts drew that line at the factory gate. Manufacturing was local. Mining was local. Agriculture was local. These activities happened inside a single state, and the fact that their outputs crossed state lines afterward was treated as irrelevant to their constitutional status. Congress could regulate the shipment. It could not regulate the production.

This was the legal environment in which Franklin Roosevelt launched the New Deal. The federal government was trying to regulate wages, prices, production quotas, and labor relations — and courts were striking these regulations down, one after another, on the grounds that the regulated activities were purely local and therefore outside Congress's authority. A chicken slaughterhouse in Brooklyn, selling only to local buyers, was not interstate commerce. *Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States* (1935). A coal mining company in West Virginia was not interstate commerce, even though it sold coal nationally. *Carter v. Carter Coal* (1936). The theory was clean and it was consistent, and it was producing outcomes that made governing a national industrial economy nearly impossible.

Then came the steel mill.

---

Jones & Laughlin Steel operated a mill in Aliquippa, Pennsylvania. It bought iron ore from Minnesota and coal from West Virginia. It sold finished steel across the country. It employed workers in eighteen facilities spread across four states. When it fired ten workers for union organizing, those workers complained to the National Labor Relations Board, the NLRB ordered reinstatement, and the company refused. The company's argument was not that the workers didn't deserve their jobs back. The argument was constitutional: Congress has no authority to regulate our employment practices, because this mill is in Pennsylvania, these workers are in Pennsylvania, and the employment relationship is a local matter.

The Supreme Court's 1937 answer in *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.* dissolved the fiction. Strip away the arbitrary legal line at the factory gate, the Court said, and look at what this enterprise actually is: a node in a national network. The employment relationship at that node is not local in any meaningful economic sense. Disruptions there — a strike, a labor dispute, an unfair practice that demoralizes the workforce — ripple through an operation whose inputs come from two other states and whose outputs go to buyers across the country. That relationship is, in the Court's word, sufficiently *close* to interstate commerce to fall within Congress's authority.

The principle the Court articulated was broader than the specific case: Congress can regulate activities that, *in the aggregate*, produce *substantial effects* on interstate commerce. Not just activities that *are* interstate commerce, but activities that substantially *affect* it. The difference sounds technical. Its consequences were enormous.

---

The contemporary doctrine organizes Congress's commerce authority into four categories. The first two are uncontroversial: Congress can regulate the *channels* of interstate commerce — the highways, waterways, airways, and rail lines — and the *instrumentalities* that move through those channels — the trucks, ships, planes, and trains, and their operators. Nobody seriously disputes that Congress can set safety standards for interstate freight carriers.

The third category is also largely undisputed: Congress can regulate tangibles and intangibles that actually cross state lines — goods, money, financial instruments, electronic data.

The fourth category is where the constitutional action has always been: the substantial-effects test.

The paradigmatic case is *Wickard v. Filburn* (1942), and it is worth understanding precisely because it seems, at first, impossible. Roscoe Filburn was an Ohio farmer who grew wheat beyond his federal allotment — not to sell, but to feed his own chickens and his own family. He never sold a bushel of the excess wheat. He never shipped any of it across a state line. The federal government fined him under the Agricultural Adjustment Act's production controls. He sued. His argument: my private consumption of homegrown wheat has nothing to do with interstate commerce.

The Supreme Court upheld the fine. Here is the reasoning, and it is worth following carefully: if Filburn grew his own wheat, he would not buy wheat on the market. If thousands of farmers made the same calculation, the aggregate effect on the national wheat market would be substantial. The fact that any individual farmer's excess production is trivially small is constitutionally irrelevant — what matters is not the individual transaction but the *category of activity* considered in aggregate. Under the substantial-effects test, the question shifts: not "does this particular transaction cross a state line?" but "does this category of activity, across all the people who engage in it, produce substantial effects on interstate commerce?"

The logical implication of that shift is large. If the constitutional question is about aggregate category rather than individual transaction, then almost any economic activity of meaningful scale can be reached by federal regulation. The family restaurant sources food from national distributors, uses equipment manufactured elsewhere, employs workers whose wages affect regional consumer spending. The farmer who feeds his own chickens affects the wheat market. And the motel in Atlanta that refuses to serve Black guests? The Court held in *Heart of Atlanta Motel v. United States* (1964) that racial discrimination in lodging, in the aggregate, burdens the interstate travel of Black Americans sufficiently to fall within Congress's commerce authority. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 — one of the most consequential statutes in American history — rests constitutionally on the substantial-effects test.

This is not a legal trick. It reflects something real about the structure of a modern economy, in which activities that appear purely local are embedded in interstate markets in ways that are often invisible at the individual level. The pre-1937 fiction of the purely local enterprise was always somewhat artificial. *Jones & Laughlin* and *Wickard* and *Heart of Atlanta* dissolved the fiction, one case at a time.

---

But notice what the logic implies if you follow it all the way out: if the constitutional question is always about aggregate category, if every economic activity can be traced to some effect on interstate commerce, then the list of things Congress *cannot* regulate becomes empty. That cannot be right. The Tenth Amendment reserves powers not delegated to the federal government to the states, and a constitutional principle with no limiting principle is not really a principle at all.

The Supreme Court re-imposed a limit in *United States v. Lopez* (1995), and it did so by drawing a line that the doctrine has respected since. The case involved the Gun-Free School Zones Act of 1990, which made it a federal crime to possess a firearm near a school. The government's Commerce Clause argument ran like this: guns near schools produce violent crime; violent crime harms educational outcomes; poor education produces less-productive workers; less-productive workers damage the national economy; therefore, interstate commerce.

The Court rejected the argument, 5-4. Chief Justice Rehnquist's opinion identified the problem precisely: the government's causal chain could be constructed for *literally anything*. If "but for this activity, economic outcomes somewhere would be different" is sufficient to trigger Commerce Clause authority, then there is no activity Congress cannot regulate, and the constitutional structure of enumerated powers becomes a formality. The limit, *Lopez* held, is this: the substantial-effects test applies to *economic* activities. Possessing a gun near a school is not an economic activity. Regulating it is not within Congress's commerce authority.

*United States v. Morrison* (2000) applied the same principle to invalidate the civil-remedy provision of the Violence Against Women Act. Gender-motivated violence is not an economic activity. Its downstream economic consequences — lost productivity, medical costs, deterred labor-market participation — are real but insufficient. Connecting non-economic activity to Congress's commerce power through downstream economic effects would swallow the limit entirely.

The most important application of this boundary came in *National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius* (2012), the challenge to the Affordable Care Act's individual mandate. The mandate required most Americans to obtain qualifying health insurance or pay a financial penalty. The government's argument: the health insurance market is unquestionably interstate commerce; virtually everyone participates in the health care system eventually; the decision not to buy insurance is itself a decision with substantial interstate effects, shifting costs to insured parties and to the system as a whole.

Chief Justice Roberts rejected the Commerce Clause argument. The Clause authorizes Congress to *regulate* existing commercial activity. It does not authorize Congress to *compel* individuals into commercial activity in the first place. Regulating inactivity — the failure to buy a product — would eliminate any principled limit on federal power, because every individual decision not to purchase something can be characterized as an economic choice with aggregate effects.

The mandate survived anyway, and this is the practical lesson the case teaches: a regulation that fails one constitutional test can still survive if another constitutional ground supports it. Roberts held that the financial penalty for non-compliance was, in substance, a *tax*, and Congress's taxing authority is broader than its Commerce Clause authority. The individual mandate was unconstitutional under the Commerce Clause and constitutional under the Taxing Clause, and it was the second holding that mattered for the law's survival.

The discipline this requires is: when analyzing a constitutional challenge, do not stop at the first ground. Work through all of them. Commerce Clause, taxing power, spending power. A challenge to one is not a challenge to all.

---

The Commerce Clause does one more thing that is easy to miss: it constrains the states even when Congress has not acted.

Follow the logic. The original purpose of the Commerce Clause was to prevent the states from erecting trade barriers against each other — the fragmentation problem under the Articles of Confederation. If that is the purpose, then the Clause must do some work even when Congress is silent. Otherwise, Congress's failure to regulate an area would simply restore the pre-constitutional condition: states free to discriminate against each other's commerce at will. That cannot be what the framers intended.

The *dormant* Commerce Clause doctrine — the term is judicial shorthand for this principle — holds that states cannot enact laws that discriminate against interstate commerce in favor of in-state economic interests, regardless of whether Congress has acted. The analysis runs in two phases.

Phase one: does the law discriminate against out-of-state interests, on its face or in its practical effect? Michigan and New York had laws allowing in-state wineries to sell directly to consumers while prohibiting out-of-state wineries from doing the same. *Granholm v. Heald* (2005). That is facial discrimination — the law's text draws the line at state borders. Laws that discriminate facially are presumptively unconstitutional; the state must show there is no non-discriminatory way to achieve any legitimate state interest. That showing is extremely difficult to make, and the wine-shipping laws did not survive it.

Phase two: even if the law does not discriminate, does the burden it imposes on interstate commerce outweigh the legitimate local benefit? The standard from *Pike v. Bruce Church* (1970) is that non-discriminatory state regulations are valid unless the burden on interstate commerce is *clearly excessive* in relation to the local benefit. This is a balancing test. It is fact-specific. It generates a lot of litigation, and it tends to produce narrow, case-by-case outcomes rather than bright-line rules.

The dormant Commerce Clause is the constitutional doctrine business lawyers encounter most often in multi-state practice. State environmental regulations, data-privacy laws, consumer-protection rules, product standards — all of these can be challenged under it when they treat out-of-state actors differently from in-state ones, in purpose or effect. The question is always the same: legitimate exercise of state authority over local affairs, or a way of favoring in-state producers at the expense of out-of-state competition?

---

When Congress does act, a different question arises: does federal law displace state authority in the same area? This is preemption, and it comes in three forms worth distinguishing.

*Express preemption* is the simplest. The federal statute explicitly says it displaces state law on the specified topic. The Employee Retirement Income Security Act expressly preempts state regulation of employee benefit plans. That is why a self-insured employer's health plan is governed by federal law even when the employer operates entirely within a single state.

*Field preemption* occurs when the federal regulatory scheme is so comprehensive that Congress is judged to have occupied the entire field, leaving no room for state regulation alongside it. Immigration law is partially governed by field preemption. Nuclear safety regulation is another example. The judgment requires an inference about congressional intent, which is contested and often litigated.

*Conflict preemption* is the residual form, and the most fact-specific: state law is preempted when it is impossible to comply with both federal and state requirements simultaneously, or when state law stands as an obstacle to the federal statute's purposes. "Stands as an obstacle" is a standard that requires courts to decide what the federal statute was trying to achieve — which is rarely free from argument.

The practical order of analysis for a business operating across multiple states is this: for any state regulation you encounter, ask three questions in sequence. Is it preempted by a federal statute? If not, does it discriminate against interstate commerce under the dormant Commerce Clause? If not, does the burden it imposes clearly outweigh the legitimate local benefit under *Pike* balancing? Work through all three before concluding the regulation is either valid or vulnerable.

---

Step back and look at the whole architecture.

Before 1937, the answer to "who decides?" was: states decide almost everything involving production and employment, and Congress decides a narrow slice involving literal movement of goods across state lines. The manufacturing worker, the miner, the farmer — they were governed by state law. Federal regulation reached them only at the moment of shipment.

After *Jones & Laughlin* and *Wickard*, the answer became: Congress can decide anything involving economic activity with substantial effects on interstate commerce, and that category is very large. The consequence — not immediately, but over the following decades — was the administrative state as it now exists. Workplace safety, environmental protection, civil rights in employment, financial regulation, food and drug safety: all of it rests constitutionally on the foundation those two cases built.

*Lopez*, *Morrison*, and *Sebelius* have re-imposed limits at the edges — non-economic activities, compelled commerce. But the center of the doctrine, the substantial-effects test applied to economic activities, has not moved since 1942. *Wickard* is still good law. A farmer growing wheat for his own chickens is still within Congress's authority to regulate.

The states retained their police power — the residual authority to regulate for the health, safety, welfare, and morals of their inhabitants — and they exercise it substantially. California's environmental standards, New York's financial-industry rules, Texas's business-formation framework: states are not passive actors. The dormant Commerce Clause and preemption doctrine constrain them, but those constraints leave large territory available, and states use it.

The result is regulatory complexity that is, by design, irreducible. Every significant federal regulation rests on a contestable constitutional authority. Every state regulation sits inside a framework that includes both the possibility of federal preemption and the possibility of dormant Commerce Clause challenge. This is not a bug in the system. It is the constitutional structure working as intended — distributing authority among multiple levels of government, preventing any single level from accumulating unchecked power, and forcing the ongoing renegotiation of regulatory boundaries through courts and legislatures rather than settling them once and for all.

Eleven words. They were never going to be enough to settle everything. They were written to start a conversation that would have to be conducted, case by case, for as long as the Constitution endures.
