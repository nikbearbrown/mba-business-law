## A Note about AI

Business law is a field where AI fluency is most dangerous in the way unauthorized practice of law is dangerous. The model knows enough doctrine to sound like a lawyer and not enough about any specific situation to be one. The students who use this textbook will not always be the lawyers in the room; some will be the entrepreneurs, accountants, and managers who need legal vocabulary to know when to call counsel and what to ask. The note's job is to mark the boundary clearly.

The model has read major US statutes, the canonical case law, the standard treatises, and the textbook explanations of contract, tort, agency, securities, and employment law. It will produce a competent paragraph on the elements of negligence, the categories of consideration in contract formation, or the requirements for a securities registration exemption. The fluency is real and useful. It becomes a problem when the question moves from doctrine to application — because application depends on jurisdiction, on facts the model cannot have, and on procedural posture the model cannot know.

Where the model genuinely helps: explaining the doctrine (what the elements of a tort actually are, what consideration means in contract law, what fiduciary duty requires), walking through the standard case in any major doctrine, comparing how a doctrine differs across jurisdictions at the level of generality the textbook treats, and producing the vocabulary you need to read a contract, a brief, or a regulatory filing intelligently. The model is also useful as a structural critic of a non-lawyer's draft — paste your contract clause and ask what a lawyer would say is missing.

Where the model does damage: producing legal advice. The model has no view of your jurisdiction, your specific facts, the procedural history of your matter, the current state of the law (training cutoff), or the strategic considerations a working lawyer brings to representation. A model-generated answer to "is this legal?" or "should I sign this?" has the texture of legal advice without the substance, and acting on it can produce consequences the model bears none of.

A specific failure mode worth naming: the model treats statute citations and case citations as authoritative when the underlying authority may have been amended, overturned, or superseded since training. The citation will look correct (a real case, a real statute number) and may no longer be good law. A non-lawyer reading model output cannot tell the difference between good law and superseded law from the citation alone.

The rule that covers all three: doctrine from the model, advice from a licensed lawyer with current research access. Use the model to learn the vocabulary and the structure of the law so you can understand what your lawyer is doing and what questions to ask. Do not let the model's fluency convince you that you do not need the lawyer — that conviction is the failure mode this note exists to prevent.
