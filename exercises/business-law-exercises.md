## Chapter 08: Chapter 8 — Warranties and Sales Contracts
*Source: `chapters/08-sales-contracts.md`*

## Exercises

### Warm-Up

**1.** A hardware store sells a claw hammer. No statements are made about it during the sale. The head separates from the handle on first use, injuring the buyer's hand. The store regularly sells tools of this kind. Which warranty, if any, has been breached? What must the buyer demonstrate to prevail?

**2.** A seller's product page states: "Our industrial pump delivers 200 GPM at 40 PSI — certified for continuous duty cycle." The buyer purchases the pump, which delivers only 140 GPM under identical conditions. Identify the type of warranty created and explain why it applies regardless of whether a separate warranty document exists.

**3.** A used-equipment dealer sells a commercial espresso machine described as "operational." It breaks down within a week. Does the implied warranty of merchantability apply? How, if at all, does the used-goods context change the standard?

---

### Application

**4.** A restaurant equipment supplier tells a buyer over the phone: "This walk-in cooler is perfect for storing dairy — it'll hold 34–38°F without issue." The written invoice contains a clause stating: "Seller makes no warranties, express or implied." The cooler consistently runs at 45°F, spoiling the buyer's dairy inventory. Analyze whether the phone statement created an express warranty and whether the invoice disclaimer can negate it under UCC 2-316.

**5.** A building contractor tells a plumbing supply house that she needs pipe fittings rated for high-pressure steam lines in an industrial retrofit. The supply house selects and recommends a specific product. The fittings fail under pressure, causing damage. Identify which warranty or warranties are potentially at issue. What must the contractor prove to establish the implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose?

**6.** A company purchases 200 branded promotional items from a vendor. The items turn out to be counterfeit — the brand's trademark is reproduced without authorization, and the brand owner sends a cease-and-desist. What UCC warranty is implicated? Who bears the risk, and does it matter whether the buyer supplied the design?

---

### Synthesis

**7.** A small manufacturer orders custom-machined parts to specifications it provided. The parts are delivered but fail to meet dimensional tolerances. The seller argues: (a) the buyer designed the parts, so there is no warranty of fitness for a particular purpose; (b) the invoice disclaimed all implied warranties. Evaluate both defenses. Does either succeed? Are there remaining warranty theories the buyer can still pursue?

**8.** Return to the MBA classroom scenario from the chapter: a restaurant buys a used commercial freezer from a restaurant-equipment dealer. The salesperson says, "It has been tested and is ready for commercial kitchen use." The invoice includes a small-print disclaimer of all warranties. The freezer fails after two days, ruining $8,000 of inventory. Apply the full warranty analysis: express warranty, implied warranty of merchantability (including the used-goods calibration), disclaimer validity under UCC 2-316, and available remedies including whether the spoiled inventory qualifies as consequential damages.

---

### Challenge

**9.** A software company licenses a machine-vision system to a manufacturer for inspecting circuit boards. The system is delivered as firmware embedded in a physical device. The manufacturer claims the system fails to detect a class of defects it was marketed as able to catch, causing $600,000 in downstream product recalls. The seller argues Article 2 does not apply because software is a service, not a good. Construct the strongest argument on each side. What factors would a court weigh under a predominant-purpose analysis? If Article 2 does apply, identify the relevant warranties and remedies.

**10.** A buyer and seller negotiate a purchase order for 1,000 industrial valves. The purchase order is silent on warranties. After the order is signed, the seller emails its standard terms, which include: (a) a disclaimer of all implied warranties, (b) a limitation of remedies to repair or replacement, and (c) an exclusion of consequential damages. Sixty valves fail, causing a production shutdown costing the buyer $250,000 in lost output. Analyze whether the post-order standard terms are part of the contract under UCC 2-207, whether the disclaimer and remedy limitations are effective, and whether the consequential-damage exclusion can survive if the limited remedy fails of its essential purpose.
